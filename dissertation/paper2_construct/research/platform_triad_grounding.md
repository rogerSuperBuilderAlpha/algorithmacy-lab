# Grounding the worked examples: platform work as a triad, not a worker–app dyad

Empirical/theoretical anchors that ground Paper 2's worked applications — especially the **false dyad**
(rideshare driver → app → rider, computed Φ = 2.0 when the rider is constitutive, Φ = 0 when dropped;
see `results.md` §2.C). The claim these sources support: platform-mediated work *presents* as a
two-party relationship with an app but is structurally a three-party system, because a third party
(customer/rider/requester/employer) is constitutive of what the platform commits while never directly
interacting with the worker. Two passes: academic connectors (Consensus, 3 searches → clusters 1, 2, 4,
5) + a deep-research harness (`wuh56bpgs`, 104 agents). The harness's search/fetch/3-vote-verify stage
completed and supplied cluster 3 (two-sided markets) plus the open-gap correction; its final synthesis
agent looped on a schema error and was killed, so those anchors were salvaged directly from the run's
verified journal rather than its (never-emitted) summary. Connectors lead for anything cited.

> Scope note: this grounds the *referent* of the worked examples so they are not toy systems; it is not
> the citation backbone of the formal argument (that is `technical_background.md`). The argument is the
> user's IP — these sources illustrate the phenomenon the instrument classifies.

## Cluster 1 — algorithmic management as mediation of the worker–third-party relation

- **[anchor — the explicit triad] Chan, N. K. (2022).** Algorithmic precarity and metric power:
  Managing the affective measures and customers in the gig economy. *Big Data & Society*, 9(1). The
  cleanest statement of the third party as constitutive: platform metrics "mediate the **trilateral
  relationship between platforms, workers, and customers**," and customer-sourced ratings are a primary
  axis of control. *Directly grounds the false-dyad referent — the customer/rider is a third pole, not
  context.*
- **[third party as controlling actor] Duggan, J., Sherman, U., Carbery, R., & McDonnell, A. (2023).**
  Algorithmic HRM control in the gig economy: The app-worker perspective. *Human Resource Management*,
  62(6). Names the "substantial direct and indirect influence of **non-organizational parties**"
  (customers) in controlling workers — the third party exerts real causal force on the worker *through*
  the platform's mechanisms, exactly the constitutive-but-mediated coupling the false dyad encodes.
- **[mediation, no direct contact] Benlian, A., Wiener, M., & Cram, W. A. (2022).** Algorithmic
  Management. *Business & Information Systems Engineering*, 64. Opens on the defining fact: "most Uber
  drivers never personally interact with an Uber manager" — coordination runs entirely through the
  algorithmic intermediary. Grounds the *strict mediator topology* (no direct W–C edge) of the worked
  examples. (Cram is also a Paper 1 author — consistent canon.)
- **[the mediation tool + taxonomy] Duggan, J., Sherman, U., Carbery, R., & McDonnell, A. (2020).**
  Algorithmic management and app-work in the gig economy: A research agenda for ERM and HRM. *Human
  Resource Management Journal*, 30(1), 114–132. Classifies gig work (app-work / crowdwork / capital-
  platform) and frames algorithmic management as the "novel **mediation tool**" between worker and
  customer — the genus of which rideshare, delivery, and crowdwork are species (the worked-example
  referents).
- **[review backbone] Kadolkar, I., et al. (2024).** Algorithmic management in the gig economy: A
  systematic review and research integration. *Journal of Organizational Behavior*, 45(?). NLP topic
  model over the cross-disciplinary AM literature; the comprehensive definition + dimensions of AM.
  (Kadolkar also appears in Paper 1 — cite once, consistently.)
- **[org-science legitimation] Keegan, A., & Meijerink, J. (2025).** Algorithmic Management in
  Organizations? From Edge Case to Center Stage. *Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and
  Organizational Behavior*, 12. AM as a "holistic system" with **multi-actor signaling** and a "gray
  zone" — supports treating the coordination as a multi-party structure rather than a worker–system
  dyad, in a top org-science venue.

## Cluster 2 — platform labor as a triangular / triadic structure

The literature explicitly theorizes platform work as triangular — and one top-tier IS result states the
triad does *not* reduce to the two-sided model, the empirical analogue of the false-dyad computation.

- **[anchor — centers the third party] Healy, J., Pekarek, A., & Vromen, A. (2024).** The triangular
  relationship in platform gig work: Consumers, platform beneficence and worker vulnerability. *New
  Technology, Work and Employment.* States it directly: platform gig work is "created and contested in
  dynamic, **triangular relationships between platforms, workers, and consumers**," and consumers
  *mediate* the platform–worker interests. The cleanest centering of the constitutive third party.
- **[the triad ≠ the dyad — formal] Sun, G., et al. (2023).** Dinner at Your Doorstep: Service
  Innovation via the Gig Economy on Food Delivery Platforms. *Information Systems Research.* The
  food-delivery "**three-sided nature**—encompassing consumers, restaurants, and gig drivers"—and the
  key result: "**insights from two-sided platforms don't seamlessly translate**" to the triad. This is
  the economics analogue of the paper's claim: the three-party structure is irreducible to the
  two-party one — a top-IS-venue precedent that the third side is structural, not incidental.
- **[the explicit triangle] Dieuaide, P., & Azaïs, C. (2020).** Platforms of Work, Labour, and
  Employment Relationship: The Grey Zones of a Digital Governance. *Frontiers in Sociology.* Digitisation
  "replaces the employment contract... with a **triangular 'worker-platform-customer' relationship**" —
  the exact W–S–C triple of the worked examples, stated as the defining structural shift.
- **[cornerstone] Vallas, S., & Schor, J. B. (2020).** What Do Platforms Do? Understanding the Gig
  Economy. *Annual Review of Sociology*, 46, 273–294. (914 cites.) Platforms as a **distinct governance
  mechanism** — "different from markets, hierarchies, or networks" — i.e., not reducible to the dyadic
  forms org theory already has. The high-citation anchor for "this is a genuinely new structure."
- **[harness anchor — triangular geometry] Stark, D., & Pais, I. (2020).** Algorithmic Management in the
  Platform Economy. *Sociologica*, 14(3), 47–72. DOI 10.2383/98583 (open access). The harness's
  single most on-point source for the false dyad: explicitly theorizes platform work as a **triangular
  geometry** — platform operator at the apex, producers and users at the other two corners — bound by
  "twisted feedback loops" of cybernetic control where the platform enrolls providers and users without
  delegating managerial authority. Supplies the irreducible three-party structure directly.
- *(corroborating triad framings: Schor et al. 2020 "Dependence and precarity," Theory & Society, 400
  cites; Cruz et al. 2023 platforms–workers–clients "service relation"; Roy-Mukherjee & Bose 2020
  "triangular business model"; Schmidt 2021 "three-sided digital labour markets.")*

## Cluster 3 — two-sided / multi-sided markets and platform intermediation  *(harness-salvaged)*

The formal economics of an intermediary binding two sides that do not transact directly — the economic
analogue of `S' = f(W, C)` and the strongest formal precedent for *non-decomposability into a
worker–platform pair*. (Salvaged from harness `wuh56bpgs`'s verified fetch/verify stage; the run's final
synthesis agent looped on a schema error and was killed, but the per-source findings were complete.)

- **[anchor — the sides are one interdependent market] Rochet, J.-C., & Tirole, J. (2003).** Platform
  Competition in Two-Sided Markets. *Journal of the European Economic Association*, 1(4), 990–1029.
  (MIT-hosted author PDF open.) The foundational result: because prices/usage on one side affect the
  other, the two sides **must be analyzed as a single interdependent market, not two independent ones**
  — the analytic core for grounding the triad's non-decomposability into a worker–platform pair.
- **[the crux distinction — why the gig triad is irreducible, not a marketplace] Hagiu, A., & Wright, J.
  (2015/2019).** Multi-Sided Platforms. *International Journal of Industrial Organization* (HBS WP 15-037
  + NBER WP 18783; open). An MSP enables **direct** interactions between sides; a gig platform where the
  worker and third party **cannot** directly interact and the platform controls the key terms falls on
  the *managed-intermediary/reseller* side of the Hagiu–Wright boundary, **not** the pure-MSP side.
  *This distinction is the crux*: it is exactly why the gig triad is irreducible (mediator-bound) rather
  than a decomposable marketplace — the formal-economics statement of the strict mediator topology.
- **[cross-side effects: the third party is causally present though invisible] Parker, G., & Van Alstyne,
  M. (2005).** Two-Sided Network Effects: A Theory of Information Product Design. *Management Science*,
  51(10), 1494–1504. DOI 10.1287/mnsc.1050.0400. (+ Parker, Van Alstyne & Choudary, *Platform
  Revolution*, 2016.) Cross-side network effects: each side's value depends on the other's
  participation, **mediated entirely by the platform** — formalizing why the third party is causally
  present even when invisible to the worker (the false dyad's mechanism in economic vocabulary).
- **[determinations, not bargaining, govern the coupling] Rochet, J.-C., & Tirole, J. (2006).** Two-Sided
  Markets: A Progress Report. *RAND Journal of Economics*, 37(3), 645–667. Two-sidedness turns on the
  platform's **price structure** (how it allocates terms across sides), not the sides' own bargaining —
  the intermediary's *determinations* bind two parties who never negotiate directly. (Cf. `S' = f(W,C)`.)
- **[intermediary internalizes what the sides cannot] Evans, D. S., & Schmalensee, R. (2007/2016).**
  The Antitrust Analysis of Multi-Sided Platform Businesses (NBER WP; *Matchmakers*, 2016). Platforms
  internalize cross-side externalities the sides cannot internalize themselves — "a single integrated
  market with interdependent sides," reinforcing non-decomposability. (Open NBER/U. Chicago PDFs.)

Sun et al. 2023 [ISR, Cluster 2] is the bridge from this economics to the computation: the three-sided
case does not reduce to the two-sided one.

## Cluster 4 — the dyadic framing that omits the third party

The constructs Paper 1 forecloses model a single user facing a system — exactly the two-party frame the
false dyad shows scores Φ = 0 because it omits the constitutive third party.

- **[the dyadic frame, named] Tarafdar, M., Page, X., & Marabelli, M. (2022).** Algorithms as
  co-workers: Human–algorithm role interactions in algorithmic work. *Information Systems Journal.*
  Theorizes the **human–algorithm** pair (algorithm as role sender, human as role taker) — the dyadic
  construct in its clearest form. The honest foil: a faithful two-party model of platform work that, by
  construction, cannot see the rider/customer that makes the structure a triad.
- **[harness — the unit-of-analysis critique] The CSCW "at least two people" problem.** The harness
  surfaced work problematizing the disciplinary move to treat *a single person + an AI model/agent* as
  the unit of analysis — noting that CSCW historically required "at least two people." This frames the
  conceptual stakes precisely: the human-AI dyadic framing **brackets out the human third party**
  (customer/requester/employer) that the platform actually couples the worker to. The clean statement of
  *what the two-party model omits*. (Specific cite to be pinned from the harness source list when drafting.)
- *(Paper 1's foreclosure section already canons the "algorithmic competence/literacy" constructs that
  this example targets — cite from there, not re-derived here.)*

## Cluster 5 — worked-example referents (the empirical "no W–C edge")

The strict mediator topology of the worked examples — worker and third party coordinate only through
the platform — is not a modeling convenience; platforms *engineer* it.

- **[anchor — disintermediation] Rosenblat, A., & Stark, L. (2016).** Algorithmic Labor and Information
  Asymmetries: A Case Study of Uber's Drivers. *International Journal of Communication*, 10, 3758–3784.
  (1209 cites.) The "information and power asymmetries produced by the Uber application are fundamental
  to its ability to structure control," and names **platform disintermediation** as the mechanism
  shaping communications between the parties. The empirical grounding for `cm[W][C] = cm[C][W] = 0`: the
  platform actively prevents the direct channel.
- **[the false-dyad phenomenology, verbatim] Stein, J. M. L., et al. (2023).** "You are you and the app.
  There's nobody else.": Building Worker-Designed Data Institutions within Platform Hegemony. *CHI '23.*
  The title *is* the false dyad: the worker experiences only the app, the third party invisible. The
  computation answers it — "nobody else" is causally a constitutive third pole (Φ = 2.0 with the rider
  in, Φ = 0 with the rider dropped).
- **[no direct contact] Walker, M., Fleming, P., & Berti, M. (2021).** 'You can't pick up a phone and
  talk to someone': How algorithms function as biopower in the gig economy. *Organization*, 28(1). The
  fragmentation/isolation that severs the direct worker–party channel — the lived form of the missing
  W–C edge.
- **[platform as necessary connector] Muralidhar, S. H., Rintel, S., & Sellen, A. (2022).** Between a
  Rock and a Hard Place. *CSCW.* Workers become "increasingly dependent on the technology-provider **in
  order to connect with the customers**" — the platform is the constitutive coupling (only through S do
  W and C meet), which is precisely what makes the structure irreducible.
- *(referent spread for the worked examples: rideshare driver–rider–platform [Rosenblat & Stark 2016;
  Stein 2023]; food delivery courier–customer–platform [Sun et al. 2023, ISR]; algorithmic hiring
  applicant–ATS–employer, applicant and manager never directly interact — the §2.B ATS case.)*

## The open gap (what positions the instrument) — corrected by the harness

The harness's dedicated gap-hunt **corrected an overclaim**: the irreducibility toolkit has *already*
been pointed at social/organizational coordination, so the honest gap is narrower and sharper. State it
carefully — do **not** claim "no one has applied Φ to groups."

- **[the closest prior — must cite] Engel, D., & Malone, T. W. (2018).** Integrated information as a
  metric for group interaction. *PLOS ONE* (and related). Operationalize integrated information (Φ),
  originally a consciousness measure, as a quantitative metric of **how much a human group's behavior is
  irreducible to its individual members**, computed from communication/activity patterns (whole-system
  vs subsystem entropy). *This is the nearest precedent and the one the paper must acknowledge* — Φ has
  already crossed into human collective behavior.
- **[the formal toolkit is mature] Mediano et al. ΦID** (eLife, DOI 10.7554/eLife.88173; already in
  `technical_background.md`) fuses IIT irreducibility with PID to separate synergistic (irreducible) from
  redundant information — the exact measure one would adapt to test whether a triad's synergy vanishes
  when the worker–platform pair is isolated.
- **[applied to multi-agent collectives, not platforms] PID-synergy / causal-emergence work** (e.g.
  arXiv 2510.05174, building on Rosas et al. 2020) measures whether a multi-agent system is an
  irreducible higher-order collective vs a mere aggregate — but targets **LLM agent collectives**, not
  platform labor, and **does not model an intermediary binding two non-interacting sides**.

**The residual gap Paper 2 fills (stated precisely).** The irreducibility toolkit (Φ; ΦID; PID synergy)
exists and has reached *group* and *multi-agent* coordination — but no work has pointed it at the
**platform-labor triad**: the structure in which a mediator's *committed determinations* bind a worker
and a third party who never directly interact (the false dyad). Paper 2's contribution is (a) to apply
an irreducibility measure to exactly that mediator-bound, non-dyadic coordination form, and (b) the
computed demonstration — Φ = 2.0 with the third party constitutive, Φ = 0 without — that the hidden
third party is not incidental context but load-bearing structure. The gap is *application + the
constitutive-mediator framing*, not the existence of the measure.
