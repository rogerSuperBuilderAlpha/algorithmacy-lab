## 1. Introduction: the form question

A driver in Chicago turns on an app and waits. A few miles away a rider opens the same company's app and asks for a car. Neither chooses the other; neither learns the other's name until the trip begins, if then. Between them sits a single determination the platform commits — a dispatch that pairs them, prices the ride, and routes the car — and that determination is the only thing that passes between the two people the platform exists to connect. Ask the driver what she is doing and she will describe a relationship with an app. Ask what the coordination *is*, in its structure, and the answer is far less obvious. There are two people and a system, but two people and a system can be arranged into either of two very different shapes, and the difference is not written on the screen.

Paper 1 drew a line through the constructs that describe how people work with algorithms. On one side sit the constructs we already have — algorithmic competence, platform literacy, the technology-acceptance family — and every one of them measures a *dyad*: a worker and the system in front of her, a person and the model she queries, a user and the interface she has learned to read. On the other side sits the form Paper 1 named algorithmacy, which measures a *triad*: a worker coordinating with a counterpart she may never meet, *through* a determination the system commits between them. The argument of Paper 1 was that the second form is real, that the dyadic constructs cannot see it, and that it asks something of a worker the dyadic constructs never name.

That argument carries a debt. It only does work if an analyst, handed a particular case, can say which form she is looking at. And here the argument runs out of road, because the dyad and the triad do not announce themselves. Counting the parties will not settle it: a setting with three people in it is still a dyad if the third is a passive conduit, or if the system between them ignores one side, because then the structure collapses back onto the pair and the third party was never load-bearing. Reading the interface will not settle it either: a worker who experiences nothing but an app — who taps a screen and is dispatched, and never sees the person on the other end — may sit inside an irreducible triad, the dyadic picture an artifact of what she happens to see. The distinction between the two forms is not given by the surface of the interaction. It has to be *classified*, and the classification can run against appearances in both directions.

So Paper 1's contribution generates a question it cannot answer from inside its own argument: given an object of study, is the coordination dyadic or triadic? This paper answers it. We map the object and its relationships into a transition matrix and run integrated information theory over it. The measure returns a number, and the number decides: **Φ = 0 means the structure factors along the lines between the parties (a dyad, whatever it looked like); Φ > 0 means it does not (a triad, whatever it looked like).** What looked like three parties and scores zero was a dyad with an extra body in the room. What looked like a worker alone with an app and scores positive was a triad the interface hid.

We want to be exact about what kind of contribution this is. It is not a new measure of "triadicity," invented for the occasion and asking to be trusted. It is the import of a measure built and tested in another field, adopted as a formal model of a question organizational research has not had an apparatus for — what methodologists call a method-introduction contribution, the kind whose value is the method itself rather than any single finding it produces (Bergh et al., 2022). The formal model is the deliverable. Without it, "this case is triadic" is an assertion an analyst makes and a reviewer takes on faith; with it, the classification is computed from a stated model, reproducible, and wrong in ways that can be caught.

There is a reason to want this that goes past tidiness. A construct earns its place in a research program only when it is drawn sharply enough to be measured and told apart from its neighbors; Suddaby (2010) argued that construct clarity — a clean definition, explicit scope conditions, and separation from adjacent constructs — is the precondition for any cumulative theory. Algorithmacy's scope condition just *is* the triadic form: the construct claims to bite on coordination that does not reduce to its pairs, and on no other. A scope condition with no test attached is a boundary an author asserts and readers cannot check. The instrument is what gives that boundary an edge, and in doing so it does the one thing a measurement paper exists to do — it lets a construct discriminate, in advance and without appeal to the author's judgment, the cases it governs from the cases it does not.

We build the model and check it on controls, and we run it on cases whose modeled answers the construct already fixes, to show it behaves as the construct predicts. We do not claim it is validated against the world — a Φ value is a property of the model, not yet shown to track any observed coordination outcome, and that empirical program is later work. The claim of this paper is narrower and, we think, prior: that whether we model a coordination form as triadic is not left to interpretation but follows by computation from a stated model, and that the apparatus that computes it can be built from a tool already sitting in a neighboring field.

The argument runs in six steps. We first defend the borrowing — that importing a formal tool is a disciplined organizational-science move, and that this particular tool is not an arbitrary analogy for non-dyadic coordination but a motivated formalization once three modeling choices are fixed (§2). We then fix where the model runs, on the observable application layer rather than the opaque mechanism (§3), and pin the choices the whole exercise rests on, above all the rule that carves the application layer into states (§4). We build the model and make it pass two controls before it is allowed to classify anything in dispute (§5), then run it on five coordination forms whose modeled classifications the construct fixes in advance, including the two where the modeled answer runs against what the situation looks like (§6). We close by stating exactly what the model classifies and what it deliberately leaves for the empirical work to come (§7).

## 2. The borrowing

The instrument is integrated information theory, and importing it asks for a defense on two fronts. The first is that reaching into another field for a formal tool is a disciplined move with standing in organizational research, not an ornament. The second is that *this* tool, in particular, is not a metaphor stretched over the problem but a motivated formalization of it, once three modeling choices are fixed. We take them in turn.

### 2.1 Borrowing is what the field does

Organizational and strategy research has imported formal apparatus from the natural sciences before, and the most heavily traveled case is the one closest to ours. Kauffman built the NK fitness landscape to model rugged adaptation in evolutionary biology; Levinthal (1997) carried it into strategy, Rivkin (2000) used it to explain why a complex strategy resists imitation even when rivals can see it whole, and a generation of simulation studies followed, enough that a review could take stock of seventy of them as a single research program (Baumann et al., 2019). The import was not a one-time raid. The NK formalism was extended inside management to carry things its biological original never modeled — Frenken (2006), for instance, generalized it so that shared standards could mediate the interdependencies between components, which is to say the borrowed mathematics was made to hold a *mediator*, a part that couples other parts. That a transplanted formalism can be stretched to model the very feature we care about — a system that binds parties who do not otherwise touch — is not a coincidence we lean on, but it is a precedent we note.

What made the NK transfer disciplined rather than decorative was a rule the methodologists who studied it stated plainly: in moving a formal model from one domain to another, the syntax can travel unchanged while the semantics must be reworked for the new domain (Marks et al., 2019; Robertson & Caldart, 2008). The equations are kept; what their symbols refer to is rebuilt for the destination. A fitness landscape's "peak" stops meaning reproductive fitness and starts meaning firm performance, and the transfer is honest exactly insofar as that re-reference is done carefully and the parts that do not transfer are left behind.

That is precisely our move. We take integrated information theory's mathematics of irreducibility, re-read what its terms denote in the setting of coordination — a "system" becomes the parties and the mediator, a "mechanism" becomes a committed determination, "irreducibility over a partition" becomes coordination that does not factor into pairwise dealings — and leave the rest of the theory at the door. Stated in the methodologists' own vocabulary, this is a method at the early phase of its life cycle in our field: mature where it came from, new here (Bergh et al., 2022). The contribution is to bring it across and show it earns its place.

### 2.2 The tool is a motivated formalization, not an arbitrary analogy

The fit is not rhetorical. It holds because, under the modeling choices we fix below, the tool and the phenomenon are built around the same notion: irreducibility to parts.

Consider what integrated information theory measures. Its quantity, Φ, is the degree to which a system's cause-effect structure is irreducible to its parts. The theory's integration postulate puts it in terms that read, for our purposes, like a definition handed over from the other side: a system's cause-effect power "must specify its cause-effect state as a whole set of units, irreducible to separate subsets of units" (Albantakis et al., 2023). The mechanics behind the slogan are simple to state. The theory considers every way of partitioning the system into parts, asks how much of the system's cause-effect power survives each cut, and reports the system's irreducibility over the partition that does it the *least* damage — the minimum-information partition. Zaeemzadeh and Tononi (2024) put the resulting quantity as plainly as it can be put: integrated information is how much of the causal power of a set of units "cannot be accounted for by its parts," and if the set is fully explained by its parts, it is reducible and its integrated information is zero. When the structure can be split along the lines between its parts with nothing lost, Φ is zero; a strictly modular system carries no integrated information at all (Balduzzi & Tononi, 2008; Oizumi et al., 2014). Φ is exactly zero when the whole is its parts, and positive when it is not.

Now consider what makes a coordination form non-dyadic. A form is triadic, in Paper 1's sense, precisely when it does not reduce to the pairwise dealings of the parties — when they must coordinate *through* a determination that binds them rather than around it. The triad is the case that does not factor into its pairs. Set the two definitions beside each other and they line up — but the alignment is something we *construct*, not something the world hands us. "Does not reduce to the parts" and "irreducible over the partition between the parties" coincide once we make a modeling choice: that the partition we read is the one running along the lines between the parties. That choice is ours, not forced; with it, the construct's distinction and Φ track the same structural property.

So, under that choice, the two descriptions track one structural property. The property that makes the *modeled* coordination triadic is the property Φ quantifies, and the value Φ returns is the value the construct's distinction needs — not because Φ and triadicity are the same object independent of the model, but because we have chosen to model triadicity as Φ over the party partition. We do not have to invent a measure of triadicity and then argue for its validity from scratch — the standard, and difficult, labor of construct measurement; the imported tool delivers a quantity directly, and our work is to defend the modeling choices that make it the right quantity to read. And the irreducibility it delivers is not an artifact of one chosen formula. Hoel and colleagues (2013) put the same whole-beats-parts structure to work as a general measure of causal emergence, in which a coarse description of a system can carry more causal power than its microscopic details; and Comolatti and Hoel (2022) found the same irreducibility recurring across more than a dozen independently developed measures of causation, because they share a few underlying causal primitives. We are not betting the construct on a quirk of one equation. We are using a member of a consilient family, chosen because it is the one with a mature, exact, open-source implementation for discrete systems.

### 2.3 What we do not import, and one objection answered

Integrated information theory was built as a theory of consciousness, and as a theory of consciousness it is sharply contested — in 2023 a large group of researchers argued in an open letter that IIT, taken as a theory of consciousness, is untestable and merits the label of pseudoscience (IIT-Concerned, 2023), a charge its proponents dispute and that remains live. We import none of that, only the irreducibility mathematics, which stands on its own as a formal account of when a system fails to factor into its parts (Negro, 2024; the consciousness identity we set aside is Tononi & Koch, 2015). The boundary is clean, and worth drawing concretely. We use the computation that takes a transition matrix and returns the irreducibility of its cause-effect structure over the minimum-information partition. We do not use, need, or assert the theory's central identity claim — that this quantity, maximized over a physical substrate, *is* the quantity of consciousness present. A coordination form scoring Φ > 0 is, for us, irreducible; it is not, for us, conscious, and nothing in the paper turns on whether the consciousness reading is right or wrong. The mathematics is indifferent to that dispute, and so are we. The formal-model framing is exactly what lets the borrowing survive the dispute: a critique aimed at IIT *as a theory of consciousness* — its testability, its identity claim — does not reach a use of Φ as a structural irreducibility measure over a stated model, because no consciousness claim is made.

Two further things must be said plainly, because a reader who knows the literature will raise them. First, borrowing an integrated-information measure outside consciousness is established, if niche, and the precedents adopt exactly the framing we do — Φ as a measure of structural integration, not of mind. Engel and Malone (2018) computed a measure of this family on work groups, Wikipedia article histories, and network traffic, explicitly decoupling its usefulness from the consciousness reading; Niizato and colleagues (2020) computed IIT Φ on coordinating fish schools; and the integrated-information-decomposition family has been turned on flocking, cellular automata, and financial markets (Rosas et al., 2020). Second, and sharper: Φ can be large for trivially regular structures. Cerullo (2015) showed that a grid of XOR gates can carry a Φ exceeding the human brain's, so a high Φ does not certify sophisticated or meaningful information processing. We treat this as a binding constraint on what we may claim. It is why we lean on the *binary* classification — Φ = 0 versus Φ > 0 — for the dyad/triad question, and read a positive Φ only ordinally within the model, never as a measure of how sophisticated a coordination is. The XOR-grid result is, in fact, visible inside our own modeling vocabulary, where parity determinations score among the highest Φ; we flag that as a property of the measure to reckon with, not a finding about coordination.

That one-sentence concession invites a sharper objection, and we would rather meet it than let it sit. The methodological literature on importing research methods holds that one should develop theory and method together, not bolt a ready-made instrument onto a question it was never shaped for (Lê & Schmid, 2022). On its face our move looks like the opposite — we are taking a tool off the shelf and calling it theory-neutral. The appearance is misleading. The theory this method is theory-neutral *toward* is its origin theory, consciousness, which we have set down. The theory it is being co-developed *with* is the organizational one — Paper 1's account of triadic coordination, which is what tells the instrument where to run, how to carve its states, and what a positive verdict means. With respect to the theory it is here to serve, method and theory are developed together exactly as the principle asks. The tool is borrowed; the construct it measures is ours, and the two were built to fit.

One thing follows that we will hold to throughout. The warrant for using Φ on coordination is not that it has been validated in neuroscience. Borrowed credibility is no credibility (Øby, 2025). The warrant is that the instrument behaves correctly on coordination cases whose structure we know in advance — which is a claim we have to earn in §5, not assume here.

## 3. The application layer

Before the instrument can run, we have to say what it runs *over*, and the answer resolves a problem that otherwise sinks any attempt to measure algorithmic coordination: the algorithm is a black box. The opacity is not incidental, and it is not only the analyst's problem. Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin (2020), reviewing how algorithms have become an instrument of control at work, describe systems that direct, evaluate, and discipline through mechanisms their own subjects cannot inspect; opacity is part of how the control operates. An approach that needed to open the box would be defeated before it started, because the box is held shut by the same party whose coordination we are trying to characterize.

The measure does not run over the algorithm's internal mechanism. It runs over the **application layer** — the states the two parties move through and the determinations the mediator commits between them. A driver logs in, holds a position, accepts or declines; the platform returns a dispatch or a price. A résumé is submitted; the system commits a forward or a reject; a manager calls back or does not. All of this is visible in ordinary interaction logs, and none of it requires seeing inside the model that produced it.

This is what dissolves the opacity problem rather than fighting it. The mechanism is opaque, and the analyst neither can see it nor needs to. Φ computed over the application-layer transition matrix is a property of the dynamics the parties actually operate in — the moves they make and the determinations that constrain them — not of the hidden process that generates those determinations. The distinction matters enough to draw it precisely, because three things that are easy to run together are genuinely different. *Epistemic opacity* is the analyst's inability to see the mechanism. *Formal irreducibility* is the structure's failure to factor over its partition. *Metaphysical emergence* is a claim that the whole is, in some inflationary sense, more than its parts in the world — a claim we are not making.

Keeping these apart is not pedantry; collapsing them is how measures of this kind get into trouble. The literature on causal emergence has spent two decades learning the difference between a coarse description that genuinely carries more causal structure than its fine-grained substrate and one that only appears to because the coarsening hid the relevant variation. Bedau (1997) gave the deflationary anchor — weak emergence, where the macro pattern is real but derivable only by running the system, with no spooky addition. Hoel (2017) showed that a macro description can, by an exact information measure, beat the micro it sits on. And critics have pressed, fairly, that some such gains are artifacts of how the macro variables were drawn rather than facts about the system (Eberhardt & Lee, 2022). We do not need to adjudicate that debate, and we are careful not to be drawn into it: our claim lives entirely on the second of the three notions. The cause-effect structure of the observed application-layer matrix either factors over its minimum-information partition or it does not, and which it does is a computation, not an interpretation.

So we state the claim and stop there: the cause-effect structure of the observed application-layer transition matrix is irreducible over its minimum-information partition. That is a computable fact about the dynamics the parties move through. It is not a claim that the analyst has seen into the algorithm, and it is not a claim that the coordination is metaphysically more than its parts in some inflationary sense. It is the narrow, checkable thing, and the narrowness is what makes it defensible.

The matrix that carries this claim is built from the logs alone, and the construction is mechanical once the parties and their determination-bearing signals are named. Three nodes carry the application layer: the worker, the mediating system, and the counterpart, each holding the value that matters for coordination at that moment — the relevant signal present or absent, the determination committed or not, the counterpart engaged or not. A system state is the joint configuration of those nodes between determinations. The transition matrix records, for each state, how each node's next determination-bearing value follows from the current configuration of all three — who constrains whom — which the committed determinations make readable in the log. Nothing in this requires the mechanism; it requires only the record of what the mediator committed and what each party did in response.

A platform engineers the very feature that makes the construction clean: it keeps the worker and the counterpart from reaching each other directly, routing everything through its own determinations. Rideshare drivers cannot contact riders off the app; the platform sits between them by design, and disintermediation is the point of the design, not an accident of it (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016). One participatory-design study with drivers caught the resulting phenomenology in its title — "you are you and the app, there's nobody else" (Stein et al., 2023). The worker experiences a dyad. Whether that is what she is in is the question the next sections answer.

## 4. The state alphabet

Everything the instrument reports rests on one prior decision, and we make it in the open. Φ is not invariant to how the application-layer states are individuated — carve the states one way and a form looks irreducible, carve them another and it factors. If the carving is done after the computation, to taste, the dyad/triad verdict is an artifact of the analyst's hand and worth nothing. So we fix the carving rule by definition, before any Φ is computed, and we defend it.

The rule is this: **a new application-layer state begins when the mediator commits a determination that alters its causal disposition toward the parties.** A determination is a commitment the mediator makes — a forward or a reject, a match or no match, a price, a rank, a score — that changes how it will afterward constrain what passes between the two parties. States are individuated by these commitments and by nothing else.

In the imported vocabulary the rule has an exact reading, and the reading is why it is the right rule. A determination is a transition that moves the mediator's cause repertoire and its effect repertoire together: it changes both what could have brought the system to this point and what the system now constrains downstream. That joint movement of cause and effect is what makes a transition irreducible rather than cosmetic, and it is exactly the quantity Φ is built to register. A change that moves neither repertoire — a re-rendering of a value the mediator has already committed — is not a new state, however much the interface dresses it up as one.

It helps to see the rule run. A rider opens the app (no determination: the mediator has committed nothing, so no new state), browses available cars shown to her (still nothing committed), and requests a ride. The request, on its own, is an action by a party; what begins a new application-layer state is the platform's *response* to it — the dispatch it commits, pairing this driver with this rider at this price. That dispatch alters how the platform will subsequently constrain both of them: the driver is now routed, the rider now charged, and the set of determinations available next has changed. A new state has begun. When the app later re-renders the fare it already set, in a larger font on a new screen, nothing new is committed and no new state begins, however much the interface has changed. The rule sorts the stream of interface events into the few that move the causal structure and the many that only move pixels.

Two rival criteria feel like state boundaries, and the rule turns both down. The first is *action availability*: the intuition that a new state begins whenever a party *could* act differently. But an option that commits nothing — a button she may or may not press — leaves the mediator's disposition toward the parties exactly where it was, and availability is a fact about the interface, not about the causal structure. The second is *visibility*: the intuition that a new state begins when a value becomes visible to a party. But disclosing a value the mediator has already committed changes who can read it, not what it constrains; the repertoires do not move, so the state does not begin. Both criteria mistake the surface of the interaction for its structure. The rule individuates on the structure, which is why two analysts applying it to the same logs carve the same states and reach the same Φ. That reproducibility is the whole point of fixing the rule in advance: it removes the analyst's discretion from the one place, the carving, where discretion would otherwise decide the verdict.

The rule also keeps the problem computable, and this is not a separate constraint bolted on but the same discipline seen from the other side. Φ grows super-exponentially in the number of states, so the state alphabet must stay small. The individuation rule keeps it small in a principled way: only determination-bearing transitions count, so the alphabet is governed by how often the mediator actually commits, not by every flicker on the screen. The rule that makes the verdict well-defined is the rule that makes it tractable.

One case strains the rule, and we name it now rather than let it ambush the empirical work to come. A platform that commits in a near-continuous stream — Uber re-pricing every surge tick, re-evaluating every match, re-weighting every repositioning — has no natural discrete boundary at which one application-layer state ends and the next begins. The rule says "a new state per committed determination," but when determinations arrive without pause the analyst must choose a window, and Φ depends on the choice. We do not resolve this. We pre-register it as the rule's open boundary and hand it forward to the empirical program, where an observed coordination outcome could one day supply the window the continuous case lacks, fixing the granularity by the scale at which Φ best tracks that outcome rather than by fiat. Until such a validation is in hand, the model is honest about where the rule is sharp (discrete, log-reconstructible determinations) and where it is not yet.

## 5. The model and its controls

We now build the model and, before running it on anything in dispute, make it prove itself on cases whose answers we already know.

A coordination form is modeled as a three-node application-layer system: the Worker (W), the System or mediator (S), and the Counterpart (C). Its state-by-node transition matrix records how each party's next determination-bearing value follows from the current state of all three. Over that matrix the instrument computes exact integrated information — specifically the IIT-4.0 system integrated information, the irreducibility of the system's cause-effect state over its minimum-information partition — by way of the repository's exact-Φ oracle, which wraps the standard PyPhi implementation (Marshall et al., 2023; Mayner et al., 2018). We name the quantity precisely because the theory offers more than one: this is the system-level φ over the minimum-information partition, not the structure-level sum over distinctions and relations, and the paper's claims attach to the former.

A word on what "validation" means here, because the methods genre has a default this paper deliberately departs from. The canonical sequence for building a measure is define, operationalize, confirm (Lambert & Newman, 2023; MacKenzie et al., 2011), and "confirm" ordinarily means demonstrating reliability and validity across a sample of respondents. Our instrument is formal, not psychometric, and there is no sampling step to run.

There is, however, a reason this is the *right* validation logic rather than a weaker substitute for the usual one. Cronbach and Meehl (1955) established that construct validity — as opposed to criterion or content validity — is precisely the mode of justification one is forced into when no external criterion is accepted as adequate to define the quality being measured, and their account turns on embedding the construct in a network of relations that generate specific, testable predictions. That is exactly our situation. There is no gold-standard "triadicity score" to validate against; the construct's content is its place in a nomological web, and the test is whether the measure behaves as that web predicts. The web makes sharp predictions: a form the construct calls dyadic must score zero, a form it calls triadic must score positive, and a form that only looks like one or the other must follow its structure rather than its appearance. "Confirm," for a formal instrument, means holding it to those predictions on cases whose structure is fixed in advance. We adapt the genre's third step rather than skip it, and we say so plainly so that a reader does not read the adaptation as an omission.

Two controls fix the ends of the scale.

The first is a **factoring** form, in which the counterpart is causally decoupled — the worker and mediator are coupled to each other, but nothing reads or writes the counterpart (W′ = S, S′ = W, C′ = C). The system splits cleanly into {W, S} and {C}, and there is nothing for an integration measure to find. The instrument returns Φ = 0.000 at every reachable state. This is the result the theory demands: a strictly modular system carries no integrated information (Balduzzi & Tononi, 2008).

The second is an **irreducible** form, in which every party reads the other two (W′ = S ∨ C, S′ = W ∧ C, C′ = W ⊕ S). No partition splits this without loss. The instrument returns Φ as high as 0.830, and the minimum-information partition is the full tripartition {W, S, C} — the system cannot be reduced along any line, including the line between the parties.

These two are not arbitrary. They are the endpoints the construct's own predictions demand: a form the construct calls maximally dyadic, where one party is causally inert, must read zero, and a form where the parties are maximally entangled must read positive and resist every partition. An instrument that failed either would be measuring something other than what we mean by irreducibility — returning Φ > 0 for a system that plainly factors, or Φ = 0 for one that plainly does not — and we would have no business pointing it at a case whose answer we do not already know. Passing them is necessary, not sufficient; it is the floor the methods genre rightly insists on before a measure is turned loose.

Zero where the structure factors, positive where it cannot be factored at all. Only once the instrument clears these two controls do we let it classify anything contested. And because the controls and everything after them are computed, not argued, the published instrument code regenerates every number in this paper from the rules that define each case — which means a skeptical reader does not have to take the values on trust; she can rerun them, and she can change a rule and watch the verdict move.

## 6. The model at work

With the controls passed, we run the model on coordination forms whose modeled classifications the construct fixes in advance. These are not illustrations chosen to flatter the measure. Each is a case where Paper 1 already tells us the answer, and the question is whether the model agrees — including the two cases where agreement means classifying against what the situation looks like.

**The dyadic limit.** A worker in conversation with a language model is the clean two-party case: she prompts, the model answers, she responds to the answer, and the model commits nothing that couples a third party (W′ = S, S′ = W, C′ = C). The instrument returns Φ = 0.000 at every state. The form factors to the two parties already visible in it, and there is nothing the worker must reason *through* to a third — the whole of her situation is the back-and-forth in front of her. This is the home ground of the dyadic constructs, the case algorithmic literacy was built for, and the measure agrees with them: it is a dyad. We include it not because the verdict is surprising but because a measure that could not return zero on the obvious dyad would have no standing to return positive on the contested case. The floor matters as much as the ceiling.

**The irreducible triad.** A résumé passes through an applicant-tracking system to a hiring manager. The applicant and the manager never deal with each other directly; each reaches the other only through the determination the system commits — the forward or the reject (W′ = S, C′ = S), which the system sets as a joint function of the résumé's signal and the manager's configured profile (S′ = W ∧ C). The verdict is positive and it is stable. Across every determination that genuinely reads both sides — and, or, nand, nor — the instrument returns Φ = 2.000, with the minimum-information partition falling between the worker and the system-counterpart pair, {W, SC}; for the two parity determinations, xor and xnor, it returns a lower but still positive Φ = 0.500 with the minimum-information partition now the full tripartition {W, S, C}. What the result is robust to is telling: the form stays irreducible for *any* determination that is a genuine joint function of both parties, and the exact value moves with how the determination mixes them, not with whether the structure is a triad. The structure does not factor, whichever way the mediator combines the two sides, even though the two human parties never touch. Already this is the more interesting direction of misclassification in miniature: the configuration that most invites a two-separate-dyads model, two parties with no link between them, is the one the measure flags as a single irreducible triad.

**The false triad.** Now the first overruling. Take the same three-party setup, but let the mediator's determination ignore one side — the system forwards on the résumé's signal alone, indifferent to the manager's profile (S′ = W). Three parties are present, on the interface and in the room. The instrument returns Φ = 0.000. The determination reads only one party, so the structure collapses onto the pair, and the third is a body without a causal role. Three parties on the surface, two in the structure; the measure overrules the count.

The case is not a contrivance. A hiring pipeline can route every résumé by a fixed keyword filter and only *show* the manager the survivors, his preferences never feeding back into what the filter does; a content queue can rank by a policy the moderator cannot tune. Each looks like a three-party coordination and each, structurally, is a worker dealing with a system while a third party watches a feed. An analyst counting heads, or trusting the org chart, would call these triads and reach for algorithmacy; the measure says they are dyads and literacy will do. This is the direction of error that the dyadic constructs do *not* make, and it matters precisely because it shows the instrument is not a machine for finding triads everywhere — it withholds the label exactly where the third party, though present, does no causal work.

**The false dyad.** And the second, which is the one the instrument exists for. A rideshare driver works a system that presents to her as nothing but an app — she taps, she is dispatched, and she can neither see nor choose the rider. Modeled honestly, though, the rider is constitutive of the dispatch the platform commits: the platform dispatches only when an available driver and a waiting rider both exist (S′ = W ∧ C), the driver's availability is consumed by the dispatch (W′ = ¬S), and the rider waits until a dispatch serves her (C′ = C ∧ ¬S). The driver and rider never interact directly; they are bound only through the platform. The instrument returns Φ = 2.000, with the partition again at {W, SC}. The structure the driver cannot see is an irreducible triad.

The sharpness of the result is worth holding still for. Strip the rider out of the determination — model the dispatch as a function of the driver alone, S′ = W, which is exactly what a dyadic construct does when it studies "the driver's relationship with the app" — and with every other rule unchanged the instrument returns Φ = 0.000. One edge of the model, the dependence of the dispatch on the rider, is the whole difference between a triad and a dyad. Everything the driver experiences is identical across the two models: the same app, the same taps, the same dispatch arriving on the same screen. What differs is invisible to her and decisive for the structure: whether the determination that governs her was, in fact, a joint function of a party she never meets. Include that party and the form is an irreducible triad; drop her and it is a dyad. The misclassification a dyadic construct makes is therefore not a misreading of the data in front of it; it is the prior choice of where to draw the system's boundary, of leaving the constitutive third party outside the frame. The instrument's contribution is to make that choice visible and consequential — to show that the boundary the dyadic constructs draw is not neutral bookkeeping but the thing that produces the dyadic answer.

This is not a toy. The triangular structure of platform work — platform, worker, and a third party who is the customer — is by now well established across three literatures that rarely cite one another, and the instrument computes what all three describe. The sociology of platform labor has theorized the triangle directly: Stark and Pais (2020) place the platform at the apex of a triangular geometry with producers and users at the other corners, Azaïs and Dieuaide (2020) cast the employment relation itself as a "worker–platform–customer" triangle, and Healy and Pekarek (2025) show the consumer *mediates* the worker–platform relationship rather than decorating it. Vallas and Schor (2020), surveying what platforms actually do, conclude that they are a distinct mode of governance, not reducible to the market, the hierarchy, or the network that organizational theory already has names for — which is to say the structure resists the two-party templates by default.

The formal economics says the same thing in its own register. The theory of two-sided markets holds that a platform's two sides cannot be analyzed as two independent markets, because price and participation on one side move usage on the other; they are one interdependent market with the platform constitutive of the coupling (Rochet & Tirole, 2003). Cross-side network effects make the point sharply: each side's value depends on the other side's participation, a dependence carried entirely by the platform, so the third party is causally present in the worker's situation even when she never sees it (Parker & Van Alstyne, 2005). And the boundary case matters — a multi-sided platform on the standard account *enables* direct interaction between its sides, so a gig system that forbids exactly that, binding the parties only through its own determinations, sits on the managed-intermediary side of the line where the coupling is tightest (Hagiu & Wright, 2015). The most direct confirmation comes from inside the strategy literature itself: a recent analysis of food-delivery platforms finds that the three-sided case does not inherit the results of the two-sided model its strategists reach for (Sun et al., 2024). What every one of these literatures has asserted, the instrument computes: the third party is not context, it is structure.

**The incentive, computed.** One result remains, and it is the one that turns the classifier from a referee of cases into a source of theory. Hold the determination fixed (S′ = W ∧ C) and vary one thing — how much the worker and counterpart can deal with each other directly. With no direct channel, the parties reaching each other only through the system, Φ is 2.00. Add a weak direct channel and it falls to 0.83. Make the direct channel strong enough that the parties can coordinate without the system and it falls to 0.00 — the triad reduces to a dyad. Coordination is most irreducible when the dyad between the parties is eliminated, and irreducibility bleeds away exactly as the parties gain the ability to route around the mediator.

This has a precise echo in the source field, which is part of why we trust it. The information-theoretic study of emergence has a name for what happens when components become able to influence the system's future without going through the rest of it — causal decoupling — and treats the suppression of such direct paths as what concentrates a system's irreducible, whole-level structure (Rosas et al., 2020). Our sweep is that mechanism seen from the platform's side: suppressing the direct worker–counterpart path is what concentrates the irreducibility on which the platform's position depends.

We did not assert the result; we computed it within the model, and the computation does what a formal model is supposed to do in this field — it generates a proposition rather than merely testing one (Davis et al., 2007). The proposition is about political economy, and we offer it as a hypothesis the model suggests rather than a fact it establishes: a platform's position, *as the model scores it*, is constituted by the irreducibility of the coordination it sits inside, direct contact between the parties lowers that irreducibility, and so the platform has a structural incentive to design the dyad out — to keep the worker and the counterpart from ever coordinating except through its own committed determinations. This reframes a familiar observation. The disintermediation that the platform literature documents as a tactic of control (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016) is, on the model, not only a way to capture rents but the very thing that keeps the modeled coordination triadic; preventing the parties from meeting is, in the model, constitutive of the platform's structural position rather than incidental to it. The model says why doing it might pay. It is also exactly the kind of proposition that would want validating against an observed outcome — work the dissertation's empirical program would take up, not work this paper claims to have done.

## 7. What the instrument establishes, and what it does not

The model answers one question and leaves another open by design, and the contribution is sharpest when the two are kept apart.

What it establishes is a formal model. Φ over the modeled application-layer transition matrix operationalizes whether a coordination form is triadic — whether the modeled cause-effect structure is irreducible to the parties — and therefore whether the form, as modeled, demands the competency[^term] Paper 1 called algorithmacy rather than the literacy a dyad asks for. This is the apparatus Paper 1's construct required and could not supply from within its own argument. The link from the measure to the competency is not loose. A reducible form is navigable one hop at a time: each party can reason about the party it deals with directly, and the local picture is the whole picture. An irreducible form denies a worker that economy. Because the structure does not factor, no part of it is self-contained; to act well she must reason forward through a determination she does not control to a counterpart she cannot see, and backward from the determination to what produced it — exactly the demand Paper 1 named and exactly the demand that a dyadic, one-hop competency does not meet. The measure is what tells the two situations apart before a single worker is studied, which is what lets the rest of the research program target the cases that actually carry the construct.

We should be candid about where this sits in a longer arc, because it is easy to overstate the novelty. The toolkit of irreducibility has already reached social coordination. Engel and Malone (2018) used a measure of this family to gauge how far a human group's behavior exceeds its individual members; Niizato and colleagues (2020) computed IIT Φ on coordinating fish schools; Mediano and colleagues (2025) developed the decomposition of integration into its synergistic and redundant parts; others have turned information-theoretic synergy on multi-agent collectives. What these efforts share is a question about a *group* — whether a set of broadly symmetric agents adds up to more than its members — and that is not our question. Our structure has an asymmetry at its center: a mediator that commits determinations, and two parties that reach each other only through it and never directly. The interesting quantity is not whether the trio exceeds its members in general, but whether the third party, the one the worker cannot see, is doing causal work or merely present. No one has pointed an irreducibility measure at that structure, and the framing it requires — the mediator as constitutive of the coupling, the third party as the variable whose inclusion or omission flips the verdict — is what the prior work, aimed at undifferentiated groups, did not need and did not supply. Our contribution is the application and that framing, not the invention of the measure, and saying so plainly is what keeps the claim defensible. A reviewer who knows the integrated-information literature will know Engel and Malone got there first with the tool; what is new is the object it is turned on and the use it is put to.

Three boundaries bound it, and we state them up front because they are what make the rest credible. First, the tool applies to discrete systems specified by a transition matrix (the class PyPhi computes), and our application-layer matrices are exactly that, so we are within scope; but Φ is not a universal solvent, and we claim nothing about systems that cannot be put in this form. Second, a single scalar Φ aggregates several distinct modes of integration that a finer decomposition would separate (Mediano et al., 2025); the binary classification we lean on, Φ = 0 against Φ > 0, is robust to this — a structure either factors or it does not — but the magnitude of a positive Φ blends heterogeneous kinds of integration into one number, and any reading of *how* triadic a form is, rather than *whether* it is, would have to reckon with that — and with the fact that Φ can run high for trivially regular structures (Cerullo, 2015), which is why we read magnitude only ordinally and never as sophistication. Third, the irreducibility is a formal property of the observable dynamics, and we keep it carefully apart from two neighbors it is easy to slide into: it is not the analyst's inability to see the mechanism (epistemic opacity), and it is not a claim that the coordination is metaphysically more than its parts (strong emergence), a reading the causal-emergence literature itself treats as level-relative and contested (Eberhardt & Lee, 2022). The claim is the narrow, computable one, and its narrowness is its strength.

Two things are deliberately unfinished. The classification depends on the modeling choices, above all the state alphabet, which is why §4 pre-registers the individuation rule rather than hiding the dependence; and a Φ value is a property of the model, not yet a validated difficulty scale. A number that separates modeled triads from modeled dyads does not yet say how hard a given triad is to navigate, and it cannot until the model is validated against an observed coordination outcome. That validation — together with the continuous-stream granularity that §4 left open — is deferred to the empirical program the dissertation opens; Paper 3 takes the model across a space of forms and a typology of organizations, not toward its validation against an outcome.

This paper sits deliberately between the other two, and a three-paper dissertation earns its coherence or fails for the lack of it. The risk in the format is that three papers become three slices of one undifferentiated study, each leaning on the others to look complete; the discipline that avoids it is to give each paper a job the others cannot do and a contribution that stands without them. Paper 1 develops the construct and argues that some coordination is triadic — conceptual work that needs no model. This paper is the bridge, and its job is the one neither neighbor can do for it: it turns the construct's central distinction from an assertion into a computation from a stated model, supplying the scope condition with an operationalization. Paper 3 takes the model across a space of forms and a typology of organizations — work that needs the apparatus this paper hands it but not the argument that built it. Each could be read on its own; together they share one object, and naming it is what binds them. The thing Paper 1 names, this paper models, and Paper 3 explores across organizations is one and the same: the irreducibility of coordination to its parts. Φ over the application layer classifies whether a modeled coordination form has it. How the model behaves across organizations is, by design, where Paper 3 begins.

[^term]: We call algorithmacy a *competency* throughout. The term keeps the construct legible to the literatures it must be distinguished from (CMC competence, algorithmic competency) and to the construct-clarity and measurement program these papers build on it. We think *sensibility* is the better long-run name for a capacity exercised under opacity and constant change, and we are developing the construct in that direction; we retain *competency* here to stay within the standard.
