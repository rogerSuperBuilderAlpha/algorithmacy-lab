# Abstract

Equivalently positioned workers passing through identical algorithmic systems receive divergent coordination outcomes, and the constructs the field has for studying work with algorithms do not explain the variance. This dissertation argues that the puzzle is the surface of a structural change: coordination has become triadic. A worker now coordinates with a counterpart she may never meet, a rider, a hiring manager, a customer, through a system that interprets both, acts on both, and commits determinations neither party controls. The existing constructs are built around a dyad, a person and a system, and cannot reach this triad; the competency it demands, which the dissertation names algorithmacy, was unnamed and unmeasured.

The dissertation develops the construct and builds a formal model of the condition that demands it, then grades that model across organizations, in three papers ordered from the conceptual to the formal. Paper 1, an integrative review, establishes that the triadic coordination form is real, that the dyadic constructs cannot be extended to it, and specifies algorithmacy to the discipline of construct clarity. Paper 2 adopts integrated information theory's measure of irreducibility, Φ, as a formal model: a coordination form is modeled as triadic exactly when its application-layer structure does not factor into independent parties under any partition (Φ over the minimum-information partition is positive), and as dyadic when some partition factors it — a condition that is computable, that the paper checks on controls whose answers are known, that it shows is strictly stronger than a cheap factorization test, and that it presents as a modeling choice — with the measure's standing critiques acknowledged — rather than as a claimed identity. The verdict is fixed once the model is declared in full, and bounded by that declaration: the four-question criterion narrows the modeling choices without fixing them, so a different faithful model, argued in the open, can give a different verdict. Paper 3 asks how far the model can be made graded, and is exact about where the answer is "not far." It computes Φ across the complete family of three-node forms, places a typology of named organizations on the bands the family produces, and codes five large corporations by the structure of how they coordinate their parties. One result is robust: the binary dyad/triad classification is clean across the whole form family, and over that family it is strictly stronger than a cheap factorization test, not a heavy reimplementation of one. Φ as a fine-grained scale fails. Its magnitude is dominated by the determination function, a disjunctive determination inflating it an order of magnitude beyond any real organization, so magnitude is read only ordinally, if at all, and the binary verdict carries the dyad/triad question. The paper's demonstration is a coding result. The score is computed from structure and takes no input about the mediator's nature, so two forms coded to the same structure receive the same score by construction. A securities exchange and a ride-hailing platform code to the same structure and the same Φ; a staffing firm and a freelance marketplace do the same. The equality is a property of the coding, not a discovery that a market and a platform are alike. That the model scores a human-mediated court at the same level as an algorithmic platform of the same structure shows it is indifferent to the mediator's seat, read as a property of the model under a shared coding, not an empirical finding about courts and platforms. The five cases are provisional, modeled from public documentation with fieldwork deferred, so Paper 3 is a formal-model result demonstrated on real cases, not a measure validated against an observed coordination outcome. That fuller validation is the empirical program it opens.

Together the three papers turn "coordination is becoming triadic" from an observation into a formal apparatus: a construct, a formal model of the condition that demands it, and a model-internal landscape that grades it. A word on what the dissertation does and does not deliver, because the two are easy to run together. Its object is two coupled things — the *demand* a coordination form makes (a property of the form) and the *capacity* a worker brings to it (a property of the person) — and the dissertation builds and demonstrates the demand side while *naming and specifying, but not measuring,* the capacity side. Algorithmacy, the competency, is specified to the discipline of construct clarity in Paper 1; the instrument that would measure a given worker's algorithmacy is named as the program's next arc, not delivered here. What is delivered is a formal model of the triadic demand that competency answers to. The empirical validation of that model against observed coordination outcomes, and the matched, person-level instrument, are the program this work opens.

*Keywords:* algorithmacy; triadic coordination; algorithmic management; platform work; integrated information; construct development; formal modeling.
