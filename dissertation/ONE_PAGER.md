# Dissertation Summary: Application Layer Communication

This dissertation examines how human coordination changes when an active algorithmic system sits in the middle of the exchange.

## Paper 1: Naming the Construct (Integrative Review)

We begin with a practical puzzle: equivalently positioned gig workers can put in the exact same effort on the exact same platform and achieve wildly different outcomes. Existing frameworks, such as algorithmic literacy or algorithm sensemaking, cannot explain this variance because they treat the system as a passive tool connecting two people. In reality, the algorithm is an active, self-interested third party that evaluates and transforms the interaction on its own terms. What was once a dyadic relationship between two people is now structurally triadic. Paper 1 argues that navigating this requires a fundamentally different adaptation, defining the specific sensibility required to operate within this triadic structure and naming it *algorithmacy*.

## Paper 2: Building the Structural Test (Construct & Measurement)

If algorithmacy is required for triadic coordination, researchers need a reliable way to prove when a system actually functions as a triad. Surface-level features—like the number of users or the UI—do not accurately reflect the underlying structure. Paper 2 borrows a concept from Integrated Information Theory (Phi, or Φ) to measure structural irreducibility. If the coordination can be broken down into isolated two-party exchanges, Φ is zero. If it is a genuine, irreducible triad, Φ is positive. Applying this measure reveals a critical policy insight: a platform's coordination becomes most irreducible when the two human parties are prevented from communicating directly, giving platforms a structural incentive to enforce isolation.

## Paper 3: Empirical Calibration (Scale Development)

Paper 3 translates this structural test into a continuous scale, functioning much like a reading-comprehension formula, but for coordination complexity. Using public Chicago rideshare data, the paper grounds the scale empirically, demonstrating that coordination forms with higher structural complexity are measurably harder to execute (e.g., they take longer per mile and fail more often). Crucially, the scale measures the underlying architecture, not the technology. A traditional courtroom—where two sides reach each other only through a judge's rulings—scores identically to a ride-hailing platform with the same structure.

Ultimately, this dissertation quantifies the structural *demand* that triadic systems place on people, laying the exact groundwork needed for future research to measure how well individual workers meet that demand.
