# The Transition to Algorithmacy — one-pager

**Roger Hunt III, Bentley University.** A three-paper dissertation. The papers move from naming a new
form of coordination, to measuring whether a given case is that form, to calibrating how much the form
demands of the people inside it.

**Paper 1 (integrative review) — names the construct.** The review starts from a puzzle the field cannot
explain: equivalently positioned workers passing through identical algorithmic systems get divergent
coordination outcomes. It argues the puzzle is the surface of a structural change. Coordination has become
triadic: a worker now coordinates with a counterpart she may never meet, a rider, a hiring manager, a
customer, through a system that interprets both, acts on both, and pursues objectives of its own across the
two of them. Reviewing the constructs the field uses to study work with algorithms, including
computer-mediated communication competence, human-machine and AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and
AI literacy, and algorithm sensemaking, the paper shows that every one is built around a dyad, a person and
a system, and cannot be extended to reach the triad. It represents each construct in its strongest form
before showing where it stops short, names the missing competency *algorithmacy*, and specifies it to the
discipline of construct clarity, opening the measurement program the next two papers carry out.

**Paper 2 (construct + measurement) — builds the test.** Paper 1 leaves a debt: if algorithmacy is what a
triad demands, an analyst must be able to say whether a given case is a triad, and the triad does not
announce itself. Paper 2 supplies the test. The distinction between a dyad and a triad is structural, a
form is triadic when its coordination does not reduce to the pairwise dealings of the parties, and that
property, irreducibility to the parts, is exactly what integrated information theory's measure, Φ, was
built to quantify. The paper imports Φ as a found tool, models a coordination form as a three-node system
of worker, system, and counterpart, fixes the rule for carving the form into states before any computation,
validates the instrument on cases whose structure is known, and then runs it: Φ is zero when the structure
factors (a dyad) and positive when it does not (a triad). It demonstrates the procedure on worked cases,
including the two where the verdict overrules appearance, a three-party form that is really a dyad and a
worker-alone-with-an-app that is really a triad, and it computes a result with a political-economy edge:
coordination is most irreducible when the parties are kept from coordinating directly, so a platform has a
structural reason to design the dyad out.

**Paper 3 (empirical calibration) — turns the test into a scale.** A verdict is not yet a measure. Paper 3
calibrates the decision procedure into a portable scale that places any organization's coordination on one
continuum of triadic demand, the way a readability score places a text on a scale of reading difficulty,
and like a readability score it is a property of the form, not of any individual. The paper anchors the
scale against an observed coordination outcome in rideshare pooling, where Φ computed from structure alone
tracks the friction of the coordination that the data record, a relationship that is strong, stable across
time, and robust within geographic strata. It then places a typology of organizations on the calibrated
scale, from dyadic baselines at the floor to higher-order forms at the top. Its central finding is that the
scale measures coordination and not technology: a human-mediated court scores the same as an algorithmic
platform of the same structure, and a staffing agency scores the same as an online freelance marketplace.
The dissertation thus measures the demand a coordination form makes; the matched instrument that would
measure a worker's capacity to meet it is the program this work opens.
