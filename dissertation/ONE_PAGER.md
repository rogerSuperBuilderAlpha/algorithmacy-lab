# The Transition to Algorithmacy — Dissertation Summary

**Roger Hunt III · Bentley University**

The dissertation concerns how the structure of human coordination changes when an algorithmic system mediates between two parties, and what competency that change demands. It comprises three papers, ordered from the conceptual to the formal. Together they develop a construct, a formal model of the condition that demands it, and a characterization of that model across the space of coordination forms.

## Paper 1 — Naming the construct (integrative review)

Equivalently positioned platform participants obtain divergent coordination outcomes through identical algorithmic systems, and the constructs available for studying work with algorithms do not explain the variance. The paper argues that these constructs share a dyadic unit of analysis: a person on one side of a medium that conveys, scores, or interprets but does not pursue objectives of its own across both human parties. The constructs are computer-mediated-communication competence, human–machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, and algorithm sensemaking. Platform-mediated coordination, the paper contends, is structurally triadic: a worker coordinates with a counterpart she may never meet through a system that interprets both parties and commits determinations neither controls. The paper represents each existing construct in its strongest form, shows where each stops short of the triad, and specifies a new construct, *algorithmacy*, to the standards of construct clarity: definition, scope conditions, dimensions, and a nomological network. The contribution is conceptual. It supplies a specification for a competency the field's existing vocabulary cannot reach, and it opens the measurement program that specification requires.

## Paper 2 — Modeling whether a form is triadic (construct development and formal model)

The surface of a coordination form does not reveal whether it is triadic, because the number of parties and the interface underdetermine the underlying structure. The paper adopts integrated information theory's measure, Φ, as a formal model of structural irreducibility: a coordination form is modeled as triadic when its application-layer structure does not factor into independent parties under any partition (Φ over the minimum-information partition is positive) and as dyadic when some partition factors it (Φ = 0). The paper is explicit that this is a modeling choice rather than a claimed identity. It rests on stated commitments: the application-layer representation, a pre-registered state-individuation rule, and reading irreducibility over the minimum-information partition. It acknowledges the standing critiques of the borrowed measure. It shows, over the complete 4,096-form family, that the binary verdict diverges from a cheap factorization test on more than 40% of cases, so Φ is strictly stronger rather than a heavy reimplementation. Worked examples classify against appearance in both directions: a three-party arrangement that factors to a dyad, and a worker-alone-with-an-app arrangement that does not. The binary "eliminate the dyad" result is robust at its endpoints, since suppressing the direct channel is what keeps the form classified as a triad. The paper withdraws the earlier claim of a smooth magnitude decline. The intermediate Φ depends on the encoding, with a conjunctive blend raising Φ where a disjunctive one lowers it, and a genuine joint determination is necessary but not sufficient, since the verdict also depends on the read structure. The political-economy proposition is offered at the binary level only, as a hypothesis the model generates rather than a validated claim.

## Paper 3 — Grading and testing the model (formal model, population study, and experiment)

Paper 3 asks how far the binary classifier can be made graded, and is exact about where it can and cannot. It computes Φ across the complete family of three-node forms (a coverage check, since most wirings correspond to no recognizable organization), across a parametric population of eighty-six simulated organizations spanning party count, mediation regime, determination function, and back-channel topology, and across a typology of named real organizations. Two results hold robustly. The first is the binary dyad/triad classification, strictly stronger than a cheap factorization test. The second, from an agent-based experiment, is the behavioral confirmation that coordinating through a strict joint determination with no direct channel is markedly harder than through the same determination with a channel left open, a large effect that survives training to three thousand rounds. A third result, the finer ordering of regimes by Φ magnitude, is *not* robustly supported in the population. With standard errors and a full-rank design only the determination function moves Φ significantly, and strict and partial mediation are statistically indistinguishable once party count is held fixed, so the ordering rests on the experiment rather than the population. That experiment reads as a behavioral consistency check, where Φ and difficulty share a structural cause, rather than an independent validation. Φ as a fine-grained scale fails outright: its magnitude is dominated by the determination function, with a disjunctive determination inflating it an order of magnitude beyond any real organization. The named organizations occupy the moderate band of the population, well below this structurally possible but organization-less tail. Paper 3 therefore presents Φ as a structural classifier and, at most, an ordinal indicator validated behaviorally at one contrast, not a readability score. Its cross-technology headline scores a court like a platform of the same structure and a human staffing agency like an algorithmic marketplace. That headline holds as a property of the model under a shared coding, where the same structure is assigned to both, not as a discovered sameness in the world. Independent inter-rater coding of organizational structures is named as required work.

Taken together, the three papers move the claim that coordination has become triadic from observation to formal apparatus: a specified construct, a computable model of the structural condition that demands it, a characterization of that model across a population of organizations, and a behavioral test of its central prediction. Three tasks make up the research program the dissertation opens: a fuller validation of the model, a decomposition that would make its magnitude a reliable scale, and a matched, person-level instrument of worker competency.
