# The Transition to Algorithmacy — Dissertation Summary

**Roger Hunt III · Bentley University**

The dissertation concerns how the structure of human coordination changes when an algorithmic system mediates between two parties, and what competency that structural change demands. It comprises three papers, ordered from the conceptual to the formal, that together develop a construct, a formal model of the condition that demands it, and a characterization of that model across the space of coordination forms.

## Paper 1 — Naming the construct (integrative review)

Equivalently positioned platform participants obtain divergent coordination outcomes through identical algorithmic systems, and the constructs available for studying work with algorithms do not explain the variance. The paper argues that these constructs—computer-mediated-communication competence, human–machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, and algorithm sensemaking—share a dyadic unit of analysis: a person on one side of a medium that conveys, scores, or interprets but does not pursue objectives of its own across both human parties. Platform-mediated coordination, it contends, is structurally triadic: a worker coordinates with a counterpart she may never meet through a system that interprets both parties and commits determinations neither controls. Through an integrative review the paper represents each existing construct in its strongest form, shows where each stops short of the triad, and specifies a new construct, *algorithmacy*, to the standards of construct clarity—definition, scope conditions, dimensions, and a nomological network. The contribution is conceptual: a specification for a competency the field's existing vocabulary cannot reach, and the measurement program it opens.

## Paper 2 — Modeling whether a form is triadic (construct development and formal model)

Whether a given coordination form is triadic is not given by its surface, since the number of parties and the interface underdetermine the underlying structure. The paper adopts integrated information theory's measure, Φ, as a formal model of structural irreducibility: a coordination form is modeled as triadic when its application-layer transition structure does not factor across the worker–system–counterpart partition (Φ > 0) and as dyadic when it does (Φ = 0). It is explicit that this is a modeling choice resting on three stated commitments—the application-layer representation, a pre-registered state-individuation rule, and the party partition—rather than a claimed identity between Φ and triadic demand, and it acknowledges and answers the standing critiques of the borrowed measure. Worked examples demonstrate that the model can classify against appearance in both directions, identifying a three-party arrangement that factors to a dyad and a worker-alone-with-an-app arrangement that does not. A sensitivity analysis shows that the modeled irreducibility is greatest when the two parties are prevented from coordinating directly, a proposition the model generates about platform design rather than a validated empirical claim.

## Paper 3 — Grading and testing the model (formal model, population study, and experiment)

Paper 3 asks how far the binary classifier can be made graded, and is exact about where it can and cannot. It computes Φ across the complete family of three-node forms (a coverage check, since most wirings correspond to no recognizable organization), across a parametric population of eighty-six simulated organizations spanning party count, mediation regime, determination function, and back-channel topology, and across a typology of named real organizations. Three results hold robustly: the binary dyad/triad classification; the mediation-regime ordering, in which strictly mediated coordination—where the parties reach each other only through a joint determination—is the most demanding once the determination function is controlled; and, in a non-circular agent-based experiment, the behavioral confirmation that coordinating through a strict joint determination with no direct channel is markedly harder than through the same determination with a channel left open. One result does not hold: Φ as a fine-grained scale, because its magnitude is dominated by the determination function, with a disjunctive determination inflating it an order of magnitude beyond any real organization. The named organizations occupy the moderate band of the population, well below this structurally-possible but organization-less tail. Paper 3 therefore presents Φ as a structural classifier and an ordinal, regime-level indicator of triadic demand rather than a readability score, with the headline that the score responds to determination structure and not to the mediator's nature: a court is modeled like a platform of the same structure, and a human staffing agency like an algorithmic marketplace.

Taken together, the three papers move the claim that coordination has become triadic from observation to formal apparatus: a specified construct, a computable model of the structural condition that demands it, a characterization of that model across a population of organizations, and a behavioral test of its central prediction. A fuller validation of the model, a decomposition that would make its magnitude a reliable scale, and a matched, person-level instrument of worker competency constitute the research program the dissertation opens.
