## §3/§5.2 Triadic structure / brokerage / higher-order networks (the object's pedigree)

Establishes that *the triad is the irreducible minimal unit of social structure* — the structural lineage §3
draws on to argue a triad is not a long dyad. **Framing caveat (load-bearing):** classical brokerage =
a **human** *tertius* between two others (conduit / tertius gaudens / tertius iungens). Algorithmacy's third
party is **not a human broker** — it is a non-human system pursuing its own objectives across both parties.
So this literature supplies the *irreducibility pedigree* (and a precise contrast case), **not** the
construct itself; cite it to fix the object, then mark where the algorithmic tertius departs.

- **Halevy, N., Halali, E., & Zlatev, J. J. (2019).** Brokerage and brokering: an integrative review and
  organizing framework for third-party influence. *Academy of Management Annals* (153 cites). **In the target
  venue; the canonical third-party-influence synthesis — anchor §3's brokerage pedigree and a model for the
  Annals register.** **NEW (high value).**
- **Obstfeld, D., Borgatti, S. P., & Davis, J. (2014).** Brokerage as a process: decoupling third-party
  action from social network structure. (the conduit / tertius gaudens / tertius iungens **process** triad —
  the behavioral typology to contrast the algorithmic tertius against.) **NEW.**
- **Siltaloppi, J., & Vargo, S. L. (2017).** Triads (brokerage / mediation / coalition — three forms of the
  three-actor system). *Marketing Theory.* (the cleanest cross-disciplinary statement that *the triad is the
  smallest unit of a network*; **directly supports the §3 "triad is irreducible" claim**.) **NEW.**
- **Nooteboom, B. (2006).** Simmel's treatise on the triad (1908). *J. Institutional Economics.* (bridges
  Simmel's triad to organization/innovation studies — the citable route to **Simmel on triadic
  irreducibility**, which §3 already invokes.) **NEW.**
- **Battiston, F., et al. (2025).** Higher-order interactions shape collective human behaviour. *Nature Human
  Behaviour* (49 cites). (mechanisms "invisible in dyadic models" — the contemporary, formal "beyond dyads"
  warrant; ties the §3 claim to the higher-order-networks frontier and forward to Paper 2's irreducibility
  formalism.) **NEW (optional but strong).**
- *(Supply-chain "service triad" literature — Vlachos 2020; Jraisat 2021 — confirms triads recur as a
  coordination problem across fields, but is empirics-heavy; cite at most one as breadth evidence, not core.)*

## §3 The three constitutive conditions + coordination-mechanism test (signal asymmetry / intent compression / opaque mediation)

The conceptual anchors for §3's three conditions and the integrating-conditions test (hierarchy/market/
network each fail inside the form). The connector surfaces a lot of finance/supply-chain signaling noise;
the two management-grade anchors are the keepers.

- **Spence, M. (1973).** Job market signaling. *QJE.* (the **signal-asymmetry** foundation — the origin of
  signaling theory the §3 condition rests on; should be in base — verify.) *foundational.*
- **Bergh, D. D., et al. (2018).** Information asymmetry in management research: past accomplishments and
  future opportunities. *J. Management* (408 cites; review of 223 articles). **The canonical management review
  of information asymmetry — anchor the §3 signal-asymmetry condition in the management literature, not just
  economics.** **NEW (high value).**
- **Felin, T., & Zenger, T. R. (2011).** Information aggregation, matching and radical market–hierarchy
  hybrids. *Strategic Organization* (38 cites). **Markets vs hierarchy as governance forms distinguished by

  market each fall short inside the triad) and the market–hierarchy-hybrid framing of platforms.** **NEW.**
- *Coordination-theory base (verify in references_base.md, all foundational):* Malone & Crowston 1994

  hierarchy — network form); Williamson 1975/1985 (markets & hierarchies / TCE); Thompson 1967 (coordination

its defense against the **New Literacy Studies / ideological-model** objection (literacy is material
enablement, not cognitive-universalist "great divide").

- **Ong, W. J. (1982).** Orality and literacy: the technologizing of the word. (5,220 cites; "writing
  restructures consciousness" — **the anchor for the in-kind-restructuring claim**; verify in base.)

  Savage Mind* / Goody & Watt (1963); Olson, D. R. (1994) *The World on Paper*; Eisenstein, E. (1979) *The

- **The NLS / ideological-model defense (load-bearing — secures the warrant against relativist critique):**
  - **Street, B. (1984)** *Literacy in Theory and Practice* — the **ideological vs autonomous model** (the
    canonical objection to answer); verify in base.
  - **Scribner, S., & Cole, M. (1981)** *The Psychology of Literacy* — the Vai study that **empirically broke

  - **Reder, S., & Davila, E. (2005).** Context and literacy practices. *Annual Review of Applied

- **ANT / posthuman adjacents (the §5.3 "closest theories stop short of what the individual must do"):**

  - **Leander, K., & Boldt, G. (2020).** Critical literacy for a posthuman world: when people read, and

## §1/§6.4 The variance puzzle — platform earnings dispersion / divergent worker outcomes (the opener)

The empirical phenomenon §1 opens on (problematization, not gap-spotting): **equivalently positioned workers
get divergent outcomes through identical systems.** This cluster supplies the crispest possible evidence.

- **Bokányi, E., & Hannák, A. (2020).** Understanding inequalities in ride-hailing services through
  simulations. *Scientific Reports* (44 cites). **THE puzzle anchor: small parameter changes "often distribute
  vastly different incomes to identically performing drivers" — the exact "same input → divergent output
  through the same system" phenomenon the §1 variance puzzle names.** (arXiv preprint "Ride-share matching
  algorithms generate income inequality," 2019, is the same work — cite the *Sci. Rep.* version.) **NEW
  (highest value for §1).**
- **Schor, J. B., et al. (2020).** Dependence and precarity in the platform economy. *Theory and Society*
  (400 cites). **Finds heterogeneity of outcomes across AND within platforms — "worker satisfaction, autonomy,
  and earnings vary significantly" among similarly placed workers; the dominant precarity/control accounts
  can't explain the within-platform variance.** The management-grade framing of the puzzle. **NEW (high
  value).**
- **Sühr, T., et al. (2019).** Two-sided fairness for repeated matchings… a ride-hailing platform. *KDD.*
  (152 cites; documents "significant inequality in the driver income distribution" from the matching
  mechanism — empirical backing that the dispersion is *system-produced*, §1/§6.4.) **NEW.**
- **Wood, A. J., et al. (2019).** Good gig, bad gig: autonomy and algorithmic control in the global gig
  economy. *Work, Employment & Society* (1,508 cites). (the foundational divergent-job-quality statement —
  same algorithmic control yields flexibility *and* low pay/overwork; verify in base, likely already there.)
- **de Ruijter, A., et al. (2024).** Ridesourcing platforms thrive on socio-economic inequality. *Scientific
  Reports* (8 cites). (agent-based: driver earnings dispersion tied to market structure — recent reinforcement
  of the puzzle; §1, optional.) **NEW.**
- *Paper 3 forward-link (NYC/ride data, not §1 core):* Raman et al. 2021 (NYC taxi data, Shapley-value driver
  fairness) and Sun 2025 (Markov-chain income-volatility model) are **method precedents for Paper 3's TNC
  calibration**, not Paper 1 cites — note in `paper3_baseline`, don't put in the review bib.
- *(The exploitation/precarity SLRs — Alauddin 2024 Heliyon; Lata 2022; Muldoon 2022 algorithmic domination;
  Dedema 2024 already logged under §3 — are context, not the puzzle; cite Dedema once, skip the rest.)*
