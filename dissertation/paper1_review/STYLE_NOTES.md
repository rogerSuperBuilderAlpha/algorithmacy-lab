# Style notes — grounded in the bibliography's own exemplars

Built by reading five style anchors from Paper 1's bibliography, spanning the exact genres in play:
Okhuysen & Bechky (2009, *Annals* coordination review — closest genre match), Rahman (2021, *ASQ*), Cameron
(2024, *ASQ*), Stark & Vanden Broeck (2024, *Organization Theory*, OA), Suddaby (2010, *AMR* editorial). The
point is to de-slop the draft by matching how these scholars actually write — not by applying generic
"anti-AI" rules, several of which the exemplars contradict.

## The headline: the reviewers diagnosed the symptoms correctly, but some prescribed fixes are wrong

The draft reads robotic because of **monotony, verbatim repetition, and missing voice** — that much the
reviews got right. But three of the prescribed cures would move the prose *away* from the target exemplars:

| Reviewer rule | What the exemplars actually do | Corrected target |
|---|---|---|
| "Em-dashes: hard ban" | **Cameron and Stark use them frequently and load-bearingly** — to name/define a coined concept (Cameron) or stage a qualification-then-reveal (Stark). Rahman/Suddaby occasional; Okhuysen rare. | Don't ban. Use the em-dash the way Cameron does — to land a *named construct*. Cut only the lazy parenthetical-aside dashes. |
| "Eliminate rule-of-three / parallelism" | **All five use triplets heavily.** The difference: theirs either name analytic categories sustained across the paper (Okhuysen's 3 conditions; Suddaby's 4 elements) or — Stark's signature — set up a *disruptive fourth term* that is the actual argument. | Keep triplets that carry analytic weight or set up a turn. Cut only *decorative* triplets that exist for rhythm. |
| "Ban personifying the literature" | Mixed. Rahman uses "research has shown" throughout the theory voice; Suddaby personifies abstractions centrally. **Stark's better move: attribute claims to *named scholars*, and personify only your *own* focal concept.** | Not a ban. Replace generic "the literature has" with named attributions where possible; let *algorithmacy* be an agent. |

## What the exemplars unanimously show (and the draft lacks)

1. **Confident first person.** Every one writes "I"/"we": Rahman ("I studied," "my goal is not to settle the
   debate but to highlight"), Cameron ("I find, surprisingly"), Stark ("we contend," "we disagree"), Suddaby
   ("my intent," "I hope"), Okhuysen ("we go beyond"). **Our draft is impersonal third-person throughout —
   this is the single biggest source of the robotic feel, and the reviewers under-weighted it.** An
   integrative review is allowed a confident "we" frame for its analytic moves.
2. **Genuine rhythm variation.** Cameron and Stark deliberately puncture long, citation-dense sentences with
   very short ones (Stark: "We disagree." / "just click." / "Now!"). Our draft's cadence is uniform
   long-then-short. Real short punches are the fix.
3. **Triplets that turn.** Stark's model — "in hierarchies command, in markets contract, in networks
   collaborate, on platforms co-opted" — is *in our bibliography* and is exactly the move our
   hierarchy/market/network/co-optation material is built for. Our §3.5 currently renders it as a flat
   metronome; it should be the rhetorical high point.
4. **Gap-framing is fine; verbatim repetition is not.** Rahman gaps explicitly ("we lack…, to address this
   gap I…"); Cameron uses a real-world *puzzle* and pivots on one word ("Yet… surprisingly"); Stark
   counter-asserts ("the dominant view says X; we disagree because Y"). All legitimate. The slop is restating
   the *same* gap sentence ~15–20 times. Keep a few strong, varied moves; cut the repetition.
5. **Closers that land a concept, not empty restatement.** Rahman/Cameron/Suddaby do close paragraphs on a
   takeaway — but it re-lands the *coined concept* or gestures outward, it doesn't just paraphrase the
   paragraph. Cut our empty tag sentences ("The dimensions follow from the form."); keep concept-landing ones.
6. **Signpost once, briefly.** All roadmap (Suddaby "proceeds in four parts"; Cameron "I begin by showing").
   The fix is not to cut signposting but to cut the *duplicate* roadmap (we have two) and keep one compact,
   first-person itinerary.

## Per-author quick profiles (for matching)

- **Okhuysen & Bechky 2009 (the genre twin):** em-dashes rare; triplets = real taxonomies, not rhythm;
  additive contribution framing ("we go beyond / provide a framework," never "remains open"); "we" frames,
  abstractions act locally; measured, even cadence; one compact roadmap. *Closest model for our Method and
  synthesis sections.*
- **Rahman 2021:** occasional purposeful em-dash (append a consequence); triplets as recurring motif; explicit
  gap-then-bridge; impersonal-collective theory voice + concrete people-centered data voice; "I" for analytic
  moves; short "But"-pivots break the cadence; closers land the takeaway.
- **Cameron 2024:** **frequent em-dashes to define coined concepts**; heavy binaries + triplets, explicitly
  flagged; strong rhythm variation with curt declaratives; puzzle-driven gap ("the puzzle remains regarding
  why…"); concrete agents (workers/algorithms/"I") as subjects; vivid touches ("social plumbing").
- **Stark & Vanden Broeck 2024:** **frequent structural em-dashes** (qualification → reveal); the
  contrastive-triplet-into-fourth-term is the signature; staccato puncturing ("We disagree."); direct
  counter-assertion not hedged gaps; attributes to named scholars, personifies only the focal concept;
  first-person with wit and direct reader address ("Do you use Spotify?").
- **Suddaby 2010:** occasional em-dash + "that is" for precision; symmetric flagged enumerations; present,
  *modest* first person ("my goal here is more modest"); gap as plain editorial observation + a call ("the
  field seems unusually silent"); vivid anchors ("no construct is an island").

## The application plan (symptom → grounded fix)

1. **Add a first-person analytic frame** (we/our for the review's moves; sparing). Highest-leverage change.
2. **Em-dash pass — surgical, not a sweep.** Keep/convert dashes that name a construct (Cameron model); cut
   the parenthetical-aside dashes. Net: fewer, but the survivors do work.
3. **Rebuild the flat triplets into Stark turns or cut them.** Especially §3.5 (hierarchy/market/network →
   co-optation) and §1.1 (the judge/market-maker/ATS quartet): collapse the symmetric march, then break it on
   the algorithmic form.
4. **De-duplicate the recurring formula-phrases** (the verbatim "what an individual has to know, perceive, and
   do"; "productive… silent on the activity that distinguishes"; "did productive work within their own units";
   the lawyer/trader/applicant/driver quartet across abstract/§1.1/§8). Keep one strong instance each.
5. **Rewrite §7 into register** (it collapses into contractions + cutesy openers — the one section all
   reviewers and the exemplars agree is off).
6. **Vary rhythm** with genuine short declaratives after dense builds (Cameron/Stark model).
7. **Cut empty tag-sentence closers and the duplicate roadmap**; keep concept-landing closers and one roadmap.

## HARD RULE (the worst tic, caught in revision): no agentless-passive throat-clears

Never open a sentence with "The form has been mapped," "X has been documented/established/shown," or any
abstract noun + agentless passive. **Zero real scholars write this way** — and it directly violates the
exemplar finding: Stark attributes claims to *named people doing things* ("Birnbaum and Somers write that…"),
Rahman writes "I studied," Cameron's subjects are *workers and algorithms*. The fix is always the same: name
the agent and use the active voice. "Curchod and colleagues documented this…" not "the case has been
documented." Also prefer people over fields as the subject ("Sociologists have established," not "Sociology
has established"). Scan every revised paragraph for `has been / have been / been <verb>ed` and for abstract
nouns in the subject slot before moving on.

## The one decision that is the author's, not mine: "competency" vs "sensibility"

Reviewer 1 proposed reframing the core construct from **competency** to **sensibility** for more fluid prose.
This is a *conceptual* change, not a style edit, and I will not make it unilaterally:
- **For "sensibility":** more fluid; distances the construct from the static "literacy/competency" frames the
  paper tears down.
- **Against:** "competency" is load-bearing. The whole foreclosure positions algorithmacy *against CMC
  competence* on structural grounds; §6 specifies it to **construct-clarity** discipline (definition,
  dimensions, discriminant validity, nomological network) precisely because it is a measurable competency —
  and Papers 2–3 build a *measurement instrument* on that. "Sensibility" is hard to operationalize and would
  weaken the through-line to the measurement program.
- **Recommendation:** keep "competency" as the construct term, but stop *repeating the word mechanically* —
  vary the surface vocabulary (capacity, what the worker does, the competency the form requires) so it doesn't
  read as a tic. This gets the fluidity without surrendering the construct's identity. **Roger decides.**
