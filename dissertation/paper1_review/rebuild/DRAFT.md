# Algorithmacy: A Communication Competency for the Mediated Triad

Roger Hunt III · Bentley University

*Paper 1 of the dissertation. Integrative review; construct contribution. Draft. Bibliography follows the body; every in-text citation resolves to it.*

---

## Abstract

A driver and a rider coordinate a trip without ever choosing each other or meeting until it begins. Between them a platform commits a match, a fare, and a route, and that commitment is the only thing that passes between them. The same shape recurs across platform work: a worker reaches a counterpart only through a system that stands between them and commits determinations both must act through. Communication and organizational research has a deep vocabulary for a person working with a machine, and another for a person reaching another person through a medium. It has no construct for a worker coordinating with a human counterpart through a system that mediates both. This review argues that the gap is real and locates it precisely. The existing constructs — computer-mediated-communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, algorithm sensemaking, and algorithmic competency — all describe a *moderated dyad*: two parties whose coordination a medium shapes but does not constitute. The phenomenon at issue is a *mediated triad*: a third party that constitutes the coordination, commits binding determinations, and holds its own interest, so the relation no longer reduces to the pair. The competency the mediated triad demands is unnamed. The review names it *algorithmacy*, places it in the line that runs from oracy to literacy, specifies it to the discipline of construct clarity, and tells it apart from its nearest neighbors. One question it cannot answer from within its own argument: handed a particular network, how does an analyst decide whether its coordination is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad? That question opens the measurement program the dissertation's later papers take up.

*Keywords:* algorithmacy; mediated triad; moderated dyad; algorithmic management; platform work; communication competency; integrative review

## 1. Introduction

A driver in Chicago turns on an app and waits. A few miles away a rider opens the same app and asks for a car. Neither chooses the other. Neither learns the other's name until the trip begins, if then. Between them a platform commits a single determination — a match, a price, a route — and that determination is the only thing that passes between the two people the platform exists to connect. Ask the driver what she is doing and she will describe a relationship with an app. The other person is somewhere on the far side of it.

The shape repeats. An applicant sends a résumé into a hiring system that scores it against a profile and forwards a few survivors to a manager who never sees the rest. A creator posts to an audience reached only through a ranking the platform commits. A courier, a freelancer, a content moderator: each coordinates with a counterpart through a system that interprets both sides, commits a determination neither controls, and keeps the two from dealing with each other directly. This is now an ordinary form of working life. Hundreds of millions of people coordinate this way, and the number grows as algorithmic management spreads beyond the platforms that invented it.

A competency becomes visible when the structure of coordination changes. Oracy was the competency of a culture that coordinated by speech. Literacy became a competency of its own when writing, and then print, reorganized how people reached each other: a reader coordinates with a writer she will never meet, across distance and time, through a text that carries the writer's meaning. The competency was new because the structure was new, not because people had changed. Something analogous is underway now. Coordination increasingly runs through an algorithmic third party that stands between the two human parties to it, and the competency that form demands is as new, in the same way, as literacy once was. This review calls that competency *algorithmacy*.

The field already has a rich vocabulary for working with algorithms. It describes how a person engages a computational system: computer-mediated-communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, algorithm sensemaking, algorithmic competency. Each of these is built around a worker on one side of a medium that conveys, scores, or interprets. None of them, applied to the cases above, names what the worker is doing, because each is built around the wrong unit. They describe a person and a system, a dyad. The coordination that produces the cases is not a dyad. It is a worker reaching a counterpart she may never meet, through a system that mediates both of them and pursues ends of its own. The unit is a triad, and the competency a triad demands has no name in the field's vocabulary.

The distinction this review draws is between two forms a third element can take. In one, the third element *moderates*: it shapes the channel between two parties, but the coordination still reduces to the pair. A letter, a phone line, a shared document, an email client — each moderates a fundamentally dyadic exchange. Literacy and its descendants are the competencies of the moderated dyad. In the other form, the third element *mediates*: it constitutes the coordination, commits determinations the parties must act through, and holds an interest across both of them, so the relation does not reduce to the pair. The matching algorithm between a driver and a rider mediates. The coordination is a *mediated triad*. The competency it demands is algorithmacy.

This review makes three moves. It establishes that the mediated triad is a real and distinct coordination form, documented across the empirical study of platform work. It represents each existing construct in its strongest form and shows that each describes a moderated dyad and stops short of the triad. And it specifies algorithmacy to the discipline of construct clarity: a definition, a domain, dimensions, a discriminant position against its nearest neighbors, and the measurement program it opens.

One thing this review does not do, because it cannot from within its own argument. Handed a particular network, an analyst has to be able to say whether its coordination protocol is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, and the answer is not given by the surface. Counting the parties does not settle it: a setting with three parties is a moderated dyad if the third is a passive conduit. Reading the interface does not settle it: a worker who experiences nothing but an app may sit inside a mediated triad she cannot see. Deciding which a given form is requires a method this review does not supply. That decision is the question the dissertation's second paper builds a model to answer and its third paper puts to the test. Paper 1 names the form and the competency. It does not measure either.

## 2. Method of the review

An integrative review is primary inquiry. It uses prior research as data to generate new knowledge, and its product is a contribution, not a summary (Torraco, 2005; Cronin & George, 2023; Post et al., 2020). The contribution here is a construct. The review represents a body of work, finds where it stops, and crystallizes the synthesis into a named competency with a domain and a discriminant position. A review that only catalogues prior work, however thoroughly, fails the genre (Breslin & Bailey, 2020; Elsbach & van Knippenberg, 2020).

The review proceeds by problematization rather than gap-spotting (Alvesson & Sandberg, 2011). It does not search for an empty cell in a matrix and propose to fill it. It identifies an assumption the existing constructs share — that the unit of analysis is a worker and a system — and shows that the assumption fails for a coordination form that is now common. The construct is the output of dissolving that assumption.

The review's instrument is a test, and naming it fixes the standard the review holds itself to. Call it the charitable-extension test: represent each existing construct in its strongest published form, grant it every extension its authors could fairly claim, and only then show where it stops short of the mediated triad. A construct foreclosed in a weakened form proves nothing. If any construct, at full strength, already reaches coordination with a human counterpart through a constitutive algorithmic mediator, the contribution collapses. Fair representation of the foreclosed constructs is therefore the review's load-bearing decision, and the one on which a hostile reader should press hardest.

Systematicity here is an orientation, not a protocol (Simsek et al., 2023). The review does not run a keyword search and report counts. It anchors the corpus at the level of the construct, and traces each construct through its foundational statement, the work that has carried it, and the empirical applications that bear on whether it extends. Inclusion follows from bearing on the moderated-dyad-versus-mediated-triad question; saturation within each construct bounds it. The synthesis is laid out in a table that sets each construct against the structure it assumes and the point at which it stops (Table 1), which is also the review's disclosure of its scope and process.

## 3. Moderation and mediation

The argument rests on one distinction, so it is worth drawing carefully. A third element that stands between two parties can do one of two things to their coordination.

It can *moderate*. A moderating element shapes how the two parties reach each other without becoming a party itself. It carries, formats, delays, or filters what passes between them, but the coordination still belongs to the pair, and in the limit the element is transparent to it. A letter carries a writer's words to a reader. A telephone line carries a voice. A shared document holds a draft two collaborators pass back and forth. In each case the coordination reduces to the two parties: remove the third element and the relation it shaped is still, recognizably, between them. Bruno Latour's term for such an element is an *intermediary*, something that transports meaning without transforming it (Latour, 2005). A moderated dyad is a two-party coordination running through an intermediary.

Or it can *mediate*. A mediating element constitutes the coordination rather than carrying it. It commits determinations the parties must act through — a match, a price, a rank, a forward — and those determinations, not the parties' direct dealings, are what passes between them. It does this on terms of its own, in pursuit of ends that belong to neither party. And it holds the two apart, so that they cannot route around it. Remove a mediator and there is no residual two-party relation to recover, because the relation was never theirs to begin with; it was constituted by the third. Latour's term is a *mediator*, something that transforms what it carries (Latour, 2005). A mediated triad is a coordination that a third party constitutes and the two human parties reach each other only through.

The line that runs from oracy to literacy to algorithmacy is a line through these forms. Oracy is the competency of direct coordination, two people speaking, no third element between them. Literacy is the competency of the moderated dyad: a reader coordinates with a writer across distance and time, through a text that, at its best, carries the writer's meaning faithfully. The text moderates; the coordination is still between writer and reader. Algorithmacy is the competency of the mediated triad: a worker coordinates with a counterpart through a system that does not carry their dealings but constitutes them, committing the determinations both must act through. The competencies differ because the structures differ. A reader who learned to read texts has not thereby learned to coordinate through a mediator that scores her, ranks her counterpart, and keeps the two apart.

The mediated triad is not a thought experiment. It is the documented structure of platform work. Across ride-hailing and across remote and freelance platforms, a system reliably interposes between a worker and a counterpart she does not choose or meet, and commits binding determinations both sides must transact through (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016; Rahman, 2021; Wood et al., 2019; Cameron, 2024). On a ride-hailing platform the worker cannot choose her counterpart or set her own price; the system makes the match and the fare, and off-platform contact is grounds for deactivation, so the two are structurally kept from coordinating around it (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016; Cameron, 2024). On a freelance platform the algorithm assigns tasks, monitors behavior, evaluates performance, and determines access to clients, standing as a binding third party between worker and client (Rahman, 2021). The counterpart's input enters as a rating that the system folds into determinations the worker must live with, and the determination is opaque: Rahman's freelancers experienced the evaluation as control but "could not align their actions with its criteria because they could not clearly identify those criteria" (Rahman, 2021). Ratings and rankings operate as the effective managerial relation, more binding than direct supervision because they gate the allocation of work itself (Wood et al., 2019). The third party here is no intermediary. It transforms what passes between the parties, on its own terms, and holds them apart. It mediates.

This sets the review's question. The mediated triad makes a demand on its human parties that the moderated dyad does not. A worker in a moderated dyad can reason one party at a time, because the local picture is the whole picture: she addresses the person she is dealing with, through a channel she can treat as transparent. A worker in a mediated triad cannot. To act well she must reason forward, through a determination she does not control, to a counterpart she cannot see, and backward, from the determination, to what produced it. She must hold the whole three-party structure in view at once. That demand is what the existing constructs do not name, and the next section shows why.

## 4. The literacies: existing constructs as moderated-dyad competencies

The constructs the field offers for working with algorithms are not weak. Each is well specified and, in its domain, well validated. The claim is narrower and sharper: each describes a moderated dyad, and none, at full strength, reaches the mediated triad. Table 1 sets out the pattern; this section walks it.

**Table 1. The existing constructs as moderated-dyad competencies.**

| Construct | What the third element is | Unit of analysis | Where it stops short of the triad |
|---|---|---|---|
| CMC competence (Spitzberg, 2006) | a channel that moderates | worker and human cointeractant | the channel carries a dyad; no third party commits determinations |
| Human-machine communication (Guzman, 2020) | the counterpart itself | worker and machine | the machine is the partner, not a third standing between two humans |
| AI-mediated communication (Hancock et al., 2020) | an agent that edits messages | two humans and a message agent | excludes matching, ranking, and recommending mediators by its own boundary |
| Algorithmic and AI literacy (Long & Magerko, 2020; Dogruel et al., 2022) | a knowledge object | worker and the system she knows | knowledge of the system, not coordination through it |
| Algorithm sensemaking (Möhlmann et al., 2023) | the object of cognition | worker's mind and the system | interpreting the system; the counterpart is not a party |
| Algorithmic competency (Zhou et al., 2025) | the thing to be navigated | worker and algorithmic management | command of the system; the counterpart is only a downstream outcome |
| **Algorithmacy (this review)** | **a mediator that constitutes the coordination** | **worker and counterpart, reached through the mediator** | **— (this is the mediated triad)** |

**Computer-mediated-communication competence.** Spitzberg's model is the foundational statement of competent interaction through a digital channel (Spitzberg, 2006). It carries the interpersonal-competence tradition into mediated settings: a competent actor is motivated, knowledgeable, and skilled, and the skills — attentiveness, composure, coordination, expressiveness — are directed at a human cointeractant. The medium enters as a separate concern, a set of channel features the actor must manage. The unit is two people and the channel between them. There is no third party that commits determinations across both; the channel moderates a dyad. CMC competence is the competency of the moderated dyad in its clearest form.

**Human-machine communication.** Guzman's framework makes a genuine break: it treats the machine not as a medium but as a communicative partner, a source of messages, ontologically distinct from the human (Guzman, 2020). This is the strongest available account of a person communicating *with* a system rather than *through* one. But that is exactly why it does not reach the triad. The unit of analysis is the human-machine dyad. The machine is the counterpart, not a third party standing between the worker and a human counterpart. Human-machine communication has theorized one of the two relations the mediated triad contains — the worker and the system — and left the other, the worker and the counterpart reached through the system, outside the frame.

**AI-mediated communication.** Hancock and colleagues define AI-mediated communication as communication between people in which an intelligent agent operates on a communicator's behalf by modifying, augmenting, or generating messages (Hancock et al., 2020). This is the closest the field comes to a third party between two humans, and it is worth stating at full strength: the agent here does act between two people. But the agent's role is to shape *messages* — to smooth a reply, suggest a phrase, translate a sentence. The framework explicitly sets aside the algorithms that rank, recommend, match, and classify, on the grounds that they support communication only indirectly. That excluded category is precisely the mediated triad. A matching or pricing system does not edit a message between the parties; it commits a determination the parties must act through. AI-MC names a third party that moderates a message and excludes, by its own boundary, the third party that mediates a coordination.

**Algorithmic literacy and AI literacy.** The literacy constructs describe what a person knows and can critically assess about algorithmic and AI systems: how they work, what they do, how to use and question them (Long & Magerko, 2020; Dogruel et al., 2022). At full strength they cover a sophisticated, critical user. But they are knowledge-of-system constructs. They describe a person's grasp of the medium, not a person coordinating with another person through it. A worker can be fully literate about how a dispatch algorithm works and still face the unnamed problem: how to reach the rider on the other side of it. Literacy of the system is the moderated dyad's competency raised to critical self-awareness. It does not become triadic by becoming critical.

**Algorithm sensemaking.** Möhlmann, Salge, and Marabelli model how platform workers make sense of an opaque algorithmic management system: a process of focused enactment, modes of selection, and sources of retention, by which a worker builds and revises an interpretation of the system she works under (Möhlmann et al., 2023). This is the nearest neighbor to algorithmacy on one axis, because it takes the worker's side of the platform relation seriously and in depth. But its unit is the worker's mind, and its object is the system. Sensemaking is what a worker does to understand the algorithm. It is individual and cognitive, and the human counterpart does not enter it as a party. A worker can make excellent sense of the system and still have done nothing, in that account, toward the rider or the client she is trying to reach through it.

**Algorithmic competency.** Zhou and colleagues specify algorithmic competency as a worker's understanding of the platform algorithms that assign and evaluate her work and her ability to adapt to and navigate them, validated as a four-dimensional scale (Zhou et al., 2025). By its label it is the closest construct of all, and the closeness is the trap. Every one of its dimensions targets the worker's relation to the *algorithm*: understanding it, accepting it, leveraging it, working around it. The human counterpart appears, but only as a downstream outcome — customer-oriented behavior that the algorithm rewards — not as a party the competency reaches. Algorithmic competency is a worker-to-algorithm construct. It measures how well a worker handles the system. It stops where the mediated triad begins, at coordination with the counterpart on the far side.

**The shared limit.** Read together, the constructs share a commitment that the moderated-dyad-versus-mediated-triad distinction makes visible. Each locates the worker on one side of something — a channel, a partner, a system, a knowledge object — and theorizes her relation to that thing. None locates her on one side of a third party that stands between her and another human party, accepts inputs from both, commits determinations across both, and pursues ends of its own. CMC competence and its descendants carry the sender-channel-receiver frame, in which the third element is a channel. Human-machine communication makes the machine a partner. The literacies make it a knowledge object. Sensemaking makes it the object of cognition. Algorithmic competency makes it the thing to be navigated. In every case the third element is something the worker relates *to*. In the mediated triad it is the thing she must reach another person *through*. That is the move none of them makes, and it is the move algorithmacy names.

## 5. Algorithmacy

A construct earns its place only when it is drawn sharply enough to be measured and told apart from its neighbors (Suddaby, 2010). This section specifies algorithmacy to that standard: definition, domain, dimensions, discriminant position, and nomological net. It states the construct. It does not measure it; measurement is the program the review opens.

**Definition.** Algorithmacy is the communication competency a worker exercises to coordinate with a human counterpart reached only through a constitutive algorithmic mediator. It is an individual-level capacity, integrating knowledge, skill, and situated practice, and it develops through participation in the mediated triad rather than through instruction about the system.

**Domain.** Algorithmacy applies wherever the mediated triad holds: a worker and a counterpart coordinate through a third party that commits binding determinations both must act through, holds an interest across both, and keeps the two from coordinating directly. The domain is the structure, not the technology. A coordination form is in the domain because its third party mediates, whether that party is a dispatch algorithm, a hiring system, or a ranking engine. A form is out of the domain when its third party only moderates, even if it is computational and sophisticated. The domain excludes the moderated dyad by definition, which is what gives the construct an edge.

**Dimensions.** Three capacities follow from the structure of the mediated triad, and each is directed across the triad rather than at the system alone. The first is *inferential*: reading a committed determination backward to what produced it and forward to the counterpart's likely state, so the worker can act on a counterpart she cannot see. The second is *translational*: encoding her own intent into the terms the mediator will act on, so that what she means survives the determination and reaches the counterpart as she intends. The third is *temporal*: tracking how the mediator's rule shifts over time, since the determination is not fixed and a competency tuned to yesterday's rule misfires under today's. The dimensions are distinct from system-understanding because each terminates in the counterpart, not in the system. Reading a determination to understand the algorithm is sensemaking; reading it to reach the rider is inferential algorithmacy.

**Discriminant position.** Algorithmacy's nearest neighbors are algorithm sensemaking and algorithmic competency, and the axis that separates it from both is the same: the relational and coordinative target. Sensemaking terminates in the worker's interpretation of the system; algorithmacy terminates in coordination with the counterpart. Algorithmic competency terminates in the worker's command of the system; algorithmacy terminates in the counterpart reached through it. The distinction is not that algorithmacy ignores the system. A worker must read the system to coordinate through it, and to that extent the constructs overlap. The distinction is what the competency is *for*. Sensemaking and algorithmic competency are about the worker and the algorithm. Algorithmacy is about the worker and the counterpart, with the algorithm as the constitutive third the coordination runs through. This is the discriminant claim, and it is the one a measurement program must eventually confirm.

**Nomological net.** Algorithmacy should relate positively to its neighbors and remain distinct from each. It should correlate most strongly with algorithm sensemaking and with algorithmic competency, because all three engage the same system, and the discriminant test is whether a measure of algorithmacy carries variance those two do not — variance tied to coordination outcomes with the counterpart rather than to command of the system. It should relate more weakly to CMC competence, which engages a human counterpart but through a moderating channel rather than a mediating third. The construct predicts that workers high in algorithmacy coordinate better with counterparts they reach only through a mediator, holding constant their sensemaking and their command of the system. That prediction is the construct's empirical content, and testing it is later work.

## 6. The open question and the agenda

Algorithmacy is the competency the mediated triad demands. The construct does its work only if an analyst, handed a particular network, can say whether the network's coordination protocol is a mediated triad or a moderated dyad. Here the review reaches the edge of what it can do.

The form does not announce itself. Counting parties does not settle it. A setting with three parties is a moderated dyad if the third only carries what passes between the other two, and a setting that looks like a worker alone with an app may be a mediated triad whose third human party the interface hides. Reading the interface does not settle it either, because what a worker experiences and what constitutes her coordination can come apart, by the platform's design. Whether a coordination protocol requires moderated-dyad or mediated-triad interactions is a structural fact that has to be determined, and determining it is a problem the construct alone cannot solve.

That problem is the dissertation's next arc. Paper 2 builds a model that catalogs a coordination form as a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, by representing the form and computing whether its structure reduces to the parties or does not. Paper 3 runs experiments with that model, to see how the classification holds up across a range of forms and against behavior. The eventual measure the program reaches is a person-level instrument: a way to score a given worker's algorithmacy against a coordination form whose structure is known. That instrument is the supply-side sibling of the structural model, and it is the measure the construct ultimately needs.

This review supplies neither the model nor the instrument, and it is exact about why. A construct paper that smuggled in its own measurement would blur the contribution. The contribution here is conceptual: a coordination form the existing constructs cannot see, a competency that form demands and the field has not named, and a discriminant position that tells the new competency apart from its neighbors. Naming the competency is the first task, because a capacity that has no name cannot be measured, taught, or studied. The form is named here. The decision procedure and the measure are named as the work that follows.

## 7. Conclusion

The vocabulary the field has for working with algorithms describes a moderated dyad. CMC competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, algorithm sensemaking, and algorithmic competency each locate a worker on one side of a medium, a partner, a system, or a knowledge object, and theorize her relation to it. The coordination that now runs through algorithmic mediators is not that. It is a mediated triad: a worker and a human counterpart, coordinating only through a third party that constitutes the coordination, commits the determinations both must act through, and holds them apart. The competency the mediated triad demands is new in the way literacy was new against oracy, because the structure of coordination has changed. This review has named that competency algorithmacy, placed it in the line from oracy to literacy, foreclosed the constructs that cannot reach it, and specified it to the discipline of construct clarity. It leaves one question open by design: handed a network, an analyst must be able to decide whether its coordination is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, and that decision is the model the next paper builds and the experiments the paper after it runs. The form has a name. The way to measure it is the program this review opens.
