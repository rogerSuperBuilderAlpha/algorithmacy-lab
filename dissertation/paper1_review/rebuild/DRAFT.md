# Algorithmacy: A Communication Competency for the Mediated Triad

Roger Hunt III · Bentley University

*Paper 1 of the dissertation. Integrative review and construct contribution. Draft. Bibliography follows the body, and every in-text citation resolves to it.*

---

## Abstract

A driver and a rider coordinate a trip without ever choosing each other or meeting until it begins. Between them a platform commits a match, a fare, and a route. That commitment is the only thing that passes between them. The shape recurs across platform work. A worker reaches a counterpart only through a system that stands between them and commits determinations both must act through. Communication and organizational research has a deep vocabulary for a person working with a machine. It has another vocabulary for a person reaching another person through a medium. It has no construct for a worker coordinating with a human counterpart through a system that mediates both. The gap is real, and this review locates it. The existing constructs all describe a *moderated dyad*: two parties whose coordination a medium shapes but does not constitute. Computer-mediated-communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, algorithm sensemaking, and algorithmic competency each take that form. The phenomenon at issue is a *mediated triad*. A third party constitutes the coordination, commits binding determinations, and holds its own interest, so the relation no longer reduces to the pair. The competency the mediated triad demands has no name. This review names it *algorithmacy*, places it in the line that runs from oracy to literacy, specifies it to the discipline of construct clarity, and tells it apart from its nearest neighbors. One question the review cannot answer from within its own argument. Handed a particular network, how does an analyst decide whether its coordination is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad? That question opens the measurement program the dissertation's later papers take up.

*Keywords:* algorithmacy; mediated triad; moderated dyad; algorithmic management; platform work; communication competency; integrative review

## 1. Introduction

A driver in Chicago turns on an app and waits. A few miles away a rider opens the same app and asks for a car. Neither chooses the other. Neither learns the other's name until the trip begins, if then. Between them a platform commits a single determination — a match, a price, a route — and that determination is the only thing that passes between the two people the platform exists to connect. Ask the driver what she is doing and she will describe a relationship with an app. The other person is somewhere on the far side of it.

The shape repeats. An applicant sends a résumé into a hiring system that scores it against a profile and forwards a few survivors to a manager who never sees the rest. A creator posts to an audience reached only through a ranking the platform commits. A courier, a freelancer, a content moderator: each coordinates with a counterpart through a system that interprets both sides, commits a determination neither controls, and keeps the two from dealing with each other directly. This is now an ordinary form of working life. Hundreds of millions of people coordinate this way, and the number grows as algorithmic management spreads beyond the platforms that invented it.

The field already has a vocabulary for this, and it is a vocabulary of the channel. A user composes a message. A medium delivers it. A receiver opens it. The medium has properties that shape what passes through it, including latency, format, and persistence, and those properties matter. The medium does not pursue objectives of its own. A letter does not score the message before forwarding it. An email client does not adjust the wording for engagement. Computer-mediated-communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, the algorithmic and AI literacies, and algorithm sensemaking are all built on this structure. Each places a worker on one side of something that conveys, scores, or interprets, and theorizes her relation to that thing. None of them, turned on the cases above, names what the worker is doing, because each is built around the wrong unit. They describe a person and a system, a dyad. The coordination that produces the cases is not a dyad. It is a worker reaching a counterpart she may never meet, through a system that mediates both of them and pursues ends of its own.

A competency becomes visible when the structure of coordination changes. Oracy was the competency of a culture that coordinated by speech. Literacy became a competency of its own when writing, and then print, reorganized how people reached each other. A reader coordinates with a writer she will never meet, across distance and time, through a text that carries the writer's meaning. The competency was new because the structure was new, not because people had changed. The transition from oracy to literacy was not gap-filling, and not a refinement of oracy. It was the recognition that a new coordination form had emerged and required a competency the previous form did not name. Print did not make people better talkers. It made literacy a condition of taking part. Something of the same kind is underway now. Coordination increasingly runs through an algorithmic third party that stands between the two human parties to it, and the competency that form demands is as new, in the same way, as literacy once was. This review calls that competency *algorithmacy*.

The triadic form is older than the platforms. Two lawyers argue through a judge who commits a ruling both must act on, and they do not negotiate the verdict between themselves. Two traders reach each other through a market maker who sets the price at which they meet, on terms that serve the maker. A job applicant sends a résumé to an applicant-tracking system that scores it for a hiring manager who never sees the rejected pile. A rider hails a driver through a dispatch algorithm that matches them and fixes the fare. These are more or less directed systems, and they differ in many ways, but the structure is one structure. A third party stands between two principals, takes input from both, commits a determination across both, and keeps them from routing around it. What is new is not the form. What is new is that algorithmic management makes the form a general condition of work, the way print made literacy a general condition of taking part. A competency that used to belong to specialized settings, where a judge or a market maker held the middle, now belongs to ordinary working life.

The distinction this review draws is between two things a third element can do. In one, the third element *moderates*. It shapes the channel between two parties, but the coordination still reduces to the pair. A letter, a phone line, a shared document, an email client: each moderates a fundamentally dyadic exchange. Literacy and its descendants are the competencies of the moderated dyad. In the other, the third element *mediates*. It constitutes the coordination, commits determinations the parties must act through, holds an interest across both of them, and keeps the two apart, so the relation does not reduce to the pair. The matching algorithm between a driver and a rider mediates. The coordination is a *mediated triad*. The competency it demands is algorithmacy.

The structure already carries names in the literature, given by scholars working on different parts of it. Stark and Vanden Broeck (2024) call the triadic form co-optation, and set it beside the older modes in which markets contract, hierarchies command, and networks collaborate. Latour's vocabulary distinguishes a third party that transports what it carries without transforming it from one that transforms what it carries, and the algorithmic third party is a mediator in his sense, not an intermediary (Latour, 2005). Curchod and colleagues describe platform work as triangular relations among operators, producers, and end users (Curchod et al., 2020). An expanding share of work happens inside this structure. What working effectively inside it requires has not been named.

The misnaming is the point, and it is a misnaming of kind, not of detail. The literacy frameworks are not failing as literacy. They are accurate accounts of the moderated dyad, well specified and, in their domains, well validated. They are misnamed when they are taken to cover the triadic form, because the triadic form is at a different structural level. The transition from literacy to algorithmacy is the same kind of transition as the transition from oracy to literacy. Each marks a new coordination form that the prior competency did not name. Refining the existing constructs cannot reach the phenomenon, because the phenomenon is not a harder case of the moderated dyad. It is a different structure.

This review makes three moves. It establishes that the mediated triad is a real and distinct coordination form, documented across the empirical study of platform work. It represents each existing construct in its strongest form and shows that each describes a moderated dyad and stops short of the triad. And it specifies algorithmacy to the discipline of construct clarity: a definition, a domain, dimensions, a discriminant position against its nearest neighbors, and the measurement program it opens.

One thing this review does not do, because it cannot from within its own argument. Handed a particular network, an analyst has to be able to say whether its coordination protocol is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, and the answer is not given by the surface. Counting the parties does not settle it. A setting with three parties is a moderated dyad if the third is a passive conduit. Reading the interface does not settle it. A worker who experiences nothing but an app may sit inside a mediated triad she cannot see. Deciding which a given form is requires a method this review does not supply. That decision is the question the dissertation's second paper builds a model to answer and its third paper puts to the test. Paper 1 names the form and the competency. It does not measure either.

## 2. Method of the review

An integrative review is primary inquiry. It uses prior research as data to generate new knowledge, and its product is a contribution, not a summary (Torraco, 2005; Cronin & George, 2023; Post et al., 2020). The contribution here is a construct. The review represents a body of work, finds where it stops, and crystallizes the synthesis into a named competency with a domain and a discriminant position. A review that only catalogues prior work, however thoroughly, fails the genre (Breslin & Bailey, 2020; Elsbach & van Knippenberg, 2020).

The review proceeds by problematization rather than gap-spotting (Alvesson & Sandberg, 2011). It does not search for an empty cell in a matrix and propose to fill it. It identifies an assumption the existing constructs share, that the unit of analysis is a worker and a system, and shows that the assumption fails for a coordination form that is now common. The constructs are well built within their assumptions. The failure is in the structural assumption itself. Dissolving that assumption produces the construct.

The review's instrument is a test, and naming it fixes the standard the review holds itself to. Call it the charitable-extension test. Represent each existing construct in its strongest published form, grant it every extension its authors could fairly claim, and only then show where it stops short of the mediated triad. A construct foreclosed in a weakened form proves nothing. If any construct, at full strength, already reaches coordination with a human counterpart through a constitutive algorithmic mediator, the contribution collapses. Fair representation of the foreclosed constructs is therefore the review's load-bearing decision, and the one on which a hostile reader should press hardest.

One illustration fixes what the test demands. Computer-mediated-communication competence comes closest at the structural level, and its strongest defense is that the algorithmic third party fits inside the model's context facet, as one more feature of the context a competent actor reads. Grant that defense in full. The failure is then structural. The context facet treats the algorithm as part of the setting two parties read while they deal with each other. But the matching algorithm is not part of the setting. It picks which driver meets which rider, and it picks on aims that serve neither of them. A worker can read a setting. She cannot read a party that chooses her counterpart and sets her terms. Section 4.1 runs this foreclosure to its conclusion. The point here is only the standard: a construct gets its strongest reading before the structural failure is named.

The test demonstrates distinctness rather than asserting it, and the standard for construct work requires the demonstration. A warrant that two constructs differ is not the same as a showing that they differ (Larsen & Bong, 2016). Section 4 therefore runs the charitable-extension test on each construct in turn, and Section 5 states algorithmacy's discriminant position against its nearest neighbors as a claim a measurement program must confirm. The review does not rest on the assertion that the new construct is distinct. It shows, construct by construct, where each existing one stops.

Systematicity here is an orientation, not a protocol (Simsek et al., 2023). The review does not run a keyword search and report counts. It anchors the corpus at the level of the construct family and traces each family through its foundational statement, the work that has carried it, and the empirical applications that bear on whether it extends. The corpus closes by saturation. It is complete when the most recent reviews and scale-development papers in each construct family cite each other without adding new constructs. Two corpora result. A primary corpus runs across the construct families that describe working with algorithms, from CMC competence through algorithmic competency. A structural-anchors corpus carries the sources that supply the form itself: Simmel on the move from dyad to triad, Krackhardt on the ties such a structure creates, Peirce on the irreducibility of a triadic relation, Latour on intermediary versus mediator, and Stark and Pais and Stark and Vanden Broeck on the triangular geometry of platform coordination. The synthesis is laid out in a table that sets each construct against the structure it assumes and the point at which it stops short of the triad. That table is the review's disclosure of its scope and process.

The construct the review produces is held to the discipline of construct clarity (Suddaby, 2010; Podsakoff et al., 2016). A construct earns its place only when it is given a definition, scope conditions, semantic relations to its neighbors, and dimensions drawn sharply enough to be measured and told apart. Section 5 specifies algorithmacy to that standard. The review states the construct. It does not measure it. Measurement is the program the review opens, and the dissertation's later papers carry it.
## 3. Moderation and mediation

The argument rests on one distinction, so this section draws it carefully and then defends it. A third element that stands between two parties can do one of two things to their coordination. It can shape the channel between them and still leave the coordination theirs. Or it can constitute the coordination itself, on terms of its own, and hold the two apart. The first case is a moderated dyad. The second is a mediated triad. The line from oracy to literacy to algorithmacy is a line through these forms, and algorithmacy is the competency the second form demands.

### 3.1 The two things a third element can do

A third element can *moderate*. A moderating element shapes how two parties reach each other without becoming a party itself. It carries, formats, delays, or filters what passes between them, and the coordination still belongs to the pair. In the limit the element is transparent: remove it and the relation it shaped is still, recognizably, between the two parties. A letter carries a writer's words to a reader. A telephone line carries a voice. A shared document holds a draft two collaborators pass back and forth. Bruno Latour's term for such an element is an *intermediary*, something that transports meaning or force without transforming it: define its inputs and its outputs follow (Latour, 2005). A moderated dyad is a two-party coordination running through an intermediary.

A third element can also *mediate*. A mediating element constitutes the coordination rather than carrying it. It commits determinations the parties must act through, a match, a price, a rank, a forward, and those determinations are what passes between them. It does this on terms of its own, in pursuit of ends that belong to neither party. And it holds the two apart, so that they cannot route around it. Remove a mediator and there is no residual two-party relation to recover, because the relation was constituted by the third. Latour's term is a *mediator*, something that transforms, translates, distorts, and modifies what it is supposed to carry (Latour, 2005). A mediated triad is a coordination that a third party constitutes and the two human parties reach each other only through.

Algorithmacy does not name the whole class of Latourian mediators. The class is far wider than the construct needs. By Latour's account nearly every transformation in a network is a mediator, since almost nothing transports its inputs untouched. The construct picks out a proper subset: third parties that stand between human principals and pursue objectives of their own, objectives authored elsewhere but operating across both principals at once. A matching system between a driver and a rider is in the subset. A chemical reaction that transforms a reagent is a Latourian mediator and is not. The mediated triad is the subset where the mediator sits between two human parties and commits determinations that span them.

This proper-subset move is also the answer to the charge that Latour already did this. He did not. Latour gave a general property of network transformation. He did not give a competency, and he did not single out the third-party-between-principals case as a structural type with conditions of its own. Algorithmacy takes that subset and raises it to a competency-level concept. The actor-network tradition itself treats the intermediary–mediator line as fluid rather than fixed, and recent work in that tradition reads the same digital systems now as transporting, now as transforming (Wiard & Maréchal, 2022; McStay et al., 2024). The construct does not contradict that fluidity. It depends on it.

### 3.2 The distinction is empirical, and that is the point

The line between conveying and acting is not analytic. It is empirical, and it does not run cleanly through device categories. A video-call platform is mostly an intermediary: it carries two voices and pursues no end across them. An Uber matching algorithm is mostly a mediator: it commits the match and the fare and keeps the parties from setting either. A smart-reply system sits in between, sometimes carrying a message and sometimes shaping it. The same entity can switch modes from one use to the next, and a single system can moderate one exchange while it mediates another (Latour, 2005).

A fluid line does not make the distinction useless. It makes it a *type* abstraction, the kind organization theory already works with. Williamson's markets and hierarchies do not partition the world into two bins with no hard cases; they name two structural types, and deciding which type a given governance arrangement is doing the work of is itself the analytic task (Williamson, 1975). Moderated dyad and mediated triad work the same way. The question "is this coordination a mediated triad?" has no answer on the surface of the device, and answering it for a given form is exactly what the competency is for. A worker who can tell when she is reaching a counterpart through a constitutive third party, and when she is merely using a transparent channel, has the discriminating capacity the construct names. The fuzziness of the line is not an objection to the construct. It is the demand that produces it.

### 3.3 The historical arc: oracy, literacy, algorithmacy

The line from oracy to literacy to algorithmacy runs through these two forms and adds a third term. Oracy is the competency of direct coordination: two people speaking, no third element between them. Its conditions are co-presence, embodied memory, immediate reciprocity, and transparency, the conditions of speech in a culture that stores what it knows in living memory and exchanges it face to face (Ong, 1982; Havelock, 1963). Each party hears the other in the moment and answers in the moment, and nothing stands between the saying and the hearing.

Literacy is the competency of the moderated dyad. A reader coordinates with a writer she will never meet, across distance and time, through a text that at its best carries the writer's meaning faithfully. The conditions change with the structure: separation across time and space, externalized memory in place of embodied memory, asynchronous exchange in place of immediate reciprocity, and a technology the reader must be able to work (Street, 1984). The text moderates. The coordination is still between writer and reader, and remove the text and the relation it carried is still theirs.

Writing did not begin as a literary technology, and the order in which it began matters for the arc. The earliest writing was administrative. It grew out of the clay accounting tokens Mesopotamian administrators used to record goods, and the tokens hardened into cuneiform script over the millennia from roughly 8000 to 3100 BCE (Schmandt-Besserat, 1992). Writing arrived as a coordination technology, a way to hold an account across people and time, before it was ever a way to hold a poem. Goody traces how the same substrate then made codified law, organized religion, commerce, and bureaucratic administration possible, reorganizing how a society coordinates at scale (Goody, 1986). And the competency moved. It began as the closely held skill of a clerical priesthood and became, over centuries, a mass requirement for ordinary participation, the same path a competency travels when a coordination form spreads from specialists to everyone (Vee, 2017). Coding sits on that same path now, a skill once held by a few becoming a general one, and reading it through the coordinative frame keeps it clear of any claim that the technology rewires the mind.

Algorithmacy is the next term. It is the competency of the mediated triad: a worker coordinates with a counterpart through a system that does not carry their dealings but constitutes them, committing the determinations both must act through. Its conditions are the conditions of that structure. Signal asymmetry: the worker cannot see what the mediator is doing or why. Intent compression: the worker must squeeze what she means into the narrow terms the mediator will act on, and what does not fit is lost. Opaque mediation: the determination arrives without its reasons, and the rule that produced it is not on view. A reader who learned to read texts has not thereby learned to coordinate under these conditions, any more than a speaker who could hold a conversation had thereby learned to read. The competencies differ because the structures differ.

### 3.4 The seriality survives the practice variation

The arc invites a standard objection, and meeting it requires saying exactly what the historical claim is. The objection comes from New Literacy Studies. On Street's account literacy is not one thing with one cognitive payoff. It is a set of socially organized practices that differ across settings, and the "autonomous" model that treats writing as a single engine of cognitive change is an ideology, not a finding (Street, 1984). Scribner and Cole's study of the Vai found that specific literacy practices sharpened specific skills and produced no sweeping cognitive transformation (Scribner & Cole, 1981). Heath showed three nearby communities using written language in three different ways (Heath, 1983). Graff named the "literacy myth," the assumption that literacy alone lifts individuals and societies (Graff, 1979). Taken together this work makes any strong cognitive reading of the oracy-to-literacy shift untenable.

The arc does not need the strong cognitive reading. The claim that carries the seriality is coordinative, not cognitive. Writing made codified law, double-entry bookkeeping, and the bureaucratic file possible. These are historically locatable practices, contestable in their effects, and unevenly distributed across people and classes. The New Literacy Studies critique lands on all of that and changes none of the coordinative point, because the mechanism survives the practice variation. A written substrate lets coordination run at a scale and across a distance speech cannot reach. It builds durable institutions that hold determinations across time. It concentrates at first among specialists. It eventually democratizes. Each of those structural features holds whether or not literacy transformed anyone's cognition. The argument therefore leans on the moderated positions, Olson on the consequences of written representation and Goody on writing and social organization, and not on the strong claims associated with Ong or McLuhan (Olson, 1994; Goody, 1986).

Print sharpens the point rather than softening it. The shift from script to print was in kind but slow. Eisenstein traced how print brought standardization and a cumulative, correctable record that manuscript culture could not sustain (Eisenstein, 1979). Johns answered that print's reliability was not automatic. It was constructed, policed, contested, and won over a long time against forgery and piracy (Johns, 1998). Both accounts describe a real transition that was neither sudden nor universal. That is what makes the print case useful. An in-kind transition need not be abrupt, automatic, or evenly spread to count as a transition in kind. The move from literacy to algorithmacy can be gradual and contested and still be a move to a new coordination form, on the same terms that the move into print was.

### 3.5 Platform work is a mediated triad

The mediated triad is not a thought experiment. It is the documented structure of platform work, and this subsection reads that record for structure alone. The record shows what the form is. It is not used here to make any claim about what the form produces.

Three features recur across the empirical work. The worker does not choose or meet her counterpart. The system commits binding determinations both sides must act through. And the parties are kept apart, with opacity built into the arrangement rather than incidental to it. On a ride-hailing platform the worker cannot pick her rider or set her fare. The system makes the match and the price, and off-platform contact is grounds for deactivation, so the two are structurally prevented from coordinating around the system (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016; Cameron, 2024). On a remote freelance platform the algorithm assigns tasks, monitors behavior, evaluates performance, and gates access to clients, standing as a binding third party between worker and client (Rahman, 2021; Wood et al., 2019). The counterpart's input enters as a rating the system folds into determinations the worker must live with, and the criteria stay hidden, so the worker can feel the evaluation as control without being able to align her actions to it (Rahman, 2021). Three independent literatures describe the same geometry: triangular relations among platform, producer, and end user (Curchod et al., 2020); a triangular geometry with twisted feedback loops (Stark & Pais, 2020); algorithms that match and control in the same operation (Möhlmann et al., 2021). These accounts describe the structure this review calls a mediated triad, without giving it that name.

The third party here is not a human third party with a digital coat. Four features set the algorithmic mediator apart from a human one (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). Its performance is black-boxed: the determination arrives without its workings. It rests on comprehensive digitization: it captures the interaction in fine and total detail. It is anticipatory and quantifying: it scores and predicts before the fact. And its politics are hidden: the interests built into it do not appear on its face. Two more features compound these. Scale: one algorithm coordinates millions of dyads at once, a span no human third party occupies. And the absence of a professional accountability community: a judge is bound by judicial conduct codes and a market maker by exchange rules, but the algorithmic mediator answers to no analogous outside body that could check it (Pasquale, 2015). A human third party between two parties is embedded in norms that constrain how it may act. The algorithmic third party, at scale and unaccountable, is not.

The applicant-tracking system is the case that shows the form outside gig work. A candidate sends a résumé into a hiring system. The system scores it, ranks it against other applications, and forwards a short list to a manager who never sees the rest. The candidate sees only the two ends: the résumé going in and the rejection or invitation coming back. The scoring, ranking, and filtering between those two points are invisible to her, and the manager sees a ranked list rather than the process that produced it. The opacity is a design feature, not a flaw. Transparency about the scoring criteria would let applicants game them, so the system's value as a coordination mechanism depends partly on the parties not knowing exactly how it works. The candidate must coordinate with the manager on the far side of a third party she cannot inspect and is not meant to inspect. That coordination, conducted through a deliberately opaque mediator, is exactly the demand the competency answers.

### 3.6 The mediator's objectives are authored, not spontaneous

The word "objectives" needs a guard, or it reads as anthropomorphism. The algorithmic mediator does not pursue ends in the way a person does, and the construct does not claim it does. Its objectives are authored. Platform owners design the reward functions, select the training data, and set the metrics the system optimizes. The system is not a spontaneous agent that woke up with interests. It is an authored artifact whose objectives were written into it by people. What makes it more than a mere tool is that its operational autonomy outruns its authors' ability to predict or control its behavior once it is deployed. The owner sets the objective and then cannot fully say what the running system will do with it.

This reading pairs with how the worker actually engages the system. She does not read the algorithm's mind, because there is no mind to read. She tracks its enacted behavior, the regularities in what it commits, and works from those. The competency is realist about practice in this sense: it operates on what the mediator does, not on what it intends. Saying the mediator holds an interest across both parties names a structural fact about the authored objective, not a claim that the machine wants anything.

### 3.7 The conceptual backbone: Simmel and Peirce

Two older arguments give the mediated triad its conceptual spine, and both stay conceptual here. No formal model belongs in this paper.

Simmel established that a triad is qualitatively different from a dyad, not a dyad with one more member. Adding a third party changes the form of the relation and opens roles that two parties alone cannot occupy: the non-partisan mediator who reconciles the other two, the *tertius gaudens* who profits from their division, and the divider who rules by keeping them split (Simmel, 1950). Later work added a fourth role, the *tertius iungens*, the third who joins the other two together (Obstfeld, 2005). A human third party can occupy one of these roles at a time, and the others in the relation can usually see which. The algorithmic mediator occupies all of them at once and opaquely. It reconciles, profits, divides, and joins in a single committed determination, and it does so behind a screen. No human third party can sustain this, because in any relationship small enough for the parties to observe directly, the contradictions among the roles become visible and force a choice. The algorithm escapes that constraint by scale and opacity, and the parties cannot see which role it is playing. That structural partiality, a third party acting on interests across both parties while neither can read its hand, is where signal asymmetry comes from. The condition is not added to the structure. It falls out of it.

Peirce gives the strongest conceptual form of the irreducibility. His reduction thesis holds that a genuine triadic relation cannot be resolved into actions between pairs, that a true three-place relation is not analyzable into any combination of two-place relations (Peirce, 1931–1958). Read onto the coordination forms, this says the mediated triad cannot be modeled as a moderated dyad with extra steps. A coordination that a third party constitutes is not the same object as a two-party coordination with a passive conduit attached, and no stacking of dyadic relations recovers it. This is the conceptual ground for treating the mediated triad as its own form rather than a complicated dyad. The formal development of the claim is later work. The conceptual claim stands on Peirce.

### 3.8 Not principal-agent, brokerage, or triangulation

Three established constructs look close enough to absorb the mediated triad, and none does. Principal-agent theory assumes a formal delegation, a principal who hands defined authority to an agent under a contract. Platform coordination lacks that formal delegation. The worker never delegates authority to the matching system, and the system is not her agent in any contractual sense (Stark & Pais, 2020). Brokerage names a human third party who bridges a structural hole and trades on the gap, but the broker is a person embedded in human accountability, answerable to the parties and to norms that govern how a broker may behave (Burt, 1992). The algorithmic mediator carries none of that embedding. Triangulation, in the family-systems sense, names a third party pulled unintentionally into a two-party tension (Bowen, 1978). The platform third party is the opposite of unintentional. It is engineered, scaled, opaque, and outside any professional accountability community (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). Each near neighbor captures something real and misses the feature that defines the form: an engineered, scaled, unaccountable third party that constitutes the coordination and keeps the parties apart.

### 3.9 The demand the moderated dyad does not make

The forms make different demands on the people inside them. A worker in a moderated dyad can reason one party at a time, because the local picture is the whole picture. She addresses the person she is dealing with, through a channel she can treat as transparent, and nothing she cannot see is committing anything across the two of them. A worker in a mediated triad cannot reason this way. To act well she must reason forward, through a determination she does not control, to a counterpart she cannot see, and backward, from the determination, to whatever produced it. She must hold the whole three-party structure in view at once, under signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation. That is the demand the mediated triad makes and the moderated dyad does not. It is also the demand the existing constructs fail to name, because each of them is built for a worker on one side of a channel, a partner, or a system, and not for a worker reaching another person through a third party that constitutes the reach. The next section shows, construct by construct, where each one stops.
## 4. The literacies: existing constructs as moderated-dyad competencies

The constructs the field offers for working with algorithms are not weak. Each is well specified, and in its own domain each is well validated. The claim against them is narrow and exact. Each describes a moderated dyad, and none, taken at full strength, reaches the mediated triad. The review owes each construct its strongest form before it shows where the construct stops, because a foreclosure run against a weakened construct proves nothing. If any one of these, granted every fair extension, already reached coordination with a human counterpart through a constitutive algorithmic mediator, the contribution would collapse. So the section represents each construct as its authors would, and only then marks the point where it stops short of the triad. Table 1 lays out the pattern. The subsections walk it.

### 4.1 Computer-mediated-communication competence

Spitzberg's model is the foundational statement of competent interaction through a digital channel. It carries the interpersonal-competence tradition into mediated settings and integrates five elements into a single individual capacity: motivation, knowledge, skill, context, and outcomes (Spitzberg, 2006). A competent actor wants to interact, knows how interaction works, and can execute the skills that carry it, and the model treats these as parts of one capacity rather than a list of traits. The skills point at a human cointeractant. The context enters as a set of features the actor reads and manages. The medium is a separate concern, a set of channel properties the actor must handle while reaching the person on the other side. The architecture has proven durable. Koponen, Turunen, Laajalahti, Julkunen, and Spitzberg (2025) carry it into managerial technology-mediated communication, extending the same five-element structure to leaders coordinating teams through digital tools.

Grant the construct its strongest defense. A defender places the algorithmic third party inside the context facet, treats the counterpart and the system together as an evolving feature of the relational and functional context, and claims that the architecture has already absorbed the mediated triad. The defense is the right one to mount, because the context facet is genuinely the most capacious part of the model, and absorbing the third party there would, if it worked, leave nothing for a new construct to do.

The absorption fails, and the reason is structural. The context facet conditions an exchange the two parties still own. The matching algorithm does not condition the exchange between a driver and a rider. It makes that exchange. It decides which driver and which rider meet at all, and what it decides answers to aims that span both and belong to neither. Conditioning an exchange and making one are different things, and the context facet only conditions. It has no room to hold a party that makes the exchange. The model's integrative architecture survives the move from channel to mediator. Its channel premise does not. Algorithmacy keeps the architecture and drops the premise. The extension changes what the construct measures, not what it integrates.

### 4.2 Human-machine communication

Human-machine communication makes a genuine break with the channel tradition. It treats the machine as a communicator with standing, a source of messages, ontologically distinct from the human who addresses it (Guzman, 2020). The machine is no longer glass between human speakers. It is a speaker. Guzman and Lewis (2020) set out the research agenda this repositioning opens: a program for studying how people communicate with machines that talk back. This is the strongest account the field has of a person communicating *with* a system rather than *through* one, and the strength is exactly what fixes its limit.

The dyad survives the repositioning. The human communicates with the machine, and that exchange is the whole of the interaction. The machine is the partner, the endpoint, the other side of the relation. It is not a third party standing between the worker and a human counterpart. An Uber driver does not converse with the matching algorithm. The algorithm is not someone the driver talks to. It is the thing through which the driver reaches a rider she never addresses directly. Human-machine communication studies how humans communicate with machines. It does not study how humans coordinate through machines with other humans. It has theorized one of the two relations the mediated triad contains, the worker and the system, and it has the machine as the endpoint of that relation. The second relation, the worker and the counterpart reached through the system, stays outside its frame.

### 4.3 AI-mediated communication

AI-mediated communication gets the structure right. Hancock, Naaman, and Levy (2020) define it as interpersonal communication in which an intelligent agent operates on a communicator's behalf by modifying, augmenting, or generating messages. The agent here does act between two people. A smart reply drafts a response a person sends to another person. A translation system rewrites a message so a reader on the far side can take it in. This is the closest the field comes to a third party between two humans, and it deserves to be stated at full strength: the agent intervenes, the two endpoints are human, and the relation is genuinely three-place.

The level is wrong. AI-MC operates at the message level. The agent smooths a reply, suggests a phrase, edits a sentence the communicator was going to send anyway. Platform coordination operates at the system level. A matching algorithm does not edit a message between a driver and a rider. It constitutes which driver and which rider encounter each other at all. The difference between augmenting a message and constituting a coordination environment is the difference between a copy editor and an architect. A copy editor works on the text two parties exchange. An architect determines which parties are in the room. AI-MC also draws its own boundary in a way that confirms the point. It sets aside the algorithms that rank, recommend, match, and classify, on the grounds that they support communication only indirectly. That excluded category is the mediated triad. AI-MC names the third party that moderates a message and excludes, by its own stated boundary, the third party that mediates a coordination.

### 4.4 Algorithmic literacy

The literacy constructs describe what a person knows and can critically assess about algorithmic systems. Dogruel, Masur, and Joeckel (2022) develop and validate an algorithm literacy scale for internet users, built on two dimensions: awareness that algorithms are at work and knowledge about how they operate. At full strength the construct describes a sophisticated, critical user who knows that a feed is sorted, knows roughly how the sorting works, and can question it. The authors are candid about the construct's reach. The scale captures two of the four components their own framework names. Critical evaluation and coping-or-design skills are not in the instrument. The construct, by its developers' account, measures awareness and knowledge, and leaves the more active components outside the measure.

The unit is the user in relation to the system. The construct describes a person's grasp of the medium she is using. It does not describe a person coordinating with another person through that medium. A worker can be fully literate about how a dispatch algorithm works and still face the unnamed problem, which is how to reach the rider on the other side of it. The other human party, whose communication runs through the same system, is absent from the frame. Knowledge of the system is not coordination through it. Literacy of the system is the moderated dyad's competency raised to critical self-awareness, and it does not become triadic by becoming critical.

### 4.5 AI literacy and awareness

The AI-literacy lineage runs parallel and lands in the same place. Long and Magerko (2020) specify AI literacy as a set of sixteen competencies organized around five questions: what AI is, what it can do, how it works, how it should be used, and how people perceive it. Ng, Leung, Chu, and Qiao (2021) organize the territory through a four-aspect framework on a Bloom taxonomy. Carolus and colleagues (2023) build the Meta AI Literacy Scale, and Koch and colleagues (2024) supply a ten-item short form. Alongside these, awareness instruments measure a narrower thing. Zarouali, Boerman, and de Vreese (2021) develop a four-dimensional scale for awareness of algorithmic content in media. The digital-divide tradition frames the same competencies as a question of unequal distribution. Cotter and Reisdorf (2020) and Gran, Booth, and Bucher (2021) ask who has algorithmic skill and who does not, and treat the gap as a stratification problem.

The unit across both lineages is the user in relation to the system. The frame holds a person and the system she knows about, uses, or fails to use. The other human party whose communication runs through the same system is not in the frame at all. Two findings sharpen the limit from inside the literature. Lomborg and Kapsch (2020) show that knowledge alone does not produce more critical engagement, so the link from knowing the system to acting on it is not automatic. DeVito (2021) shows that a folk theory can be pragmatically effective while being mechanistically wrong, so a user can act well on a model of the system that is false. The knowledge-action gap is documented, and it cuts the same way the foreclosure does. Knowing how the system works is one thing. Coordinating with a counterpart through it is another, and the second is the thing the literacies do not measure.

### 4.6 Algorithm sensemaking

Algorithm sensemaking takes the worker's side of the platform relation seriously and in depth. Möhlmann, Alves de Lima Salge, and Marabelli (2023) model how platform workers make sense of an opaque algorithmic management system. The model is Weickian. It moves through focused enactment, modes of selection, and sources of retention, the process by which a worker tries something, reads what comes back, keeps what holds, and arrives at a working model of the system she labors under. This is the nearest neighbor to algorithmacy on one axis, because it treats the worker as actively building an account of the algorithm rather than passively receiving its outputs.

The unit is the worker's mind. The object is the system. Sensemaking is the cognitive process by which a worker comes to a model of the algorithm. It is individual and interpretive, and the human counterpart does not enter it as a party. A worker can make excellent sense of the system and, on this account, have done nothing toward the rider or the client she is trying to reach through it. Reading a determination to understand the algorithm is sensemaking. Reading a determination in order to reach the counterpart on the far side is a different act with a different terminus. Sensemaking describes how a worker arrives at a model of the algorithm. It does not describe how she coordinates with another human party through the algorithm, in the algorithm's terms.

### 4.7 Algorithmic competency

Zhou, Lei, Liu, Huang, and Hou (2025) supply the nearest construct by label, and the closeness is the trap. They develop and validate algorithmic competency as a twelve-item scale with four dimensions: understanding, embracing, leveraging, and remediating algorithmic management. The construct is carefully built and properly validated, and its name sits one word away from the one this review proposes. That proximity is exactly why it has to be read closely rather than waved past.

Every dimension targets the worker's relation to the algorithm. Understanding the system, accepting it, putting it to use, and working around its failures are four ways of relating to the same object, and the object is the system. The human counterpart does enter the construct, but only as a downstream outcome. Customer-oriented behavior, the kind the algorithm rewards, appears at the end of the chain as something the competency predicts, not as a party the competency reaches. So algorithmic competency is a worker-to-algorithm construct by its theoretical object. It measures how well a worker handles the system. Algorithmacy is worker-system-counterpart by its theoretical object. It measures how well a worker reaches a counterpart through the system. The constructs differ by what the form introduces, the third human party on the far side, not by what the worker does cognitively in front of the screen.

This is the discriminant target the new construct must clear, and it is the algorithmic-competency scale, not sensemaking, that sets the bar. The empirical pattern to expect follows from the structural difference. A measure of algorithmacy should correlate with this scale at a low-to-moderate level, because both engage the same system, and should carry incremental prediction beyond it, because algorithmacy reaches a target the scale does not. Zhou and colleagues report a correlation of .17 between algorithmic competency and perceived algorithmic management, which fixes the order of magnitude a discriminant test should anticipate: a real but modest association, leaving room for incremental validity.

### 4.8 The shared limit

Read together, the constructs share a commitment that the moderated-dyad-versus-mediated-triad distinction makes visible. Each locates the worker on one side of something and theorizes her relation to that thing. CMC competence puts a channel between sender and receiver. Human-machine communication makes the machine the partner. AI-MC puts an agent on a message. The literacies make the system a knowledge object. Sensemaking makes it the object of cognition. Algorithmic competency makes it the thing to be navigated. In every case the third element is something the worker relates *to*. In the mediated triad the third element is the thing she must reach another person *through*. That is the move none of the constructs makes, and it is the move algorithmacy names.

The foreclosure is not even across the constructs, and stating it honestly matters more than stating it strongly. CMC competence, human-machine communication, and AI-mediated communication are dyadic by construction. The channel carries a dyad, the machine is one partner of a dyad, the agent edits a message inside a dyad. The literacies are dyadic by neglect. Nothing in a literacy construct forbids a second human party, but the frame is built around the user and the system, and the counterpart simply never appears in it. Algorithm sensemaking and algorithmic competency are the subtle cases. In their empirical settings both engage platforms that are plainly triadic, since the workers studied are coordinating with riders and clients through the system. The constructs themselves, however, are theorized as worker-system relations. The triad is in the field site and not in the construct.

So the strongest discriminant claim is structural and theoretical, not phenomenological. The argument does not need to say that these authors missed something in front of them. Möhlmann and colleagues did not fail to notice the rider. Zhou and colleagues did not overlook the customer. The constructs answer a different question. Sensemaking answers how a worker comes to understand an opaque system. Algorithmic competency answers how well a worker commands the system that manages her. Algorithmacy answers how a worker coordinates with a counterpart she reaches only through that system. Three different questions, and the third one has had no construct.

One boundary remains to mark, because it limits what algorithmacy claims and protects the construct from overreach. A Weberian reading treats the algorithm as a form of legal-rational authority that encodes rule-following without the appellate structure that rule-following requires for legitimacy (Weber, 1978). The worker faces obligations to follow rules she had no part in setting and cannot revise, and she has no standing to contest a determination once it is committed. Algorithmacy equips her to comply with the rules as they stand and to track them as they shift. It does not equip her to challenge the rules or to claim standing within them. The competency operates on the compliance side of the compliance-legitimacy gap. Whether the gap should exist is a question of justice that the construct does not address and does not pretend to. Algorithmacy is a competency for working inside the mediated triad, not a remedy for its terms.
## 5. Algorithmacy

A construct earns its place only when it is drawn sharply enough to be measured and told apart from its neighbors (Suddaby, 2010). This section specifies algorithmacy to that standard. It gives a definition, the conditions under which the construct applies, the dimensions that compose it, the position that separates it from its nearest neighbors, and the web of relations that locates it among antecedents and outcomes. It states the construct. It does not measure it. Measurement is the program the review opens, and the section names the strategy without running it.

### 5.1 Definition

Algorithmacy is the integrated communication competency through which a worker coordinates with another human party through an algorithmic third party that pursues objectives of its own. The third party's objectives are authored. Platform owners design the reward functions, choose the training data, and fix the metrics the system optimizes, so the objectives belong to the system in operation while originating with its authors. The construct does not require the algorithm to be an agent. It requires only that the algorithm's operational behavior exceed its authors' capacity to predict or control it once deployed, which is the ordinary condition of a deployed optimization system. This is the mediated triad: a worker, a counterpart, and a constitutive algorithmic mediator between them.

Algorithmacy is an individual-level capacity. It integrates knowledge, skill, and situated practice into a single competency, in the way the interpersonal-competence tradition treats motivation, knowledge, and skill as facets of one capacity rather than as separate variables. It develops through participation in the mediated triad. A worker acquires it by coordinating inside the form, not by being instructed about the system. The capacity is socially and unequally distributed, which follows from its developmental source: workers with more exposure to the form, and more varied exposure, develop more of it (Gran, Booth, & Bucher, 2021; Hargittai et al., 2020).

### 5.2 Scope conditions

Algorithmacy applies wherever three conditions hold together. The conditions are signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation. They are jointly sufficient and individually necessary. Where all three hold, the worker faces the mediated triad, and the competency the construct names is the competency that form demands. Where any one fails, the form falls outside the construct's domain.

Signal asymmetry is the condition that the worker reaches the counterpart only through a determination the mediator commits, and cannot observe the counterpart or the process directly. The worker sees inputs and outputs and not the steps between them. A job applicant submits a résumé and receives a rejection or an invitation. The scoring, ranking, and filtering between submission and outcome stay invisible, and the hiring manager on the far side sees a ranked list rather than the process that produced it (Curchod et al., 2020).

Intent compression is the condition that the worker must encode situated intent into the narrow vocabulary the mediator will act on, and the encoding discards part of what the worker meant. What the worker intends does not pass to the counterpart whole. It passes as whatever survives the compression into the system's terms.

Opaque mediation is the condition that the rules the mediator applies are unpublished, unstable, and adaptive, and the worker cannot read them off the interface. Transparency about the rules would let workers game them, so the mediator's effectiveness as a coordination mechanism depends partly on the rules staying hidden. The opacity is a design feature of the form, not a defect in it (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018).

The domain is the structure, not the technology. A coordination form is in the domain because its third party mediates, whether that party is a dispatch algorithm, a hiring system, or a ranking engine. A form is out of the domain when its third party only moderates, however computational and sophisticated it is. Where the third party is transparent and accountable through prior coordination mechanisms, such as a published procedure or an enforceable contract, the worker faces a different form and a different competency governs it. The exclusion of the moderated dyad is what gives the construct an edge.

### 5.3 Dimensions

Three capacities compose algorithmacy. Each follows from one scope condition, and each aims at the counterpart, not the system. The dimensions are formative, not reflective (Edwards, 2001; Jarvis, MacKenzie, & Podsakoff, 2003; Law, Wong, & Mobley, 1998; MacKenzie, Podsakoff, & Jarvis, 2005). They are not interchangeable indicators of one underlying trait that causes them all. They are distinct capacities that together constitute the competency, and a worker can be strong in one and weak in another. The competency is the composite they form.

The first dimension is inferential. It follows from signal asymmetry. The inferential worker reads a committed determination backward to what produced it and forward to the counterpart's likely state, and acts on a counterpart she cannot see. The capacity runs on working models of the system that workers build and share, the folk theories and the gossip about how the algorithm behaves (Bishop, 2019; Bucher, 2017; Cotter, 2019; DeVito, 2017, 2021; Eslami et al., 2015, 2016; Karizat et al., 2021). These working models need not be mechanically correct to work. A folk theory can be wrong about the mechanism and still let a worker act well (DeVito, 2021). The inferential dimension is bounded against two neighbors. It is not literacy, which is declarative knowledge of how the system works. It is not sensemaking, which is the cognitive process of arriving at a model. It is the use of a model to read across the triad to the counterpart.

The second dimension is translational. It follows from intent compression. The translational worker encodes situated intent into the system's vocabulary and stays aware of what the encoding discards, so that what she means survives the determination and reaches the counterpart close to intact. The problem is the pragmatic-boundary problem: meaning has to cross a boundary into a representation that cannot hold all of it, and crossing it well takes work (Carlile, 2002, 2004). The work has a name in the study of cooperative systems. It is articulation work, the effort of making situated action fit a shared representation (Schmidt & Bannon, 1992; Star & Strauss, 1999), and the representation the parties coordinate around is a boundary object that means somewhat different things on each side of it (Star & Griesemer, 1989). The residue of compression shows up at the message level, where systems that operate on a communicator's words leave measurable traces in what gets sent (Jakesch, Hancock, & Naaman, 2023; Mieczkowski et al., 2021). The dimension rests on an old point about rules. Following a rule requires judgment the rule does not contain, so encoding intent into a system's categories always leaves something to the worker that the categories cannot specify (Wittgenstein, 1953/2009; Bowker & Star, 1999; Suchman, 2007).

The third dimension is temporal-tracking. It follows from opaque mediation. The temporal-tracking worker detects when the unpublished, unstable, adaptive rules have shifted and updates her practice, because a competency tuned to last month's rule misfires under this month's. The properties that make this hard are the properties that make the mediator opaque in the first place: its performance is black-boxed, its operation digitized and comprehensive, its measurement anticipatory, its politics hidden (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). The rules do not change in announced steps. They drift, in a continuous environment where matching and control run together and adjust without notice (Möhlmann, Zalmanson, Henfridsson, & Gregory, 2021). Workers exposed to the same policy changes adapt to them differently, which is what makes tracking a competency rather than a fact about the platform (Cameron, 2024). When the sanctioned path stops working, tracking turns into shadow learning, the development of practice off the approved route because the approved route has failed (Beane, 2019).

The dimensions describe practice, not an agent. Tracking is something the worker does as her practice meets a shifting system, and it is framed around enactment rather than around the algorithm as a party with intentions (Seaver, 2017; Rieder, 2020; Lebovitz et al., 2021). This keeps the construct clear of anthropomorphism. The system has authored objectives and operational autonomy, and the worker tracks its behavior. Neither claim makes the system a mind.

What separates all three dimensions from system-understanding is the same feature. Each one aims at the counterpart. Reading a determination to understand the algorithm is sensemaking. Reading the same determination to reach the rider is inferential algorithmacy. The act can look identical and the competency is different, because the competency is defined by what it is for.

### 5.4 Discriminant position

Algorithmacy has two nearest neighbors, and the construct must be told apart from each along a different axis.

The first axis separates algorithmacy from algorithm sensemaking. Sensemaking is a cognitive process. It is how a worker arrives at a working model of the system she operates under, through focused enactment, selection, and retention (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023). Algorithmacy is a coordinative competency, not a cognitive process, and its object is the counterpart, not the system. A worker can make excellent sense of the algorithm and, in that account, have done nothing toward the rider or the client she is trying to reach through it. Sensemaking sits inside algorithmacy as a contributor to the inferential dimension and does not exhaust it.

The second axis separates algorithmacy from the algorithmic competency of Zhou, Lei, Liu, Huang, and Hou (2025). That construct is a competency, which makes it closer than sensemaking on the first axis. It differs on a second axis: its theoretical object. Algorithmic competency is a worker-to-system relation, a dyad. Its dimensions target the worker's relation to the algorithm, the understanding of it, the acceptance of it, the leveraging of it, the working around it. The counterpart enters only as a downstream outcome that the algorithm rewards. Algorithmacy is a worker-to-counterpart relation reached through the system, a triad. The two constructs differ by what the form introduces, the constitutive third party between two human principals, not by what the worker does cognitively.

The discriminant strategy is empirical, and the review names it without running it. Place algorithmacy items alongside the nearest neighbors and test for incremental validity above all of them. The neighbors are Spitzberg's measure of computer-mediated-communication competence, the twelve-item algorithmic-competency scale of Zhou and colleagues, the algorithmic-awareness scale of Zarouali, Boerman, and de Vreese (2021), and an operationalized measure of algorithm sensemaking. The outcomes are coordination outcomes with the counterpart: counterpart ratings, repeat-coordination success, and dispute rates. The target pattern is low-to-moderate correlation with each neighbor combined with significant incremental prediction of the coordination outcomes. The neighboring literature reports correlations in that range for related associations, near r equals .17, which is the magnitude the discriminant test should expect to see and to clear (Zhou et al., 2025).

### 5.5 Nomological net

Algorithmacy should relate to a set of antecedents and a set of outcomes, and the pattern of those relations is the construct's empirical content.

The antecedents are three. The first is co-optation exposure, the worker's history inside coordination forms where a platform stands between the parties and pursues ends of its own. The second is prior literacies, the worker's declarative grasp of how algorithmic and computational systems work, which supplies material the inferential dimension draws on without amounting to the dimension. The third is algorithm sensemaking, which feeds the inferential dimension and may stand either before algorithmacy as an input or alongside it as a correlate. The order is an open question the measurement program can settle.

The outcomes are coordination effectiveness with the counterpart and the returns that follow from it: earnings, satisfaction, and tenure on the platform. The anchor for the outcome side is the account of coordination as an achievement. Coordination is produced through accountability, predictability, and common understanding, the three conditions that integrate the work of separate parties (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009). Algorithmacy is the individual-level competency that produces these three conditions when the coordinating partner is reached through an algorithm. A worker high in algorithmacy makes the determination predictable enough to act on, holds herself and the counterpart accountable across an opaque mediator, and builds enough common understanding to coordinate with a counterpart she never meets. The construct predicts that workers high in algorithmacy coordinate better with counterparts they reach only through a mediator, holding constant their sensemaking and their command of the system. Testing that prediction is later work.

### 5.6 The construct the field is already reaching for

Algorithmacy is not a unilateral proposal. The shift from a dyadic frame to a triadic one is already underway across the literatures that study work with algorithms (Anthony, Bechky, & Fayard, 2023; Bailey et al., 2022; Etzrodt, 2022). The clearest statement of the shift comes from inside the platform-research program itself, which observes that prior work has focused on dyadic configurations while platforms in fact involve multiple agents working together (Stelmaszak, Möhlmann, & Sørensen, 2025). That observation is an endogenous warrant. It is the field noticing that its own unit of analysis has fallen behind its object. Algorithmacy is the competency construct that the convergent move requires. The literatures have begun to see the triad. None of them has named the competency it demands.

## 6. The open question and the agenda

Algorithmacy is the competency the mediated triad demands. The construct does its work only if an analyst, handed a particular network, can say whether that network's coordination is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad. Here the review reaches the edge of what it can do.

The divide already shapes the field's work. Scholars who study coordination with algorithms increasingly split, in informal terms, over whether a dyadic frame can absorb the algorithmic third party or whether the third party makes the relation triadic. The split runs through how they frame their cases and does not yet have an explicit treatment in print. It warrants one. The question that decides the split, whether a given form is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, is exactly the question the competency exists to answer in practice. A worker high in algorithmacy is, among other things, a worker who reads correctly whether her counterpart is reached through a mediator or through a moderator, and coordinates accordingly.

The form does not announce itself, and two natural tests for it fail. Counting parties fails. A setting with three parties is a moderated dyad if the third only carries what passes between the other two, and a setting that looks like a worker alone with an app may be a mediated triad whose third human party the interface hides. Reading the interface fails. What a worker experiences and what constitutes her coordination can come apart, by the platform's design, so the surface the worker sees does not disclose the structure she is in. Whether a coordination form requires moderated-dyad or mediated-triad competence is a structural fact that has to be determined, and the construct alone does not determine it.

A landscape built on the moderated dyad cannot even pose the questions the construct opens. It cannot ask how algorithmacy develops over a worker's engagement with a platform, because it has no competency at that level to track. It cannot ask whether algorithmacy transfers across platforms with different architectures or whether each platform demands its own version. It cannot ask whether algorithmacy is antecedent to algorithm sensemaking or consequent on it. It cannot ask whether the competency scales from individuals to teams and institutions. Each of these is a question a measurement instrument shaped to the construct can answer, and none of them is available while the unit of analysis stays the dyad.

That is the work the dissertation's later papers take up. Paper 2 builds a model that catalogs a coordination form as a moderated dyad or a mediated triad, supplying the decision procedure this review names and does not provide. Paper 3 runs experiments with that model, to see how the catalog holds up across a range of forms and against behavior. The eventual measure the program reaches is a person-level instrument, a way to score a given worker's algorithmacy against a form whose structure the model has already settled. The instrument is the supply-side counterpart to the structural model, and it is the measure the construct ultimately needs. This review supplies neither the model nor the instrument. A construct paper that smuggled in its own measurement would blur the contribution, which is conceptual: a coordination form the existing constructs cannot see, a competency that form demands and the field has not named, and a position that tells the new competency apart from its neighbors.

The diagnosis is paradigm-level, not gap-filling. The existing constructs are well built within their assumptions, and refining them further will not reach the phenomenon. The construct landscape describes moderated-dyad navigation. The phenomenon is mediated-triad coordination. Refining the existing constructs cannot reach the phenomenon, because the phenomenon sits at a different structural level from the constructs that are meant to capture it (Suddaby, 2010; Alvesson & Sandberg, 2011). The failure is not in the execution of the constructs. It is in the structural assumption they share. Naming the competency is the first task, because a capacity with no name cannot be measured, taught, or studied. The form has a name now. The decision procedure and the measure are the work that follows.
## 7. Conclusion

The vocabulary the field has for working with algorithms describes a moderated dyad. CMC competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, algorithm sensemaking, and algorithmic competency each locate a worker on one side of a medium, a partner, a system, or a knowledge object, and theorize her relation to it. The coordination that now runs through algorithmic mediators is not that. It is a mediated triad: a worker and a human counterpart, coordinating only through a third party that constitutes the coordination, commits the determinations both must act through, and holds them apart. The competency the mediated triad demands is new in the way literacy was new against oracy, because the structure of coordination has changed. This review has named that competency algorithmacy, placed it in the line from oracy to literacy, foreclosed the constructs that cannot reach it, and specified it to the discipline of construct clarity. It leaves one question open by design. Handed a network, an analyst must decide whether its coordination is a moderated dyad or a mediated triad. The next paper builds a model for that decision. The paper after it runs the experiments. The form has a name. The way to measure it is the program this review opens.
