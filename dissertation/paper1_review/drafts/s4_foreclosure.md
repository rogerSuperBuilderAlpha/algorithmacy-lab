## **4\. The existing construct landscape**

The construct landscape that bears on triadic algorithmic coordination accumulated across three fields in roughly two decades, and the constructs each field produced are cousins whose family resemblance is structural. Computer-mediated communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, the algorithmic and AI literacies, algorithmic competency, and algorithm sensemaking each name something real about a worker's relation to a digital or algorithmic system. Each inherits its unit of analysis from a parent tradition built for other phenomena. What the cluster cannot reach, at the level of conceptual domain, is what the form requires: a worker on one side of an active intermediary that has another human party on its other side and acts on inputs from both. Refining any one of the existing constructs will not produce the construct the form requires, because the unit of analysis the cluster shares is not the unit the form demands.

### **4.1 CMC competence**

CMC competence is the construct algorithmacy most resembles, and the one it most has to leave behind. Spitzberg (2006) folds motivation, knowledge, skill, context, and outcomes into a single individual capacity, the fullest specification of communicative competence the mediated landscape has, and rests the whole of it on a premise the triadic form cannot inherit: that the medium is a channel. The five elements themselves are portable across mediated contexts. Motivation operates as the ratio of approach to avoidance attitudes, knowledge as cognitive comprehension of process and content, and skill as four behavioral clusters carried over from face-to-face research: attentiveness, composure, coordination, and expressiveness. Context spans cultural, chronological, relational, environmental, and functional facets. Outcomes integrate coorientation, appropriateness, effectiveness, efficiency, satisfaction, and relational development. What an integrated capacity at the individual level has to include is what the architecture specifies, regardless of what the medium does.

The channel premise is the structural commitment the architecture preserves throughout. CMC is "any human symbolic text-based interaction conducted or facilitated through digitally-based technologies," and the medium "at some point is computerized" (Spitzberg, 2006, pp. 630–631). The medium is a set of constraints and affordances. The model assumes face-to-face and CMC are more similar than they are different, which means the medium does not pursue objectives across the parties communicating through it. The model itself flags the limit at the level of measurement: items written to capture context, message, and media factors fell short of the multidimensional complexity the original specification expected (Spitzberg, 2006). The context facet carries weight the operationalization never reached. Recent extensions to managerial technology-mediated communication preserve the same architecture under more sophisticated terrain (Koponen, Turunen, Laajalahti, Julkunen, & Spitzberg, 2025).

The architecture transports cleanly into the triadic frame; the channel premise does not. Algorithmacy will inherit motivation, knowledge, skill, context, and outcomes as the elements of an integrated capacity, and it will leave behind the assumption that the medium between the worker and her counterpart is the kind of object the model was built to handle.

### **4.2 Human-machine communication**

Human-machine communication promotes the algorithm from channel to partner, framing artificial intelligence as a communicative subject in its own right (Guzman & Lewis, 2020). The move is necessary for any account of an active intermediary, and it makes machine-side properties tractable in ways the channel framing could not. But the dyad survives the promotion, with one term reassigned to a non-human party. The research program organizes around three orienting domains: the functional asks what the AI does communicatively, the relational asks how humans relate to it, and the metaphysical asks what kind of being is communicating when a machine takes a communicative role. The earlier programmatic foundations of human-machine communication predate the synthesis (see Guzman & Lewis, 2020, for the lineage), and mediatization theory has since extended the program into wider social and institutional structures (Hepp, 2020).

The structural unit throughout is worker on one side, machine on the other. A human counterpart whose communication runs through the machine does not appear in the frame. The frame's limit is now under explicit pressure within its own literature. Recent systematic work names the inheritance directly, observing that earlier research described the mediation as a dyadic arrangement in which a human interacts with an AI and the AI returns a response. Ontological critiques inside the program ask what kind of partner the machine is, and the answers travel toward sharper specification of the human-machine bond rather than toward a structure in which the machine sits between two human parties.

What the repositioning gives algorithmacy is real: license to treat the algorithm as an agent rather than a conduit. A third human party is the one thing the human-machine dyad was never built to hold.

### **4.3 AI-mediated communication**

AI-mediated communication is the first construct in the lineage to put a human counterpart back in the picture, and then it sets the AI on the sender's side. That placement is what triadic coordination asks the field to give up. The construct names "mediated communication between people in which a computational agent operates on behalf of a communicator by modifying, augmenting, or generating messages to accomplish communication or interpersonal goals" (Hancock, Naaman, & Levy, 2020). The structural unit places sender, AI, and receiver in linear order, with the AI on the sender's side and the receiver across the channel. Five dimensions parametrize what the agent does to the message: magnitude, media type, optimization goal, autonomy, and role orientation (Hancock et al., 2020). The dimensions are useful for any setting in which an AI interposes on the production side of a communicative event. Bilateral mediation, where the agent acts on inputs from both human parties, sits outside what the dimensions parametrize.

The empirical literature in this lineage documents effects that travel across both sides of the channel without dissolving the sender-side agency clause. Algorithmic response suggestions change communication speed, the prevalence of positive emotional language, and how partners evaluate one another after the conversation (Hohenstein et al., 2023). A related asymmetry shows up in attribution: when AI-mediated conversations failed, participants assigned less responsibility to their human partners than to themselves, an effect named the moral crumple zone (Hohenstein & Jung, 2020). The agent on one side reshapes evaluation of the human party on the other, and the framing remains within the sender-side agency clause.

The platform algorithm in worker-algorithm-counterpart settings does not operate on behalf of either human party. It pursues platform-specified objectives on inputs from both at once. The role-orientation dimension assumes the AI sits on one side of the channel; the platform algorithm sits between the parties and acts for the principal that owns the system. For what an AI does to one party's message, the five dimensions are exact. For an intermediary that works on both parties' inputs at once, they have nowhere to put the second party.

### **4.4 The literacy frameworks**

The algorithmic and AI literacies measure what a user knows about the systems she engages, and knowledge of the system is the unit of analysis they share. Coordination through the system with another human party stays outside both frames. The algorithmic literacy lineage opens with the finding that 62.5% of Facebook users were unaware their News Feed was algorithmically curated, and the awareness gap motivated a decade of work on what users know and how they come to know it (Eslami et al., 2015). The algorithmic imaginary names the ways users think about what algorithms are, what they should be, and how they function, and the imaginary plays a generative role in the algorithm's continued operation (Bucher, 2017). The validated awareness scales formalize the cognitive layer at the level of measurement: a four-dimensional algorithmic media content awareness scale covering content filtering, automated decision-making, human-algorithm interplay, and ethical considerations (Zarouali, Boerman, & de Vreese, 2021); a two-dimensional algorithm literacy scale covering awareness of algorithm use and knowledge about algorithms (Dogruel, Masur, & Joeckel, 2022). The digital-divide literature frames algorithmic awareness as a stratified meta-skill (Cotter & Reisdorf, 2020; Gran, Booth, & Bucher, 2021). The cognitive-process side of the lineage distinguishes functional theories of algorithms (basic awareness, causal effects) from structural theories (mechanistic ordering), and locates literacy in the move from the former to the latter (DeVito, 2021).

The AI literacy lineage operates in parallel and aims at a more ambitious competency taxonomy. Sixteen competencies organize around five high-level questions about AI (what it is, what it can do, how it works, how it should be used, how people perceive it), and a Bloom-derived four-aspect taxonomy covers knowing and understanding, using and applying, evaluating and creating, and ethical engagement (Long & Magerko, 2020; Ng, Leung, Chu, & Qiao, 2021). The Meta AI Literacy Scale extends the cognitive scaffolding into psychological meta-competencies, adding self-efficacy in learning AI, self-management around AI, and a sub-facet for AI persuasion to the use-understand-detect-ethics-create core, and a validated short version reduces the instrument to ten items (Carolus, Koch, Straka, Latoschik, & Wienrich, 2023; Koch, Carolus, Wienrich, & Latoschik, 2024). The unit across both lineages is the user in relation to the system. The other human party whose communication runs through the same system is absent from the conceptual frame.

Knowledge of an algorithm does not entail action with respect to it, and the studies in this cluster bear the gap out. Knowledge by itself does not produce more critical engagement with algorithmic systems (Lomborg & Kapsch, 2020). Pragmatically effective folk theories of algorithmic behavior can be mechanistically wrong, which means the literacy construct measures something workers may not need at high resolution to coordinate effectively (DeVito, 2021). Stretching literacy to cover algorithmic coordination repeats the move oracy-extension would have been at literacy's emergence: productive on what it covers, silent on the activity that distinguishes the new form. The family has by now been integratively reviewed in its own right, and the synthesis organizes the whole lineage around the user's awareness of, knowledge about, and competence toward algorithms, never around coordination with another party through them, which confirms the user-system axis in the literature's own self-description (Gagrčin et al., 2026). What the cluster measures is reading the algorithm; what it leaves out is coordinating through it with another party.

### **4.5 Algorithmic competency and adjacent measurement scales**

By its label, algorithmic competency is the nearest neighbor of all, and the nearness is exactly the trap. Zhou and colleagues (2025) define it as on-demand platform workers' "understanding of platform algorithms that assign and evaluate their work and their ability to adapt to and navigate those algorithms," building on earlier qualitative work with Upwork freelancers (Jarrahi & Sutherland, 2019). The labels nearly coincide; the units of analysis do not, and the methodological tradition algorithmacy rests on was built to expose that kind of gap. Four dimensions emerge through deductive specification and inductive coding of interviews with ride-hailing and food-delivery workers. Understanding gives the worker sophisticated comprehension of how the system operates and the capacity to navigate its dynamic nature. Embracing captures her willingness to trust the system for matching and other functionalities, a dimension added against the earlier qualitative model that reflects observed patterns of higher trust in algorithmic systems among Chinese platform workers. Leveraging is her capacity to use the system strategically, with the label chosen to replace "Manipulating" and remove its negative connotations. Remediating is her ability to address or supplement the system's deficiencies, with the label chosen to replace "Circumventing" and indicate active engagement (Zhou et al., 2025).

The construct describes worker proficiency with respect to the algorithmic management system, and the empirical validation that supports it does not extend to worker coordination with the counterpart through that system. Five samples support the second-order four-factor structure through exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis, with convergent validity against digital competence and competence need satisfaction and discriminant validity against the Algorithmic Management Questionnaire at r \= 0.17 (Zhou et al., 2025). The construct adds explanatory variance beyond perceived algorithmic management, digital competence, and competence need satisfaction in predicting customer-oriented service behavior and identification with gig work. The customer or rider appears in scale items only instrumentally: politeness toward customers is framed as leveraging the algorithm via behavior toward the customer, since high customer ratings feed the platform's matching and incentive systems. The construct's domain is worker proficiency with respect to the system. Coordination with the counterpart through the system is outside the scope the validation addresses.

A second instrument operates one structural level up from algorithmic competency. The Algorithmic Management Questionnaire measures workers' perceived exposure to algorithmic management practices across five dimensions (monitoring, goal setting, scheduling, performance rating, compensation), and the unit of analysis is the algorithmic stimulus the worker experiences (Parent-Rocheleau, Parker, Bujold, & Gaudet, 2024); recent agenda-setting work on the "duality" of algorithmic management, its simultaneously constraining and enabling character, likewise theorizes the worker's relation to the management system and keeps the worker-system dyad as its unit (Jiang, Hu, & Li, 2025). The worker's response to that stimulus operates at a different level. The two scales are complementary inside the worker-system unit. The Questionnaire measures the algorithmic stimulus, the competency scale measures the worker's response, and neither measures the worker's coordination with a counterpart through the algorithm.

The methodological warrant for distinguishing algorithmacy from algorithmic competency comes from the construct identity fallacy framework. Two errors threaten any new construct that shares lexical overlap with an existing one: the jingle fallacy uses the same name for substantively different constructs, and the jangle fallacy uses different names for the same construct (Larsen & Bong, 2016). The first defense against the jangle fallacy is conceptual specification of how the construct's domain differs from related constructs in unit of analysis. The empirical defense that follows it, representing each construct's nomological network and testing whether the indicators cohere separately, is the discriminant-validity procedure developed to arrest construct proliferation (Lawson & Robins, 2021; Shaffer, DeGeest, & Li, 2016). Algorithmic competency is dyadic in unit of analysis (worker, algorithm). Algorithmacy is triadic (worker, algorithm, counterpart). The behavioral lexical overlap between the two constructs is real, and the structural domain is different in kind.

### **4.6 Algorithm sensemaking**

If algorithmic competency is the closest cousin by label, algorithm sensemaking is the closest by process, and the parent literature has itself begun to mark the difference. Sensemaking happens inside the worker; algorithmacy happens between two parties through a third. Drawing on Weick's enactment theory, Möhlmann, Alves de Lima Salge, and Marabelli (2023) specify three sub-elements platform workers use to keep up with algorithmic instructions. Focused enactment is the bracketing move: a worker seeks information by searching, requesting, and finding cues on online communication platforms, and she conducts backward testing to validate hunches about algorithmic actions through data acquired on the job. Selection operates through finding glitches, where the worker compares algorithmic instructions against real-time conditions, and through discovering patterns, where she analyzes data to develop a stable interpretation of what happened. Retention runs through physical encounter, where the driver remembers incidents she experienced herself, and through community-shared theorizing, where she tests other drivers' interpretations against her own data. The model frames algorithm sensemaking as a mediator linking workers' perceived tensions in the work environment and their behavioral responses.

The construct does work the literacy frameworks cannot. It captures how a worker comes to a working model of an opaque adaptive system through participation rather than through instruction, and it specifies the cognitive moves that generate the model. The unit of analysis is worker-system. The riders, passengers, and customers whose communication runs through the same algorithm do not appear in the model.

The parent literature is now extending past the dyadic frame from inside the same author network. Algorithmic management on online labor platforms operates along two dimensions, matching and control, where matching presupposes a counterpart on the other side of the algorithm whose request the algorithm joins to the worker's input (Möhlmann, Zalmanson, Henfridsson, & Gregory, 2021). The matching dimension analyzes the platform from the design perspective. More recent work on Uber explicitly frames the setting as one that "draws on human drivers, human passengers, and algorithms that regulate their interactions," and theorizes how a collective of algorithms delegates to drivers and riders alike through complex interactions between multiple algorithms and many humans (Stelmaszak, Möhlmann, & Sørensen, 2025). The same first author who specified algorithm sensemaking is now publishing in explicit three-party terms. The 2023 model supplies the foundational worker-side cognitive process; the more recent work acknowledges that the structural extension at the worker level is open theoretical territory.

The directional relationship between sensemaking and algorithmacy is an empirical question the dissertation's later papers will test. Sensemaking may be antecedent to algorithmacy, may be a consequence of it, or may stand as an independent capacity that interacts with it under specific conditions. The two constructs do not occupy the same unit of analysis. Sensemaking specifies how a worker comes to a model of the system. Algorithmacy specifies what she does, through the system, to coordinate with another party.

### **4.7 The shared structural limit**

The constructs reviewed share a commitment that links them across the changes each made to its predecessors: each locates a worker on one side of a medium, channel, partner, system, knowledge object, or cognitive process, and none locates her on one side of a third party that acts on inputs from both her and the human party she is trying to reach. The commitment is the dyadic ontology each construct inherited from its parent tradition. CMC competence and its descendants in human-machine communication and AI-mediated communication carry the sender-channel-receiver frame forward from interpersonal communication theory (Spitzberg, 2006); the channel becomes a partner (Guzman & Lewis, 2020\) or the AI takes a sender-side production role (Hancock et al., 2020), and the dyad survives each repositioning. The algorithmic and AI literacies carry forward the user-reads-system axis from media and digital literacy (Dogruel et al., 2022; Long & Magerko, 2020). Algorithmic competency carries forward the user-system axis from sociotechnical systems theory (Zhou et al., 2025), and algorithm sensemaking carries forward Weick's enactment-theoretic actor making sense of an environment (Möhlmann et al., 2023). None of the parent traditions theorized a third party that simultaneously sits between two human parties, accepts inputs from both, and pursues objectives that span both.

Each parent tradition did real work at its own unit of analysis. What separates these constructs from triadic coordination is not a deficiency to be refined away but a difference of domain: a construct's domain is fixed by its unit of analysis, and refining a dyadic construct yields a more sophisticated dyadic measure, never a triadic one (Larsen & Bong, 2016). The limit holds across the whole cluster because the unit does.

Where Section 2 checked that the recent reviews had not simply missed the axis in their search, the point returns here at the level of theoretical synthesis: the field's most recent integrative frameworks leave it out. Hillebrand, Raisch, and Schad (2025) integrate human-AI collaboration and algorithmic management along context, agency, interaction, and outcome, reading the two literatures as accounts of how humans use AI to manage and how humans are managed by AI. Murray, Rhymer, and Sirmon (2021) and Raisch and Krakowski (2021) extend conjoined-agency theory into organizational routines and work out the automation-augmentation paradox in manager-machine terms. None of the three names the worker-counterpart axis through the algorithm as a distinct object. The everyday case of platform work, where the worker coordinates with a counterpart through algorithmic management without managing it or being managed by it, has no place in the field's leading frames.

The construct the form requires has to be built from the form. Refine any of the existing constructs and the dyadic unit survives.
