## **8\. Conclusion**

Coordination through a third party that pursues its own objectives across both parties is an old form of human cooperation, and the worker-level competency it demands is the one the field has had no name for. A lawyer reaches the opposing counsel only through what the judge admits; a driver reaches a rider only through what the matching algorithm assigns; in every such case the third party's objectives, not the parties' intentions, set what passes between them. Competence inside that form is not competence in any of the dyadic arrangements the third party displaces.

Three conditions hold across every instance of the form, the ones Section 3 specified: each party sends signals the third party transforms before the other sees them, the system takes inputs only in its own vocabulary, and the rules of transformation stay opaque and shifting. Coordination forms have to secure accountability, predictability, and common understanding (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009); hierarchies secure them through delegated authority, markets through prices and contracts, networks through trust and reciprocity. Triadic algorithmic coordination has none of those mechanisms, and the integrating conditions hold only insofar as workers exercise some competency under the three conditions the form imposes.

The competency constructs the field has built for algorithmic systems do not reach the form, and they fail for one structural reason. CMC competence, human-machine and AI-mediated communication, the literacies, and algorithm sensemaking each place a worker on one side of a medium, a partner, a system, or a process, and never on one side of a third party that acts on inputs from both. The dyadic inheritance is the field-level pattern, and it is why refining any one construct cannot reach the triadic form.

A new coordination form has produced a new competency before, and the new competency was not a refinement of the prior one. Writing produced cognitive operations and coordinative arrangements oral practice could not sustain, and the institutions that grew up around documents required a competency that took on a name (literacy) which survived. The literacy frameworks treat the algorithm as a complex text, and the treatment misses what makes algorithmic engagement structurally different from reading: a document conveys what its author wrote without acting, and an algorithm transforms each input as part of its operation in pursuit of objectives that span both human parties. Sociologists have settled the form's distinctness at the organizational level: platforms operate as a fourth coordination mechanism beside hierarchy, market, and network, and their principle is co-optation (Stark & Pais, 2020; Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Latour drew, two decades ago, the line between media that transform what passes through and media that merely convey (Latour, 2005). What the individual must do inside the form has stayed open at the worker level, and the structural account is where to begin.

Algorithmacy is the communication competency that explains why equivalently positioned platform participants achieve divergent coordination outcomes when working through identical algorithmic systems. The construct integrates knowledge, skill, motivation, and situated practice into measurable individual capacity, and it develops through participation in the form rather than through instruction about it. Three dimensions correspond to the three conditions: a worker exercises algorithmacy by reading outcomes back to the inputs that produced them under signal asymmetry, by translating situated intent into the system's vocabulary under intent compression, and by tracking shifts in the rules under opaque mediation. The construct extends CMC competence at the structural level rather than refining it; it runs orthogonal to the literacy frameworks; it stands adjacent to algorithm sensemaking, with the directional relationship between them open to empirical resolution; it overlaps partially with HMC and AI-MC. The four elements of construct clarity (Suddaby, 2010\) hold, and the measurement program the construct makes possible is what the dissertation's later papers undertake.

The variance puzzle is what algorithmacy makes tractable. Equivalently positioned workers achieve divergent coordination outcomes on identical platforms (Cook et al., 2021; Rahman, 2021), and the divergence has been read either as bias the platform produced or as luck the worker drew. Neither reading reaches what the variance is. The variance is a difference in what workers know how to do under signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation, and the existing competency vocabulary has not equipped the field to read it that way. With the construct in place, the empirical question is whether divergence in coordination outcomes tracks divergence in algorithmacy, and the measurement work the construct makes possible is the means of answering it.

Institutions are already building curricula for the form, and the vocabulary available to them was built for a different one. Bootcamps, certification programs, gig-worker advocacy organizations, platform-cooperative experiments, and state-level workforce-development efforts converge on what they variously call algorithmic skill, AI fluency, or platform literacy. Each of those terms names something the algorithm does or shows, and the curricula built around them train workers to read what the algorithm displays. The work requires coordination with a human counterpart through a third party that acts on inputs from both sides in pursuit of its own ends, and a curriculum that names the third party without naming the coordination through it will train a competency the form does not require. The vocabulary the field supplies over the next several years will determine whether the workers who pass through those curricula are trained for the coordination they will actually have to do.

The case for adopting algorithmacy rests on the same warrant that justified literacy two centuries ago: a new coordination form has produced a competency of its own kind, and the form is best served by terms built for it. The work workers have been doing in triadic algorithmic coordination has had no name in the field's competency literature, and that absence has been what the institutions converging on the new form have been working around. With the construct in place, the field can now see what workers have been doing and ask what the institutions that will train them are training for.
