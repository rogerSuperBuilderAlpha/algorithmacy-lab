## **8\. Conclusion**

Coordination through a third party that pursues its own objectives across both human parties is a structurally distinct form of human cooperation, and the worker-level competency the form requires is what the field's existing literature has not had a name for. Two lawyers reach the opposing counsel only through what the judge admits, rules on, or excludes. Two traders reach each other only through what the market maker matches. An applicant reaches a hiring manager only through what the applicant tracking system forwards. A driver reaches a rider only through what the matching algorithm assigns. In each case the third party's objectives, not the human parties' intentions, determine what passes between them, and the human parties' competence inside the form is something distinct from competence in any of the dyadic arrangements the third party displaces.

Three conditions hold across every instance of the form, and they are constitutive of the form rather than incidental to particular implementations. Each party sends, and neither sees what reached the other side or what the third party did between: signal asymmetry. The third party accepts inputs in its own vocabulary or not at all, and a worker's situated intent will not always translate without loss: intent compression. The rules governing the transformation are unpublished, unstable, and revised as the system learns from the interactions it mediates: opaque mediation (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). Coordination forms have to secure accountability, predictability, and common understanding (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009); hierarchies secure them through delegated authority, markets through prices and contracts, networks through trust and reciprocity. Triadic algorithmic coordination has none of those mechanisms, and the integrating conditions hold only insofar as workers exercise some competency under the three conditions the form imposes.

The competency constructs the field has built for engaging algorithmic systems do not reach the form, and the limit they share is structural. CMC competence integrates motivation, knowledge, skill, context, and outcomes into an individual capacity for working through a medium that conveys what the sender produces (Spitzberg, 2006); the medium has properties that shape latency and persistence, and it does not pursue objectives across both human parties. HMC repositions the algorithm as a communicating partner (Guzman & Lewis, 2020), and the dyad survives the repositioning. AI-MC describes communication an AI on the sender's side has shaped (Hancock, Naaman, & Levy, 2020), and the AI is in the sender's production process rather than in the structural position between sender and receiver. The literacy frameworks measure what users know about the systems they engage, and the conflation of knowledge with capacity to coordinate cannot be undone within the literacy vocabulary. Algorithm sensemaking captures how a worker comes to a working model of an algorithmic system through participation (Möhlmann, Alves de Lima Salge, & Marabelli, 2023), and the construct describes what happens inside the worker rather than the coordination she conducts through the system. Each construct names something real, and the constructs share a sender-medium-receiver unit of analysis the triadic form does not fit.

A new coordination form has produced a new competency before, and the new competency was not a refinement of the prior one. Writing produced cognitive operations and coordinative arrangements oral practice could not sustain, and the institutions that grew up around documents required a competency that took on a name (literacy) which survived. The literacy frameworks treat the algorithm as a complex text, and the treatment misses what makes algorithmic engagement structurally different from reading: a document conveys what its author wrote without acting, and an algorithm transforms each input as part of its operation in pursuit of objectives that span both human parties. The structural literature has settled the form's distinctness at the organizational level. Platforms operate as a fourth coordination mechanism alongside hierarchy, market, and network, and their operative principle is co-optation (Stark & Pais, 2020; Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Network theory has carried, for two decades, the distinction between media that transform what passes through and media that merely convey (Latour, 2005). What an individual working inside the form has to know, perceive, and do has remained open at the worker level, and the structural account is the right place to begin.

Algorithmacy is the communication competency that explains why equivalently positioned platform participants achieve divergent coordination outcomes when working through identical algorithmic systems. The construct integrates knowledge, skill, motivation, and situated practice into measurable individual capacity, and it develops through participation in the form rather than through instruction about it. Three dimensions correspond to the three conditions: a worker exercises algorithmacy by reading outcomes back to the inputs that produced them under signal asymmetry, by translating situated intent into the system's vocabulary under intent compression, and by tracking shifts in the rules under opaque mediation. The construct extends CMC competence at the structural level rather than refining it; it runs orthogonal to the literacy frameworks; it stands adjacent to algorithm sensemaking, with the directional relationship between them open to empirical resolution; it overlaps partially with HMC and AI-MC. The four elements of construct clarity (Suddaby, 2010\) hold, and the measurement program the construct makes possible is what the dissertation's later papers undertake.

The variance puzzle is what algorithmacy makes tractable. Equivalently positioned workers achieve divergent coordination outcomes on identical platforms (Cook et al., 2021; Rahman, 2021), and the divergence has been read either as bias the platform produced or as luck the worker drew. Neither reading reaches what the variance is. The variance is a difference in what workers know how to do under signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation, and the existing competency vocabulary has not equipped the field to read it that way. With the construct in place, the empirical question is whether divergence in coordination outcomes tracks divergence in algorithmacy, and the measurement work the construct makes possible is the means of answering it.

Institutions are already building curricula for the form, and the vocabulary available to them was built for a different one. Bootcamps, certification programs, gig-worker advocacy organizations, platform-cooperative experiments, and state-level workforce-development efforts converge on what they variously call algorithmic skill, AI fluency, or platform literacy. Each of those terms names something the algorithm does or shows, and the curricula built around them train workers to read what the algorithm displays. The work requires coordination with a human counterpart through a third party that acts on inputs from both sides in pursuit of its own ends, and a curriculum that names the third party without naming the coordination through it will train a competency the form does not require. The vocabulary the field supplies over the next several years will determine whether the workers who pass through those curricula are trained for the coordination they will actually have to do.

The case for adopting algorithmacy as the vocabulary the form requires rests on the same warrant that justified literacy two centuries ago: a structurally distinct coordination form has produced a structurally distinct competency, and the form is best served by terms built for it. The work workers have been doing in triadic algorithmic coordination has had no name in the field's competency literature, and that absence has been what the institutions converging on the new form have been working around. With the construct in place, the field can now see what workers have been doing and ask what the institutions that will train them are training for.
