## The variance puzzle (the open) — cornerstone + recent

- **[cornerstone] Cook, C., Diamond, R., Hall, J. V., List, J. A., & Oyer, P. (2021).** The gender earnings
  gap in the gig economy: evidence from over a million rideshare drivers. *Review of Economic Studies*,
  88(5), 2210–2238. **VERIFIED via Consensus.** ~7% gender earnings gap fully attributed to three
  *non-discrimination* factors — experience (learning-by-doing), where/when to drive, and driving speed; no
  customer discrimination. *Use as:* the large-N real-world correlate — the gap is not discrimination but a
  function of how drivers operate the system, i.e. of something the worker does, unnamed. DOI:
  10.1093/restud/rdaa081.
- **[recent] Bokányi, E., & Hannák, A. (2020).** Understanding inequalities in ride-hailing services through
  simulations. *Scientific Reports*, 10, 6500. **VERIFIED.** Calibrated simulation: small parameter changes
  "distribute vastly different incomes to **identically performing drivers**," producing an unpredictable
  system. *Use as:* THE puzzle anchor — performance held constant, divergence manufactured downstream by the
  matching mechanism. (arXiv 2019 "Ride-share matching algorithms generate income inequality" is the same
  work; cite the *Sci. Rep.* version.) DOI: 10.1038/s41598-020-63171-9.
- **[recent] Schor, J. B., et al. (2020).** Dependence and precarity in the platform economy. *Theory &
  Society*, 49, 833–861. **VERIFIED.** n=112 across 7 platforms: outcomes "vary significantly across **and
  within** platforms"; the two dominant accounts (precarity, algorithmic control) both predict *common*
  experience and so cannot explain the within-platform variance. *Use as:* the management-grade framing of
  the puzzle — a field-level mechanism is missing. DOI: 10.1007/s11186-020-09408-y.
- **[recent] Sühr, T., et al. (2019).** Two-sided fairness for repeated matchings… a ride-hailing platform.
  *KDD.* Documents "significant inequality in the driver income distribution" from the matching mechanism on
  real data. *Use as:* the dispersion is *system-produced*, not a data artifact. DOI: 10.1145/3292500.3330793.
- **[recent] Pidoux, J., et al. (2025).** Gaining transparency in Uber's algorithmic management. *Sociologie
  du travail / Socio.* (and Pidoux et al. 2024, *First Monday*, Geneva). Driver personal-data ethnography:
  "significant variability in working hours and rates depends on the behavior of the market, which is
  **invisible to drivers**," and this is passed to them individually as "**algorithmic predictability
  risk**." *Use as:* recent empirical bridge from the variance puzzle (§1) to the opacity condition (§3) —
  divergent outcomes individualized and hidden. **NEW — strong.**

## Concern — institutional/training vocabulary (WEB HARNESS run w8qc4nfzg — COMPLETE, slotted into §1.3)

The harness (108 agents, adversarially verified) grounded the Concern claim with concrete, dated, primarily
*official* sources. **"AI literacy" is the dominant institutional term, and the institutions define it as
context-dependent / under-specified** — exactly the paper's point.

- **[official] EU AI Act, Article 4** (Regulation 2024/1689) — binding since **2 Feb 2025**: providers and
  deployers must ensure "a sufficient level of AI literacy"; defined only by worker knowledge/experience/
  training and "the context in which the AI systems are to be used." The flagship "under-theorized but
  binding" example.
- **[official] EU Platform Work Directive (2024/2831)** — first EU law regulating workplace algorithms;
  transparency + human-oversight rights for algorithmic management — **confined to platform workers** (the
  rest of the algorithm-coordinating workforce uncovered). Reinforces the §1 stakes.
- **[official] OECD, *Bridging the AI Skills Gap* (April 2025)** — two-tier vocabulary: "advanced AI skills"
  (~1% of jobs) vs "general AI literacy" (the vast majority); training supply meets 0.3–5.5% of demand.
- **[industry] LinkedIn, *Skills on the Rise 2025*** — "AI literacy" = #1 fastest-growing skill.
- **[official] WEF, *Future of Jobs 2025*** — "Global Skills Taxonomy"; "AI and big data" = fastest-growing
  skill; calls for "GenAI literacy."
