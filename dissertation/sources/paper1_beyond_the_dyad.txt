Paper 1 - Algorithmacy Beyond the Dyad
1. Algorithm sensemaking and the variance phenomenon
Platform algorithms connect workers to tasks. They score, rank, match, monitor, and discipline workers through opaque computational processes that combine coordination and control in a single system (Möhlmann, Zalmanson, Henfridsson, & Gregory, 2021). Workers navigating these systems develop what some organizational theorists call algorithm sensemaking: a process of attending to algorithmic cues, interpreting them by finding glitches and discovering patterns, and storing those interpretations in personal memory and peer networks (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023). The quality of that sensemaking process varies dramatically across workers. Experienced Uber drivers earn 14% more per hour than newcomers, a premium attributable to learning how to strategically accept, cancel, and time trips rather than to demographic or vehicle differences (Cook, Diamond, Hall, List, & Oyer, 2021). Importantly, formal platform training correlates negatively with gig worker income while learning by doing correlates positively (Zheng, Zhan, & Xu, 2024). Some workers game algorithmic systems; others remain trapped in an “invisible cage” of opaque evaluation where the changing criteria for success remain unpredictable (Rahman, 2021). Critically, identical algorithms yield non-identical outcomes because workers develop varying degrees of capacities.
Möhlmann’s algorithmic sensemaking framework explains the cognitive process through which workers interpret algorithmic management. However, we still need to explore how the process differs systematically across workers, and explain why formal instruction fails to close the gap.  This paper argues that platform coordination is not a relationship between two parties, a worker and a tool, but a relationship among three: a worker, an algorithmic intermediary, and another party (customers, employers, audiences). The intermediary does not passively transmit information between the parties. Instead, it first transforms inputs using proprietary logic, agentic models, or token prediction.  Secondly, it pursues optimization objectives independent of both sides.  Finally, it adjusts its behavior in response to aggregate patterns neither side can observe. Georg Simmel established over a century ago that the transition from a two-party to a three-party structure is qualitatively transformative: the third introduces coalition dynamics, autonomous interests, and mediation capacities absent from any dyad (Simmel, 1908/1950). Recent organizational research empirically confirms the structural claim that two-person leadership arrangements are uniquely unstable across both NASA space analog teams and five centuries of Imperial Roman governance, whereas three-or-more-person arrangements self-stabilize through coalition formation (DeChurch, Lungeanu, Chan, & Contractor, 2025).
Nevertheless, current frameworks of human interaction with algorithmic systems rely on a two-party (dyadic) structure inherited from communication theory. “Computer-Mediated Communication” treats technology as a channel between two humans (Clark & Brennan, 1991; Walther, 1996). “Human-Machine Communication” treats technology as a partner in a two-party exchange (Guzman & Lewis, 2020). “AI-Mediated Communication” treats AI as an agent operating on behalf of a single communicator in a sender-receiver pair (Hancock, Naaman, & Levy, 2020).  “Algorithmic literacy” reduces the challenge to factual knowledge. “Higher algorithmic knowledge” predicts less effective behavioral response, not more (Chung, 2025). “Algocracy” describes rule by algorithm, but not the ability to navigate it (Aneesh, 2009). “Algorithmic governmentality” describes a power that bypasses the subject, leaving no room for the differential agency documented empirically (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). Each framework captures critical aspects of algorithmic environments and enables our research, suggesting a leap similar to when oracy became literacy.
Each major shift in communication technology produced a new coordination architecture and a corresponding competency required to operate within it. Writing emerged in Sumer around 3200 BCE as an administrative technology for recording grain stores and tax obligations, enabling bureaucratic coordination at a scale oral memory could not sustain (Goody, 1977). For three millennia, literacy remained confined to scribal elites. Gutenberg’s press (circa 1440) democratized access to written communication, and within two centuries, literacy became the baseline for participation in professional life (commercial, legal, and political). The cognitive restructuring necessary for this shift is clear: writing enabled abstraction, analytic classification, and reasoning from general principles rather than situational experience, transforming not just what people could communicate but how they thought (Ong, 1982; Havelock, 1963). The parallel today follows the same path from administrative roots to popular expansion: Charles Reich documented the collapse of tangible property into administrative status, rights, and access as the dominant form of wealth (Reich, 1964), and the end of Bretton Woods in 1971 shifted economic coordination from fixed institutional structures to floating, market-driven mechanisms. The subsequent rise of platform capitalism completed the transition from ownership to access as the organizing economic principle (Rifkin, 2000). Algorithms now mediate the coordination relationships that bureaucratic literacy once governed: hiring, pricing, matching, evaluation, and reputation. The Sumerian-to-Gutenberg trajectory took three millennia to move writing from elite administration to mass competency. The Long 1968-to-AI trajectory is compressing an analogous shift into decades. In both cases, the communication technology restructured coordination itself, and a new competency became the condition for participation.
This paper proposes algorithmacy as the third iteration of this historical sequence. Oracy is the competency for oral coordination (Wilkinson, 1965). Literacy is the competency for written communication. Algorithmacy names the competency for coordinating through an opaque algorithmic intermediary. Algorithms restructure coordination by discretizing negotiation, trust-building, and skill deployment into computable units, a process Bernard Stiegler calls grammatization (Stiegler, 1998). The divergent sensemaking trajectories Möhlmann documents reflect the differential development of this competency: workers who develop higher algorithmacy recognize signal asymmetry, minimize the loss when compressing complex intentions into narrow platform channels, and build working models of the intermediary despite its opacity. These advantages compound through informational feedback, producing the power-law outcome distributions observed across every platform domain studied ethnographically in the past decade (Schor, Attwood-Charles, Cansoy, Ladegaard, & Wengronowitz, 2020).
The paper then explores co-optation, a fourth coordination mechanism alongside markets, hierarchies, and networks, that requires algorithmacy. In platforms, autonomous actors participate through algorithmic mediation (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Co-optation explains how workers become enrolled. Algorithmacy explains what develops after enrollment. Sensemaking quality varies because workers develop different levels of competency for navigating a three-party coordination structure.
Paper 2 develops the structural properties of Application Layer Communication (ALC), the communicative practices through which algorithmacy operates (oracy operates through speech and listening; literacy operates through reading and writing; algorithmacy operates through ALC), and formalizes them in a construct model. Paper 3 will develop an experimental design to demonstrate the key properties and relationships of ALC.  Paper 1 remains conceptual, approximately 30,000 words,  grounded in Simmelian sociology, organizational evidence from leadership research, and engagement with communication theory and information systems literatures.


1.1 Algorithmic management combines coordination and control
Platform algorithms connect workers to tasks but go further than that. They blend two key functions that traditional organizations keep separate: matching (coordinating supply and demand) and control (monitoring and shaping worker behavior). A study of Uber drivers, along with interviews with executives and engineers, labels this blend the matching-control duality—a core feature of how online platforms manage workers. The same algorithm that assigns rides to drivers also scores their performance, shifts future assignments based on those scores, and can even change what it measures without telling the driver. Workers feel tension in three areas: how tasks are assigned and performed, how pay is set and changes, and what kind of relationship they have with the platform (employment, entrepreneurship, or something else). These tensions are built into the system's merging of coordination and control into a single algorithmic process.
The matching-control duality appears across various platform types. In hospitality, Airbnb leverages algorithmic information asymmetry to increase its influence over host behavior, while hosts develop their unique strategies in response (Cheng & Foley, 2019). For crowdwork, survey data from 1,291 workers indicate that directive oversight by algorithms (algorithmic control) lowers perceived meaningfulness. In contrast, algorithmic matching (the process of task assignment) fosters more positive perceptions. Identity and belonging further mediate both effects (van Zoonen, Sivunen, & Treem, 2024). Consequently, the matching-control duality extends beyond ride-hailing; it typifies algorithmic management wherever platforms coordinate and govern distributed work at once.
A detailed taxonomy lists six control functions within this dual system: restricting (limiting worker options), recommending (suggesting choices), recording (gathering data), rating (scoring performance), replacing (swapping out workers), and rewarding (offering incentives). Each uses behavioral data to create scores, ranks, or classifications that shape access to future work. This process is ongoing: today's performance shapes tomorrow's opportunities. For example, if a driver declines three ride requests in a row, the algorithm may send future offers to other drivers—without any warning. Likewise, a freelancer who responds slowly may drop in search rankings, even if the platform never explains the cutoff.
Platforms do not uniformly apply these control functions. They dynamically calibrate market principles (autonomy, matching, flexibility) and hierarchy principles (oversight, directives, standardization) in response to task characteristics, blending both in nuanced, task-contingent ways rather than operating on a simple autonomy-versus-control binary (Robinson, Jarrahi, Keegan, & Meijerink, 2026). Tasks that require high skill and customization receive a more market-like algorithmic treatment. Tasks that require speed and standardization receive a more hierarchical treatment. The same platform may apply different algorithmic management configurations to different task types simultaneously.
What sets algorithmic management apart from bureaucracy is not speed or scale, but the way coordination and control occur together within a single hidden process. In a traditional company, a manager schedules shifts and also evaluates workers—two separate tasks. Workers can respond differently to each interaction. With algorithms, matching and scoring occur simultaneously and use the same data, so workers cannot see where coordination ends and control begins. They cannot focus solely on getting good assignments or high ratings, because both are shaped by the same hidden algorithm. This leads to measurable effects. A survey across the European Union found that as algorithmic management increased, so did workers' stress and health risks. Another survey in Nordic countries found that more intense algorithmic management nearly doubled stress compared to workplaces without it.
Algorithmic management is no longer confined to gig platforms. It has spread rapidly from platform work to logistics, retail, healthcare, warehousing, and content moderation, encompassing the semi-automated and fully automated execution of managerial functions in traditional employment settings (Keegan & Meijerink, 2025). The matching-control duality first identified in ride-hailing now characterizes algorithmic scheduling in retail, algorithmic performance tracking in warehouses, algorithmic hiring in corporate recruiting, and algorithmic content distribution in media. The organizational phenomenon is general, even if the most granular empirical evidence still comes from gig platforms.
1.2 Workers develop algorithm sensemaking
When faced with algorithmic management, workers do not just accept its decisions. Instead, they develop methods to understand how the system works and what behaviors it rewards. By adapting Karl Weick’s framework, researchers call this algorithm sensemaking—a step-by-step process through which workers interpret and navigate the algorithms managing them. Weick’s original idea sees organizing as actors noticing cues (enactment), interpreting them (selection), and saving successful strategies (retention). Algorithm sensemaking follows the same steps but includes three elements specific to working with algorithms.
Focused enactment describes how workers attend to and experiment with algorithmic cues. Drivers monitor surge pricing maps, track how ride assignments change after declining offers, notice when ratings shift following particular behaviors, and design informal experiments to test hypotheses about algorithmic responses. A driver who accepts every ride for a week and then declines them selectively the following week is conducting a behavioral experiment to determine whether acceptance rate affects future assignment quality. This is not random trial-and-error. It is structured hypothesis testing under conditions of opacity. Selection modes describe how workers interpret the cues they have attended to through two specific mechanisms: finding glitches, which involves identifying algorithmic errors and inconsistencies that reveal something about the system’s logic, and discovering patterns, which involves detecting regularities in how the algorithm responds to particular inputs over time. Retention sources describe the knowledge repositories where workers store and share their interpretations: personal memory, peer networks maintained through messaging groups and driver meetups, and online forums such as UberPeople.net and platform-specific Reddit communities. The sensemaking process mediates between workers’ perception of algorithmic opacity and their behavioral responses, with richer sensemaking producing more sophisticated strategic options.
Sensemaking leads to different work strategies. One study of Uber drivers identified two major approaches: relational games (building good relationships with customers to earn higher ratings and tips) and efficiency games (maximizing earnings by choosing trips, adjusting routes, or using multiple apps). Some drivers build their own tracking tools since the app limits what they can see. Crowdworkers interpret algorithmic scores in four ways: as a barrier (to avoid), as visibility (for self-promotion), as self-extension (as part of their professional identity), or as a rule to optimize. Each approach leads workers to behave differently under the same system.
Sensemaking extends beyond algorithmic management into algorithmic communication more broadly. Interviews with 50 Uber drivers in France find that Weick’s seven properties of sensemaking operate differently under conditions of algorithmic opacity, with certain properties privileged depending on the platform's communicative architecture (El Bourkadi, 2023). Drivers organize both individual and collective resistance strategies to protect occupational health, deploying them on and off the platform when algorithmic communication fails under abnormal working conditions. The finding bridges algorithmic sensemaking to communication theory: the algorithm is not merely a management system that workers interpret but a communicative infrastructure through which coordination occurs and sometimes breaks down.
Algorithm sensemaking is not confined to individual cognition. Online worker communities function as communities of practice for social learning about algorithms, where experienced workers share interpretive frameworks with newcomers through structured mentoring and informal advice (Le Breton & Galière, 2023). YouTube creators develop communally constructed strategic knowledge about recommendation algorithms through what one researcher calls “algorithmic gossip,” informal exchanges about what the algorithm appears to reward and punish (Bishop, 2019). TikTok users share “stories about algorithms” that function as collective sensemaking mechanisms, constructing shared narratives about how the platform distributes visibility (Schellewald, 2022). The social dimension of sensemaking accelerates competency development among workers embedded in rich peer networks, while disadvantaging those who are isolated. In an extreme case, irregularized migrant food delivery workers in the United Kingdom develop informal sensemaking networks, including account-sharing and substitution strategies, to navigate algorithmic systems that function simultaneously as employers and immigration enforcement mechanisms (Sigona, Piemontese, Achi, & Soares Mendes, 2025). The algorithm constitutes not just working conditions but legal visibility itself, and sensemaking under these conditions requires navigating both a labor market and a regulatory apparatus through the same opaque intermediary.
1.3 The variance phenomenon
The empirical evidence converges on a consistent finding: identical algorithmic systems produce dramatically different outcomes across workers with equivalent access. Algorithm sensemaking quality varies across workers; that variance produces differential economic outcomes, and formal instruction does not close the gap.
The quantitative anchor is a study of administrative data on over one million Uber drivers in the United States. Drivers with 2,500 or more lifetime trips earn 14% more per hour than those with fewer than 100 trips (Cook, Diamond, Hall, List, & Oyer, 2021). The premium is attributable to learning where and when to drive and how to strategically accept and cancel trips, not to demographic differences or vehicle characteristics. Experience explains roughly one-third of the observed 7% gender earnings gap in gig work, with men accumulating experience faster because they drive more hours per week and are more likely to have driven in the past. Analysis of over one million food delivery orders on a retail delivery platform confirms the pattern in a different domain: newcomers rely heavily on algorithmic recommendations for task selection while experienced workers strategically deviate from those recommendations using personalized strategies, with the experience premium concentrated in the ability to identify and reject algorithmically recommended tasks that experienced workers recognize as unprofitable (Jiang & Sinchaisri, 2025). The experience premium represents a lower bound on the variance attributable to differential algorithmic navigation. It aggregates all experiential learning without isolating algorithmic understanding specifically, meaning the portion of the variance driven by sensemaking quality alone is likely larger than the 14% headline figure suggests.
Platforms produce this variance across regulatory contexts. The Fairwork Project’s assessment of labor conditions across nine Latin American countries confirms that platforms systematically engineer relations characterized by low pay, poor protections, surveillance, and opaque algorithmic decision-making, yet worker outcomes vary across platforms (Valente et al., 2026). The variance phenomenon is not an artifact of a particular regulatory environment or labor market. It appears wherever algorithmic management operates.
Formal instruction does not close the gap. Platform training correlates negatively with gig worker income, while learning by doing correlates positively (Zheng, Zhan, & Xu, 2024). The finding is counterintuitive: instruction about the system predicts worse outcomes than unstructured participation. Algorithmic environments are what cognitive scientists call “wicked” learning environments, where feedback is delayed, rules change without notification, and the relationship between actions and outcomes is opaque (Hogarth, Lejarraga, & Soyer, 2015). Formal training creates simplified models calibrated to “kind” environments where rules are stable, and feedback is clear. The mismatch between the training and target environments explains why declarative knowledge of algorithms does not produce the procedural competency needed to navigate them. The finding extends beyond platform work: a meta-analysis of 166 comparisons (N > 12,000) shows that learners who struggle with problems before receiving instruction consistently outperform learners who receive instruction first, with effect sizes ranging from d = 0.36 to d = 0.58 under high design fidelity (Sinha & Kapur, 2021). The training paradox is not an anomaly of gig work. It reflects a general cognitive principle: competency in opaque, dynamic environments develops through structured struggle with the environment itself, not through instruction about it.
Qualitative research reveals the divergent trajectories this variance produces. A three-year ethnography of an online labor platform documents workers developing divergent reactivity paths in response to opaque algorithmic evaluation: high-performing, platform-dependent workers who experience setbacks experiment with improvement strategies, while others constrain their activity or exit the platform entirely (Rahman, 2021). The “invisible cage” metaphor captures the experience of working within an evaluative system whose criteria for success and their changes remain unpredictable. A seven-year ethnography of ride-hailing drivers finds that workers develop qualitatively different tactical repertoires for navigating identical algorithmic systems (Cameron, 2024). Engagement tactics (following algorithmic nudges to earn bonuses and maintain ratings) and deviance tactics (manipulating inputs to extract better outcomes) are behavioral opposites, yet both manufacture consent through the subjective experience of skillful agency within structurally confined choices. In a typical shift, a driver completes roughly a dozen rides but has more than one hundred unique interactions with the algorithm, each presenting a micro-decision that the driver’s sensemaking repertoire shapes. Ethnographic interviews across platforms reveal three worker typologies produced not by individual attributes alone but by the interaction between prior capital and algorithmic navigation capacity: Success Stories who leverage platform flexibility into meaningful income, Strugglers who find platform work precarious and unrewarding, and Strivers who hustle across multiple platforms without achieving stability (Ravenelle, 2019). Significant heterogeneity persists even after accounting for platform type, worker demographics, and dependence level, suggesting that neither the precarity frame nor the algorithmic control frame adequately explains the observed variation (Schor, Attwood-Charles, Cansoy, Ladegaard, & Wengronowitz, 2020).
The mechanism linking sensemaking quality to outcomes unfolds through a developmental sequence documented across all platform domains studied. Users develop algorithmic folk theories at varying levels of sophistication, ranging from basic awareness that an algorithm curates content (functional theories) through causal reasoning about specific algorithmic responses to particular inputs (mechanistic fragments) to integrated understanding of how multiple algorithmic processes interact as a system (structural theories) (DeVito, 2021). Workers and users who develop structural theories navigate platforms more effectively than those who operate with only functional awareness. The developmental trajectory begins with a remarkable absence of awareness: 62% of Facebook users in one study were unaware that any algorithm curated their News Feed, believing they saw everything their friends posted in chronological order (Eslami et al., 2015). When given visible algorithmic cues, these users rapidly developed working theories about how the algorithm operated. Critically, they then deployed those theories selectively: actionable theories (those enabling strategic behavioral adjustment) guided behavior, while passive theories (those providing understanding without enabling action) were discarded (Eslami et al., 2016). The pattern repeats across domains. YouTube creators develop communally constructed folk theories about recommendation algorithms. TikTok users construct identity-inflected theories about content distribution. Dating app users theorize how matching algorithms weigh different profile features. In each case, variance in folk theory sophistication produces variance in strategic behavior, which, in turn, produces variance in outcomes. Having knowledge of algorithms does not, by itself, lead to more effective engagement. The competency is navigational, not declarative (Swart, 2021).
The convergent finding across these literatures is the phenomenon this paper sets out to explain. Algorithm sensemaking quality varies across workers. That variance produces differential economic outcomes. Formal instruction does not close the gap. The competency develops through participation in the system rather than instruction about it. Möhlmann’s algorithm sensemaking framework correctly identifies the cognitive process through which workers interpret algorithmic management. It explains how workers make sense of algorithms. It does not explain why their sensemaking differs so systematically across workers who face identical systems. The sensemaking framework adapts Weick’s enactment-selection-retention model to algorithmic contexts, and the adaptation is productive: focused enactment, selection modes, and retention sources specify observable sub-processes that prior accounts left unspecified. Weick’s model, however, assumes that the environment being made sense of is accessible to observation and relatively stable over time. Algorithmic environments violate both assumptions. The system being learned changes continuously through updates, A/B testing, and machine learning adaptation, without notification. Feedback confounds individual action with aggregate platform dynamics, making it impossible for any single worker to isolate the effect of their own behavior. The sensemaking target adapts strategically to the sensemaking process itself: when workers change behavior in response to algorithmic interpretations, algorithms may update to counter those changes, invalidating the sensemaking that produced them (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). In organizations adopting algorithmic decision tools, differential engagement with AI opacity produces qualitatively different knowledge practices: some groups develop “interrogation practices” that enable productive collaboration with algorithmic systems while others adopt superficial compliance that undermines the tool’s value (Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf, & Levina, 2022; Pachidi, Berends, Faraj, & Huysman, 2021). The variance in sensemaking quality is not random. It is systematically patterned.
These properties are not limitations of Möhlmann’s theorizing. They are structural features of the coordination environment that no existing framework has named. The next section examines why.
2. Communication forms
The variance phenomenon documented in the preceding section requires explanation, and the natural place to look is communication theory. Platform coordination is, at bottom, a communication problem: workers express intentions through algorithmic interfaces, algorithms interpret and transform those expressions, and other parties receive the transformed output. The question is whether existing communication frameworks can accommodate the structure of that process. This section examines three major theoretical traditions and five parallel concepts, treating each generously as a genuine advance in understanding mediated interaction. The finding is consistent: every framework inherits a two-party structural assumption that prevents it from modeling the three-party coordination that Möhlmann’s sensemaking research shows workers actually navigating.
2.1 The dyadic inheritance
Modern communication theory begins with a mathematical model designed for telephone engineering. Shannon’s information theory defines communication as the transmission of a signal from a source through a channel to a destination, with noise as the primary obstacle to fidelity (Shannon & Weaver, 1949). Three structural assumptions embedded in this model shaped every communication framework that followed. Communication is point-to-point: one source, one destination. The channel is passive: it degrades, constrains, or adds noise but does not produce, select, or transform content. The success of communication is measured against the source’s intention: fidelity means the destination receives what the source sent.
These assumptions were not arbitrary. They described telephone networks accurately. They also became what one influential critique calls the “conduit metaphor”: the pervasive conceptual structure in which speakers put ideas into words, send them along a conduit, and listeners extract ideas on the other end (Reddy, 1979). The metaphor is not merely a linguistic habit. It is cognitive architecture that shapes how communication scholars conceptualize mediated interaction, concealing the active interpretive work that occurs at every stage and naturalizing the two-party, passive-channel structure as the default configuration of all communication (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980). James Carey identified this as the “transmission view” of communication, contrasting it with a “ritual view” in which communication constructs shared worlds rather than transporting messages between pre-existing minds (Carey, 1989). The transmission view, Carey argued, became the dominant American paradigm, reducing communication to a process of imparting, sending, and transmitting information for the purpose of control. Robert Craig’s metamodel of communication theory locates this inheritance in the “cybernetic tradition,” one of seven foundational approaches, which treats communication as information processing between nodes (Craig, 1999). The cybernetic tradition’s emphasis on signal flow between discrete endpoints privileges dyadic exchange as the prototypical communicative structure.
Every major Computer-Mediated Communication framework preserved this structure while improving theory within it. Social Presence Theory evaluates communication media by the degree of salience of the other person, a metric that presupposes exactly two communicators whose mutual awareness constitutes the relevant variable (Short, Williams, & Christie, 1976). Media Richness Theory ranks communication media along a bandwidth dimension from lean to rich, evaluating them as passive channels with varying capacity for transmitting equivocal information between a manager and a subordinate (Daft & Lengel, 1986). Social Information Processing theory demonstrated that communicators adapt to reduced nonverbal cues by altering language to compensate, achieving interpersonal intimacy through text-based media that earlier theories predicted could not support rich relational communication (Walther, 1992). The hyperpersonal model extended this further: computer-mediated communication can exceed the quality of face-to-face interaction through selective self-presentation and idealized perception (Walther, 1996). The advance was real and substantial. The four-element architecture remained: sender, channel, receiver, and feedback. Even Walther, the CMC tradition’s most prominent theorist, acknowledged that most CMC research had focused on the dyadic relationship, calling for expansion beyond this frame (Walther & Parks, 2002).
The most precise articulation of the dyadic assumption appears in grounded theory. Communication requires a presentation-acceptance cycle in which two participants collaboratively establish mutual understanding through sequential contributions (Clark & Brennan, 1991). Grounding depends on three conditions: participants can observe each other’s contributions, respond to them in real time, and pursue mutual understanding as a shared goal. The framework specifies how different media constrain grounding through “costs” associated with particular communication channels: production costs, reception costs, delay costs, and others. Every medium Clark and Brennan analyzed preserves the three conditions. The telephone constrains visual access but preserves auditory co-presence. Email introduces a delay but preserves editability. Video conferencing restores visual access at the cost of spatial separation. In each case, the medium functions as an infrastructure that enables or constrains a dyadic grounding process without participating in it.
Media Synchronicity Theory refined media richness by distinguishing conveyance (transmitting novel information) from convergence (developing shared understanding), arguing that different communication processes require different media capabilities rather than that richer media are universally superior (Dennis, Fuller, & Valacich, 2008). The refinement was productive. It replaced a single bandwidth dimension with a multidimensional capability space. The medium remained passive, serving as infrastructure supporting dyadic exchange.
The affordance framework represents the most sophisticated evolution beyond media richness. Communication technologies offer relational possibilities for action that emerge between users and technological features rather than residing in either alone (Evans, Pearce, Vitak, & Treem, 2017). Affordances are not fixed properties of a medium but relational configurations shaped by user characteristics, technological features, and context. The framework moves beyond ranking media along static dimensions. Applied to platforms, “platform-sensitive affordances” account for how platforms function as technological intermediaries that orchestrate relationships among multiple stakeholder types (Bucher & Helmond, 2018). This is the closest any CMC-lineage framework comes to recognizing the active channel. The affordance framework can describe what platforms make possible. It does not model the triadic communicative structure through which platform coordination occurs, because affordances characterize what a technology enables or constrains for individual users rather than specifying the structural relationship between three interdependent parties.
The pattern across five decades of CMC development is consistent. Each framework improved on its predecessors, correcting limitations and expanding scope. Social Presence Theory replaced Shannon’s engineering model with a psychological one. Media Richness Theory added organizational context. Social Information Processing theory added temporal dynamics. The hyperpersonal model added sender strategies. Grounded theory added a collaborative process. Media Synchronicity Theory added task contingency. Affordance theory added relational emergence. Each preserved the structural assumption: communication occurs between two parties through a medium that enables, constrains, or degrades their exchange without independently producing, transforming, or distributing content.
Algorithmic platforms violate every element of this structure. When a gig worker crafts a service listing, the presentation goes to an algorithm that classifies, ranks, and distributes it according to logic the worker cannot observe. The acceptance comes from the algorithm’s classification decision, not from the intended audience. Neither party can observe the other’s contributions directly. Neither is working toward mutual understanding with the other. The algorithm pursues optimization objectives independent of both. Algorithms have become a communication technology operating by entirely different logics: selecting, filtering, ranking content through opaque procedures that serve platform interests rather than transmitting messages between senders and receivers (Gillespie, 2014). The CMC tradition’s frameworks do not fail because they are wrong about dyadic communication. They fail because algorithmic platforms are not dyadic.
2.2 Human-Machine Communication
Human-Machine Communication challenges the conduit assumption by recognizing the machine as a communicative entity rather than a passive channel. The field emerged from the observation that a fundamental disconnect exists between communication theory and emerging technology: machines increasingly participate in communicative exchanges rather than merely facilitating them (Guzman & Lewis, 2020). The advance is genuine. The CASA paradigm (Computers Are Social Actors) was established through experimental evidence that humans apply social rules to computers, treating them as social entities deserving of politeness, reciprocity, and personality attributions (Nass, Steuer, & Tauber, 1994; Reeves & Nass, 1996). Extensions of CASA propose that humans develop unique scripts for machine interaction rather than mindlessly importing human-to-human scripts (Gambino, Fox, & Ratan, 2020). Recent work distinguishes two foundational HMC paradigms: Media Equation (machines elicit social responses because they trigger evolved human scripts) and Media Evocation (machines prompt ontological reflection about the nature of communicative agents) (van der Goot & Etzrodt, 2023). The Media Are Social Actors paradigm extends CASA through evolutionary psychology and mindful anthropomorphism, proposing nine updated propositions about human-media social responses (Lombard & Xu, 2021). These are real theoretical advances. Machines are not pipes.
The structural limitation persists. HMC replaces one dyad (human communicating through technology to another human) with another (human communicating with technology as a partner). The machine moves from channel to communicator. The structure remains two parties. The field’s founding editorial frames HMC as addressing the question: “When we say that humans communicate with a machine, do we mean the same thing as when we say a human communicates with another human directly or through a medium?” (Fortunati & Edwards, 2020). The phrasing encodes the limitation. HMC theorizes communication WITH machines. It defers communication THROUGH machines, the case where a machine intermediates between two humans who lack direct access to each other, to other frameworks that have not yet adequately theorized it.
The most direct test of this limitation comes from research on triadic situations in HMC. A quasi-experiment comparing dyadic (one human and a voice assistant) and triadic (two humans and a voice assistant) interactions found that the triadic situation led to more confounded categorization of the voice assistant’s subjecthood and altered the relational dynamics among all three parties (Etzrodt, 2022). The second human’s presence changed how users perceived and related to the machine. HMC’s dyadic model could not predict or explain the triadic effects because it was not built to accommodate them. The finding is consistent with HMC’s theoretical development: even the most recent programmatic statements, which introduce the Act-Mean-Relate paradigm and acknowledge “design as a third party in communicative triads,” locate the third party in the designer’s intentions rather than in the algorithm’s real-time intermediation between users (Edwards & Etzrodt, 2025).
The 4C’s framework represents the most expansive HMC reconceptualization, categorizing AI’s communicative roles as Communicator, Creator, Curator, and Conversant (Sundar & Lee, 2022). The Curator role, in which AI selects, filters, and distributes content to audiences, is closest to the intermediary function that algorithmic platforms perform. The framework recognizes the role. It does not provide a structural theory of how curation constitutes a three-party communicative relationship in which the curator’s optimization objectives differ from those of both the content creator and the audience.
HMC correctly identified that machines are communicative entities. The field’s dyadic architecture prevents it from theorizing the case most relevant to platform coordination: the machine that communicates neither with a single human as a partner nor as a channel between two humans, but rather as an active intermediary transforming both sides’ inputs in accordance with optimization objectives that neither controls.
2.3 AI-Mediated Communication
AI-Mediated Communication addresses the specific case in which AI modifies human communication. The foundational definition frames AIMC as interpersonal communication in which an intelligent agent operates on behalf of a communicator by modifying, augmenting, or generating messages to accomplish communication goals (Hancock, Naaman, & Levy, 2020). The framework specifies five dimensions along which AIMC systems vary: magnitude of AI modification, media type, optimization goal, level of autonomy, and role orientation. The advance is precise and productive. AIMC names a real phenomenon that neither CMC nor HMC addresses: the case in which AI transforms the content of human communication rather than merely transmitting it or serving as a conversation partner.
The framework’s structural assumption is that the AI operates instrumentally within a sender-receiver dyad. The sender retains communicative agency. The AI serves the sender’s goals. The “on behalf of” framing preserves the dyadic architecture by treating the AI as an extension of one party rather than an independent third party. Five dimensions characterize the system, but all five presuppose the human communicator retains primary agency: the AI modifies ON BEHALF OF the user, augments TOWARD the user’s goals, and generates IN SERVICE OF the user’s intentions.
Three empirical findings from within the AIMC research program itself undermine this assumption. Users co-writing with opinionated language models shifted their attitudes toward the AI’s bias, an effect the researchers call “latent persuasion” (Jakesch, Bhat, Buschek, Zalmanson, & Naaman, 2023). The shift was not performative compliance. It persisted into post-task attitude surveys conducted after the writing task ended. A follow-up study across five societal topics confirmed that the effect resists advance warning about bias and survives post-task debriefing (Williams-Ceci, Jakesch, Bhat, Kadoma, Zalmanson, & Naaman, 2026). The AI did not operate on behalf of the user. It constituted a new attitudinal position that the user did not hold before the interaction. AI-generated smart replies change how people interact with each other, increasing speed and positive emotional language while causing negative evaluations when the recipient suspects AI involvement (Hohenstein et al., 2023). The AI introduces its own linguistic tendencies that restructure the communicative exchange, producing effects that the user did not authorize and may not recognize. Across six experiments with over 4,600 participants, humans cannot reliably distinguish AI-generated self-presentations from human-generated ones, and the heuristics they rely on for detection are systematically flawed (Jakesch, Hancock, & Naaman, 2023). If AI-generated communication is undetectable and the AI independently reshapes both content and the communicator’s own beliefs, the “on behalf of” framing describes a dyadic relationship that no longer obtains.
The distinction between communicating through AI and communicating with AI collapses under these conditions (Natale, 2021). AI simultaneously functions as the channel through which communication passes and as a producer of communication content. The “on behalf of” framing cannot accommodate an entity that occupies both positions at once, because the framing presupposes that the AI serves one party’s goals while the evidence shows the AI reshaping those goals in the process of serving them. AI writing suggestions lead users from non-Western cultures to adopt Western writing styles, overriding the cultural communicative norms they presumably wish to express (Agarwal, Naaman, & Vashistha, 2024). When AI generates political communication on behalf of elected officials, the representative’s authentic views may not survive the generation process, raising questions about democratic accountability that the “on behalf of” framing cannot address (Kreps & Jakesch, 2023). The human communicator becomes what one researcher calls a “moral crumple zone”: the entity held responsible for communicative outcomes produced by a system operating according to its own logic (Elish, 2019).
AIMC correctly identified that AI transforms human communication rather than merely transmitting it. The “on behalf of” framing preserves a dyadic sender-receiver architecture that the framework’s own empirical program has shown to be inadequate. The AI does not serve one party. It constitutes a third party whose optimization objectives, linguistic tendencies, and cultural biases enter the communicative exchange independently of either human participant’s intentions.
2.4 Alternative frameworks and their shared limitation
Five parallel concepts name aspects of the algorithmic phenomenon without naming the communication competency it requires. Each captures something real. Each inherits a two-party structure: user and system, subject and medium, governed and governor, consciousness and technology.
Algorithmic literacy defines competency as awareness and knowledge of how algorithms work. The most comprehensive validated scale measures two dimensions: awareness of algorithm use and knowledge about algorithms, operationalized through 22 items administered to over 1,000 internet users (Dogruel, Masur, & Joeckel, 2022). Higher algorithmic knowledge predicts a less effective behavioral response rather than a more effective one, a finding replicated across multiple studies (Chung, 2025; Swart, 2021). Algorithmic knowledge gaps are socially stratified: approximately 61% of a Norwegian national sample had low or no awareness that algorithms curate their online experience, with awareness concentrated among younger, more educated, and male respondents (Gran, Booth, & Bucher, 2021). A comprehensive review of 169 studies on algorithmic media use and algorithm literacy found that the outcomes of algorithmic literacy are “rarely explicitly examined” and remain “relatively uncharted territory” (Gagrčin, Naab, & Grub, 2024). Algorithmic literacy frameworks capture the declarative dimension of the phenomenon. They reduce the full challenge of algorithmic navigation to factual knowledge about a system, treating the relationship as user-and-object rather than as a communicative process among three interdependent parties.
Electracy names the shift in the civilizational apparatus from literacy to electronic media, operating at the level of what Gregory Ulmer calls “grammatology”: the study of how recording technologies restructure thought (Ulmer, 2003). The concept situates the current technological moment within a grand historical sequence. Its level of abstraction prevents it from specifying the cognitive operations workers perform when navigating specific algorithmic systems, and its scholarly community has not engaged substantially with algorithmic platform studies (Markelj & Sundvall, 2023).
Algocracy refers to governance by algorithm, theorizing how algorithmic systems threaten the legitimacy of public decision-making because citizens cannot comprehend the algorithmic basis of decisions that affect them (Aneesh, 2009; Danaher, 2016). The concept identifies a real governance condition. It describes the structure of algorithmic power without specifying the navigational capacities individuals develop in response to it. Algorithmic governmentality extends this by theorizing a form of power that operates through datafication, profiling, and behavioral modulation, bypassing the conscious subject entirely (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). The framework captures a genuine feature of algorithmic systems: their capacity to shape behavior without engaging conscious deliberation. It forecloses the differential agency documented in the empirical record. Workers on identical platforms develop different capacities and achieve different outcomes through active sensemaking. A framework that theorizes subjects as bypassed cannot account for subjects who navigate. Recent extensions demonstrate that despite algorithmic governmentality’s claims about the evacuation of the subject, spaces for ethical conduct and agentic engagement persist in algorithmically governed organizations, modified but not eliminated by algorithmic systems (Weiskopf & Hansen, 2023).
Secondary orality treats the current technological moment as a return to oral-like communication conditions: participatory, communal, and present-focused rather than analytical, isolated, and abstract (Ong, 1982; Logan, 2010). The analogy captures surface features of digital communication. The cognitive operations platform workers develop, including systematic hypothesis testing through behavioral experiments, pattern extraction from opaque data streams, and folk theorization at multiple levels of abstraction, have no oral-culture analogue. These are analytical, strategic, and cumulative competencies that build on literate cognition rather than returning to pre-literate forms. The “secondary orality” framing misdescribes the cognitive architecture of algorithmic navigation by assimilating it to a prior communicative form rather than recognizing it as structurally novel.
The “algorithmic imaginary” offers an alternative framing that foregrounds affective and imaginative dimensions rather than cognitive ones, showing that people’s ways of thinking about what algorithms are and how they function generate feelings and behavioral orientations that shape engagement independently of factual knowledge (Bucher, 2017). “Practical knowledge of algorithms” locates algorithmic competency at the intersection of practice and discourse: knowing how to accomplish things within algorithmically mediated spaces rather than knowing that an algorithm does something (Cotter, 2022). Both concepts move closer to the communicative dimension by recognizing that algorithmic engagement involves situated practice rather than detached knowledge. Neither provides the structural framework connecting individual navigational competency to the three-party coordination architecture through which platform work operates.
The pattern across all five concepts repeats the pattern across the three communication traditions. Each concept improves understanding of a specific dimension of algorithmic experience. Each preserves a two-party structure: user and algorithm, subject and medium, governed and governor. None models the three-party communicative relationship in which a worker, an algorithmic intermediary, and another party (customer, employer, audience) coordinate through a system whose optimization objectives differ for both sides. The algorithmic intermediary is not a tool the worker uses, a partner the worker communicates with, or a system that governs the worker. It is a third party in the coordination relationship, possessing autonomous interests, transforming both sides’ inputs, and constituting the conditions under which coordination becomes possible. The next section develops the theoretical basis for this structural claim.
3. The dyad-to-triad argument
The preceding section demonstrated that every available communication framework inherits a two-party structural assumption. This section asks whether that assumption can be relaxed or whether it is load-bearing. The argument proceeds through three claims. The transition from a two-party to a three-party structure is qualitatively transformative in a precise sociological sense, not merely a quantitative expansion. Dyadic communication frameworks cannot accommodate triadic coordination for the same structural reasons that dyadic organizational arrangements cannot sustain distributed authority. The algorithmic intermediary occupies the structural position not of a tool, partner, or assistant but of a constitutive rule system that defines the coordination environment, preexists individual encounters, and produces subject positions rather than merely constraining pre-existing ones.
3.1 Claim 1: The transition from dyad to triad is qualitatively transformative
Georg Simmel established that the move from two participants to three is not an increase in quantity but a change in kind (Simmel, 1908/1950). In a dyad, each member’s withdrawal destroys the group. The dyad depends on the irreplaceable participation of both parties; it cannot survive the defection of either. It generates no structure beyond the relationship itself. A triad introduces properties absent from any dyad: the group can persist despite one member’s withdrawal, coalitions can form between two members against the third, and the structure acquires what Simmel calls a “supra-individual” character, an institutional quality that transcends the individuals who compose it. The third party can occupy one of three archetypal roles. The mediator resolves conflict by neutrally formulating claims from both sides, depriving them of affective charge so that agreement becomes possible. The tertius gaudens, the “third who enjoys,” exploits divisions between the other two for personal advantage, maintaining an equally independent relationship to both and profiting from their competition (Simmel, 1908/1950). The divide et impera, the ruler who divides, actively creates or maintains conflict between the other two to preserve dominance. A fourth role, the tertius iungens or “third who joins,” connects previously disconnected actors and facilitates new coordination, a role empirically demonstrated by a multimethod study of 276 automotive engineers, which showed that orientation toward connecting others predicts involvement in innovation (Obstfeld, 2005).
The theoretical distinction has been operationalized and confirmed across organizational research. “Simmelian ties,” ties embedded in reciprocal triads, constrain behavior differently than dyadic ties: whereas dyadic actors can always threaten departure, triadic actors face coalition pressures and group norms that emerge only when a third party creates the structural possibility of two against one (Krackhardt, 1999). Simmelian bridging ties, those that span structural holes while remaining embedded in triadic closure, facilitate the trust and shared understanding required to transfer complex knowledge across organizational boundaries, a finding that pure bridging ties without triadic embeddedness cannot replicate (Tortoriello & Krackhardt, 2010). Experimental tests confirm Simmel’s prediction: triads produce more group cohesion than dyads, but through a different mechanism. In dyads, positive affect drives cohesion. In triads, uncertainty reduction is the primary driver, and the tertius gaudens condition paradoxically activates two-against-one pressures that “objectify” the social unit, making it feel like an entity rather than a relationship (Lawler, Thye, & Yoon, 2013).
Independent organizational research confirms the structural instability of dyadic arrangements and the stability of triadic ones. Across NASA space analog teams and historiometric data spanning 523 years of Imperial Roman governance (32 BCE to 491 CE), dyadic leadership arrangements persist at the lowest rates of any configuration: 27-31% compared with 53-67% for single-leader hierarchies and 47-59% for group leadership with three or more leaders (DeChurch, Lungeanu, Chan, & Contractor, 2025). Three mechanisms produce this instability. Dyads lack the coalition dynamics that stabilize triads: when conflict arises between two leaders, there is no third party to mediate, arbitrate, or form a stabilizing coalition. Binary conflict amplifies without a buffer: disagreements polarize because followers must choose sides rather than forming cross-cutting coalitions. Follower polarization reinforces the instability: once followers sort into two camps, the organizational pressures for resolution drive the dyad either back toward hierarchy (one leader absorbs the other’s authority) or forward toward group leadership (additional leaders emerge to stabilize the structure). The dyad functions as a transitional state, a “unidirectional bridge” during structural expansion, not a stable organizational form.
The finding extends beyond extreme contexts. Co-leadership in pluralistic organizations is consistently described as “fragile,” requiring continuous maintenance of coupling across leaders, organizations, and environments (Denis, Lamothe, & Langley, 2001). Studies of co-leadership dyads in healthcare find that the bridging potential of shared leadership is often achieved through assimilation of one side by the other rather than through balanced integration (Gibeau, Langley, Denis, & van Schendel, 2020). Executive duos, trios, and quartets studied through a Simmelian lens show that larger leadership constellations offer greater stability through redundant ties and coalition possibilities that two-person arrangements lack (Alvarez & Svejenova, 2005). A meta-analysis of shared leadership confirms that network-based conceptions (which capture triadic and higher-order structure) produce higher effect sizes than aggregation-based approaches (which flatten structure into averages), suggesting that the specific structural topology of leadership relationships determines effectiveness (D’Innocenzo, Mathieu, & Kukenberger, 2016). The evidence converges: dyadic arrangements are structurally fragile across every organizational context studied, while triadic and higher-order arrangements self-stabilize through coalition formation, mediation, and the emergent supra-individual properties Simmel identified.
The algorithmic intermediary occupies a structural position Simmel did not anticipate: a third that performs all four roles simultaneously and at scale. Consider Uber’s algorithm. It mediates between drivers and riders, resolving fare disputes through automated review and facilitating trust through rating systems. It profits from the transaction as tertius gaudens, extracting 25-50% of passenger fares while maintaining equally independent relationships with both sides. Its optimization logic can set the parties’ interests against each other without either party seeing, through opaque pricing, how it keeps drivers from comparing wages while surge algorithms extract the maximum willingness to pay from riders, a form of divide et impera through information asymmetry (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016; Dubal, 2023). It connects previously unrelated actors at the planetary scale as tertius iungens, matching riders and drivers who have no prior relationship and no means of finding each other outside the platform.
Human brokers cannot do this. Research on brokerage processes shows that human brokers alternate between tertius gaudens and tertius iungens orientations over time: short-term interactions favor gaudens strategies while long-term relationships favor iungens strategies, but individuals cannot sustain both simultaneously because the two orientations require contradictory relational postures (Quintane & Carnabuci, 2016). Human brokers’ role orientation is shaped by identification processes: they choose to keep contacts apart or bring them together based on identity commitments that constrain their strategic flexibility (Endacott & Leonardi, 2022). Algorithms face no such constraint. They lack the identification processes that force humans to choose one orientation over another. They can mediate, extract, divide, and connect simultaneously across millions of interactions because they operate through computational logic rather than relational identity. The structural novelty is not that algorithms are faster or larger than human brokers. It is that they occupy a position in the triadic structure that no human third can occupy: simultaneous performance of all four Simmelian roles without the cognitive and social constraints that limit human thirds to alternation.
The platform’s “triangular geometry” constitutes a qualitatively distinct organizational form. Platforms co-opt the behavior of providers and users simultaneously through non-bureaucratic rules, ratings translated into rankings, and feedback loops that deflect accountability while concentrating information advantage in the algorithmic intermediary (Stark & Pais, 2020). The platform treats the market as a search algorithm that blends software computation with human interaction, constructing the market's social order rather than merely facilitating transactions within a pre-existing order (Rilinger, 2024). This is Simmel’s supra-individual character instantiated computationally: the platform creates a structure that transcends the individuals who compose it, defines the terms on which they interact, and persists regardless of which specific individuals participate.
3.2 Claim 2: Dyadic communication frameworks cannot stabilize an account of triadic coordination
DeChurch, Lungeanu, Chan, and Contractor (2025) demonstrate that dyadic leadership arrangements function as unidirectional bridges during structural expansion rather than as stable endpoints. Co-leadership appears during transitions from hierarchy to distributed coordination, then either reverts to hierarchy or expands to group leadership. The dyad cannot persist because it lacks the structural properties that produce stability: coalition dynamics, mediation capacity, and the supra-individual character that emerges only when three or more parties create the possibility of shifting alliances.
An analogous instability characterizes dyadic communication frameworks applied to algorithmic coordination. The analogy is not metaphorical. Organizational structures and theoretical structures share a common property: both are sustained by the relationships among their elements, and both require sufficient structural complexity to generate the emergent properties on which their stability depends. This is what Andrew Abbott calls “self-similarity,” the fractal-like recurrence of common structural oppositions across domains of knowledge and practice (Abbott, 2001). The claim is not that communication theory resembles organizational structure by coincidence. The claim is that both are instances of the same structural principle: dyadic arrangements lack the emergent properties that triadic arrangements generate, whether the arrangement is composed of organizational leaders or theoretical constructs.
Computer-Mediated Communication can describe what happens when two humans communicate through a passive channel. It correctly identifies how communicators adapt to reduced nonverbal cues, develop hyperpersonal relationships, and establish grounding through presentation-acceptance cycles. It cannot describe what happens when the channel actively transforms, selects, and generates content in accordance with optimization objectives, independent of both communicators. The hyperpersonal model explains how a sender can optimize self-presentation through the editability of text. It cannot explain how an AI writing assistant shifts the sender’s attitudes during composition, producing beliefs the sender did not hold before the interaction began (Jakesch, Bhat, Buschek, Zalmanson, & Naaman, 2023). Grounded theory explains how two participants collaboratively establish mutual understanding through sequential contributions. It cannot explain how an algorithmic intermediary selectively shows or hides content, changes temporal sequencing, and inserts recommended material into the grounding process according to logic neither participant controls (Clark & Brennan, 1991).
Human-Machine Communication can describe what happens when a human communicates with a machine as an endpoint. It correctly identifies that machines are communicative entities deserving of ontological analysis rather than mere tools. It cannot describe what happens when the machine serves as an intermediary between two humans who lack direct access to each other, transforming both sides’ inputs in accordance with optimization objectives that neither controls. The field’s own empirical work demonstrates the limitation: triadic situations (two humans and a voice assistant) produce qualitatively different relational dynamics than dyadic situations (one human and a voice assistant), including more confounded categorization of the machine’s subjecthood and altered relational definitions that HMC’s dyadic model cannot predict (Etzrodt, 2022).
AI-Mediated Communication describes what happens when AI augments one party’s messages in service of that party’s communication goals. It correctly identifies that AI transforms the content of human communication rather than merely transmitting it. It cannot describe what happens when the AI operates according to optimization objectives independent of both parties, reshaping the communicator’s beliefs in the process of ostensibly serving them, and producing linguistic and cultural biases that override the communicator’s intentions (Williams-Ceci et al., 2026; Agarwal, Naaman, & Vashistha, 2024). The “on behalf of” framing preserves a dyadic sender-receiver architecture that the framework’s own empirical program has shown to be inadequate.
Each framework extends the dyad to accommodate one additional feature of algorithmic coordination. CMC accommodates the medium’s effects on message quality. HMC accommodates the machine’s communicative agency. AIMC accommodates the AI’s transformative role. These extensions represent genuine theoretical progress. They function as transitional states in the expansion from dyadic to triadic communication theory, just as co-leadership serves as a transitional state in the expansion from hierarchy to group leadership in DeChurch et al.'s data. The frameworks cannot represent the destination because it requires structural properties, coalition dynamics among three interdependent parties, autonomous third-party optimization objectives, and constitutive mediation that transforms both sides’ inputs, features that no dyadic framework can generate, regardless of how many features it accommodates. One historian of communication theory names this constraint directly: much of culture and communication is “not necessarily dyadic, mutual, or interactive,” and dialogue is “only one communicative script among many” (Peters, 1999). The dyadic script has served communication theory well for fifty years. It cannot serve the next fifty because the dominant communication infrastructure of the twenty-first century is triadic.
The triadic structure of communication has been theorized before, though not for algorithmic contexts. A systematic argument for triadic communication draws on Peirce’s triadic semiotics (the irreducible relationship among sign, object, and interpretant), Simmel’s sociology of the triad, and Mead’s interactionist tradition, concluding that communicative meaning is constitutively triadic: it emerges from the irreducible relationship among expression, interpretation, and the mediating structure through which both occur (Shepherd, 1996). Peirce demonstrated that sign relations cannot be decomposed into dyadic components without destroying the semiotic relationship that constitutes meaning (Short, 2007). If signification itself is irreducibly triadic, then communication theories built on dyadic sender-receiver architectures are not merely incomplete but structurally incapable of accounting for how meaning is produced in any mediated context. Algorithmic platforms make this structural incapacity visible because the mediating structure, the algorithm, is active, opaque, and pursuing its own objectives, forcing the triadic character of communication into organizational consequence rather than allowing it to remain a philosophical observation. The Communicative Constitution of Organizations tradition provides a parallel resource: communication constitutes organization through distributed agency among texts, objects, and people, always in relationships that resist dyadic reduction (Cooren, Kuhn, Cornelissen, & Clark, 2011). Texts have genuine agency in organizational settings: they assert, commit, direct, declare, and express (Cooren, 2004). Algorithms are far more powerful organizational “texts” than any document, performing constitutive functions at computational speed and planetary scale.
3.3 Claim 3: The algorithmic third occupies the structural position of a constitutive rule system
The algorithmic intermediary does not transmit (the CMC channel position), partner (the HMC communicator position), or augment (the AIMC assistant position). It constitutes the coordination environment: the system of rules, optimization functions, and classificatory schemes through which coordination becomes possible. The distinction between mediation and constitution is the distinction between a telephone network and a legal system. A telephone network enables communication between parties whose identities, interests, and relationships preexist the network. A legal system constitutes the parties as particular kinds of subjects (plaintiff, defendant, citizen, corporation), defines the terms on which they may interact, and produces the very categories through which coordination occurs. Platform algorithms occupy the structural position of a legal system, not a telephone network. They preexist individual interactions with the platform. They constitute the parties as particular kinds of platform subjects: driver and rider, creator and viewer, host and guest, applicant and hiring manager. These subject positions do not exist prior to the system that generates them. Nobody is an “Uber driver” before the algorithm assigns that identity.
The constitutive character of algorithmic systems has been theorized across multiple traditions. In economic sociology, financial models brought markets into alignment with their predictions rather than merely describing pre-existing market dynamics: the Black-Scholes option pricing model actively constituted the markets it claimed to model (MacKenzie, 2006). Economics does not describe a pre-existing economy; it creates and formats economic reality through models, devices, and calculative practices (Callon, 1998). In organizational theory, algorithms create “classification situations” in which scoring sorts people into categories that determine life chances: credit scores, employability rankings, and content visibility tiers (Fourcade & Healy, 2017). The classificatory power does not merely sort pre-existing subjects into pre-existing categories. It produces the categories and, through them, the subjects who inhabit them. In platform studies, algorithms take on a “constitutive or performative role in ordering the world on our behalf,” circulating and imposing particular forms of knowing and valuing that reshape rationalities and worldviews (Beer, 2017). “Programmed sociality” names the condition in which algorithms create the conditions for sociality and connectivity rather than merely facilitating them: the algorithm produces the social world within which coordination occurs rather than coordinating within a pre-existing social world (Bucher, 2018).
Empirical research across organizational contexts confirms the constitutive claim. In an ethnographic study of power asymmetries in online work, “triangular relations” among platform operators, producers, and end users are structured by an algorithmic third that monitors all parties simultaneously, enforces rules automatically, and adjusts governance in real time (Curchod, Patriotta, Cohen, & Neysen, 2020). The algorithmic intermediary constitutes what counts as a qualified worker, an acceptable deliverable, and a fair resolution. Three hospital departments adopted identical AI diagnostic tools; only one achieved productive collaboration through developing “interrogation practices” that enabled physicians to challenge and refine algorithmic outputs (Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf, & Levina, 2022). The difference in outcomes did not reflect the AI’s accuracy, which was identical across departments. It reflected the departments’ relationship to the AI as a constitutive third party in diagnostic reasoning. In a sales organization adopting algorithmic decision-making, the introduction of the algorithm triggered a clash between competing “regimes of knowing,” challenging expert-based knowledge with data-driven classification and provoking symbolic resistance that paradoxically enabled full implementation (Pachidi, Berends, Faraj, & Huysman, 2021). The algorithm did not supplement existing organizational knowledge. It reconstituted what counted as knowledge.
The constitutive claim extends beyond organizational settings. AI writing assistants do not merely help users express pre-existing opinions. They shift users’ attitudes through co-writing, creating new attitudinal positions that persist after the interaction ends and resist correction through debriefing (Jakesch et al., 2023; Williams-Ceci et al., 2026). Recommendation algorithms do not merely sort pre-existing preferences. They constitute preferences through selective exposure, creating the very taste patterns they claim to detect (Bucher, 2018). Hiring algorithms do not merely screen pre-existing qualifications. They constitute what counts as a qualified candidate through the features they weigh and the training data that encodes historical patterns of inclusion and exclusion. In each case, the algorithm preexists the interaction, defines the subject positions available to participants, and produces rather than merely constrains the coordination outcomes.
Psychoanalytic theory provides a structural vocabulary for this constitutive function. The Symbolic Order in Lacanian theory names the system of rules, categories, and positions that preexists individual subjects and constitutes them as particular kinds of actors within a social field. The subject does not enter the Symbolic Order as a pre-formed agent; the Symbolic Order produces the subject through the process of positioning within its categories (Arnaud & Vidaillet, 2018). Organizational scholarship has productively deployed this framework to analyze how workplace authority operates through subjects’ desire for recognition from an organizational “big Other” rather than through direct coercion (Driver, 2009; Stavrakakis, 2008). The algorithmic platform occupies this structural position. It categorizes workers through data traces without articulating its logic through discourse, functioning as what one analysis calls the “algorithmic big Other” that subjects workers to a symbolic order they cannot directly interrogate (Salter & Dutta, 2023). The algorithm exercises authority not by commanding compliance but by defining the categories within which compliance and resistance both occur. A driver who “games” Uber’s surge algorithm is operating within the algorithm’s symbolic order just as much as a driver who follows every algorithmic prompt. Both are positioned as subjects within a rule system that defines what gaming and compliance mean.
The constitutive rule system has a recursive property that distinguishes it from any prior communication technology. Writing constituted literate subjects, but writing did not change in response to how people read. Broadcast media constituted audiences, but broadcast schedules did not adapt to individual viewing patterns in real time. Platform algorithms constitute subjects AND adapt in response to the subjects they constitute. The system being learned is simultaneously learning the user, creating a recursive loop in which sensemaking and the environment of sensemaking co-evolve (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). This recursive property explains two features of the variance phenomenon documented in Section 1. It explains why formal instruction fails: instruction can teach declarative knowledge about a system, but the system changes in response to the behavior that declarative knowledge produces, invalidating the knowledge before it can be applied. It explains why sensemaking quality varies systematically: workers who develop richer working models of the algorithm generate inputs that yield richer feedback, which accelerates model refinement, while workers with impoverished models generate inputs that yield impoverished feedback, reinforcing the impoverishment. The compounding mechanism is informational rather than merely financial: better sensemaking yields better data on the algorithm’s response patterns, which in turn yields better sensemaking, which yields better data. The loop is multiplicative rather than additive, which is why platform outcome distributions follow power-law distributions rather than normal distributions across all domains studied (Schor, Attwood-Charles, Cansoy, Ladegaard, & Wengronowitz, 2020; Cook, Diamond, Hall, List, & Oyer, 2021).
The three claims converge on a single structural diagnosis. The transition from dyad to triad is qualitatively transformative: it introduces coalition dynamics, autonomous third-party interests, and constitutive mediation that dyads cannot generate. Dyadic communication frameworks cannot accommodate triadic coordination because they lack the structural properties that stabilize triadic systems, just as dyadic leadership arrangements lack the structural properties that stabilize organizations. The algorithmic third occupies the structural position of a constitutive rule system: it preexists individual encounters, defines subject positions, and produces the coordination environment rather than operating within one. The variance phenomenon documented in Section 1, identical platforms producing non-identical outcomes, resolves once the coordination structure is correctly identified. Workers are not merely using a tool (the dyadic framing). They are navigating a three-party coordination environment in which the third party defines the terms of navigation, adapts in response to being navigated, and produces the subject positions from which navigation occurs. The competency required for this navigation has a name. The next section places it in historical context.
4. The historical sequence
4.1 The oracy-literacy precedent
Writing was invented as a coordination technology, not as a medium for literature, religion, or personal expression. The archaeological record is unambiguous: the earliest writing systems served accounting and administration for approximately 600 to 1,000 years before any literary, legal, or religious texts appeared. The competency required to operate within writing-based coordination systems took millennia to develop as a mass capacity after the technology existed. The transition from elite-restricted scribal competency to near-universal literacy required not just the technology of the printing press but institutional supports, public education, copyright law, standardized vernacular languages, that took centuries to develop after the technology was available. No comparable institutional supports for algorithmic competency yet exist.
The sequence from direct possession to mediated coordination begins with the simplest possible case. Around 8000 BCE, clay tokens appeared across the Fertile Crescent, coinciding with the rise of agriculture. Plain tokens represented farm products through simple geometric shapes: cones for small measures of grain, spheres for large measures, disks for sheep, ovoids for jars of oil (Schmandt-Besserat, 1992). The relationship between token and referent was one-to-one. A farmer who held three sheep-tokens held a direct, tangible representation of three sheep. No intermediary system determined what the tokens meant. No third party evaluated whether the farmer deserved those tokens. The coordination technology was transparent, physically possessed, and directly legible to anyone who knew the conventions. For five thousand years, this system sustained economic coordination at the scale agricultural communities required.
Urbanization broke the one-to-one relationship. Around 4000 BCE, complex tokens bearing incised markings appeared for urban products: textiles, metals, processed goods requiring multiple production stages. Around 3300 BCE, administrators began storing tokens inside hollow clay balls, called bullae, with surface impressions for external verification. The bullae introduced a structural novelty: the tokens inside could not be seen without breaking the envelope, so the impressions on the surface served as a representation of what lay within. Around 3200 BCE, the three-dimensional tokens were replaced entirely by two-dimensional impressions on solid clay tablets, producing proto-cuneiform. The earliest tablets from Uruk document economic administration exclusively: analysis of proto-cuneiform reveals multiple simultaneous numerical sign systems, sexagesimal for animals, bisexagesimal for grain, area measures for fields, and reconstructs the administrative activities of a named accountant, Kushim, from approximately 3100 BCE, likely the oldest individual known by name in the historical record (Nissen, Damerow, & Englund, 1993). Writing did not emerge to record stories. It emerged to coordinate an economy too complex for tokens to manage.
The structural shift matters for the algorithmacy argument. The token system operated through direct correspondence: one token, one sheep, held in the farmer's hand. The cuneiform system operated through institutional mediation: a tablet in an archive, maintained by a scribe, interpreted through conventions the farmer could not read, within a bureaucratic apparatus the farmer could not navigate without assistance. The farmer no longer held a direct representation of value. The administrative system held the representation, and the farmer's access to what the representation tracked depended on the system's operations. Writing emerged not as a supplement to accounting but as accounting's institutional formalization: "writing emerged as a supplement to accounting, money emerged as the double supplement to both accounting and writing" (Ezzamel & Hoskin, 2002). Writing and state formation were mutually constitutive: without written records, the size and complexity of government is inherently limited, and states required literate administrators to function at scale (Goody, 1986). The technology that enabled coordination also created dependence on a system only specialists could operate.
During the first 500 to 600 years following its invention, writing in Mesopotamia served exclusively administrative and accounting functions. The earliest literary texts, hymns and proverbs, appeared around 2600 to 2500 BCE. The Epic of Gilgamesh took shape around 2100 to 1800 BCE. This gap of 600 to 1,000 years during which writing served only coordination functions is the strongest archaeological evidence that writing was fundamentally a coordination technology. Literature, law, and religion were secondary applications discovered after the technology matured. Scribal training at the Sumerian edubba required approximately twelve years of cuneiform education and was restricted to sons of wealthy families: governors, ambassadors, temple administrators, military officers, and scribes. Literacy in Classical Athens reached approximately 5 to 10 percent of the population. In the Roman Empire, the figure never exceeded 10 to 15 percent even in favorable urban areas. In ancient Mesopotamia, literacy rates fell well under 1 percent of the total population (Harris, 1989). The competency existed for millennia as an elite monopoly. The technology existed. The institutional mechanism for making the competency universally accessible did not.
Johannes Gutenberg's movable-type printing press, developed around 1440, transformed the economics of text production, reducing the cost of a book by a factor of roughly 80 within fifty years. The press enabled dissemination of identical copies, standardization of texts ending scribal drift, preservation through fixity so knowledge could accumulate rather than decay, and collective data correction as widely scattered readers contributed improvements to new editions (Eisenstein, 1979). None of these capabilities automatically produced mass literacy. At the time of Gutenberg's invention, literacy across Western Europe stood at approximately 18 percent. Eighty years later, overall rates had risen only modestly, with urban centers reaching 10 to 30 percent while rural populations remained largely illiterate.
The Protestant Reformation generated the first mass demand for literacy. Martin Luther's German Bible translation (1522 to 1534) made the case that every Christian should read Scripture directly. The principle of sola scriptura required individual Bible reading, creating demand that had not previously existed. The demand alone could not overcome the economic barriers: for most pre-industrial families, the costs of sending children to school outweighed the benefits (Gawthrop & Strauss, 1984). By 1650, the Netherlands and England had achieved literacy rates above 50 percent while most of continental Europe remained between 15 and 25 percent. The gap reflected not religious enthusiasm but institutional investment: the Netherlands had established parish schools across virtually every community, while southern and eastern European states had not (Houston, 2002). Near-universal literacy arrived only through state-mandated public education, a process that required 270 to 440 years after the printing press: Prussia mandated compulsory education in 1763, England passed the Forster Education Act in 1870, France enacted the Jules Ferry laws in 1881 to 1882, and the United States achieved compulsory education across all states by 1918. The Statute of Anne (1710), the first modern copyright law, incentivized authorship and professionalized publishing. Standardized vernacular languages created the linguistic infrastructure mass literacy required. By approximately 1900, leading industrialized nations reached near-universal literacy, while southern European nations still lagged: Portugal reported 76 percent illiteracy as late as 1890 (Vincent, 2000; Cipolla, 1969). Globally, only 12 percent of the world's population was literate in 1820. The full arc from Gutenberg's press to near-universal literacy in industrialized nations spans approximately 460 years. This timeline reflects not the difficulty of learning to read but the institutional requirements for making literacy accessible: educational institutions, legal frameworks, standardized languages, professional publishing, and economic conditions that permitted families to send children to school rather than to work. The technology created the possibility. Institutions created the reality.
Learning to read is not merely acquiring a skill. It is a cognitive transformation. Brain regions evolved for visual object recognition are repurposed for letter and word recognition, creating the Visual Word Form Area in the left occipito-temporal cortex through what neuroscientists call "neuronal recycling" (Dehaene & Cohen, 2007). Functional MRI studies comparing illiterate adults, adults who acquired literacy as adults, and childhood-literate adults confirm that literacy enhances left fusiform activation, broadly strengthens visual processing, amplifies phonological activation for speech, and enables top-down activation of orthographic representations from spoken language (Dehaene, Pegado, Braga, et al., 2010). Both childhood and adult literacy produce these effects: education can "profoundly refine cortical organization" regardless of when it occurs (Dehaene, Cohen, Morais, & Kolinsky, 2015). The writing systems of Sumer, Egypt, China, and Greece produced different neural arrangements because the brain adapts to whatever coordination technology it encounters (Wolf, 2007). Each coordination technology reshapes the cognitive apparatus of the people who use it. The question is whether algorithmic coordination produces an analogous restructuring, and the variance in platform outcomes documented in Section 1 suggests it does.
Three foundational critiques warn against overstating what literacy alone accomplishes, and each strengthens rather than weakens the algorithmacy argument. Literacy is always embedded in social practices and power relations, never operating as a neutral technology that automatically produces cognitive benefits: the "autonomous model" of literacy as a universal engine of progress obscures the "ideological model" in which literacy practices are always shaped by the social structures within which they develop (Street, 1984). Literacy alone does not produce broad cognitive transformation: specific literacy practices enhance specific skills, but the sweeping cognitive changes attributed to literacy are associated with institutional context, particularly schooling, not with the ability to read and write per se (Scribner & Cole, 1981). The "literacy myth," the assumption that literacy automatically translates to economic success and social mobility, was historically deployed to control working-class populations and instill traits required for factory employment rather than to empower them (Graff, 1979). Each critique applies directly to algorithmacy. Algorithmacy is not a neutral skill. It is embedded in power relations that determine who develops it and who does not. Algorithmacy alone will not produce economic success without institutional support. Claims about algorithmacy's benefits must be evaluated against the historical pattern in which competency discourses serve the interests of those who already possess the competency. The critical tradition does not undermine the oracy-literacy-algorithmacy sequence. It specifies the conditions under which the sequence operates and warns against treating the competency as sufficient without the institutional infrastructure.
4.2 The institutional break
The institutional framework governing coordination in industrialized democracies from roughly 1945 to 1975 rested on a structural relationship between individuals and value that echoed, at institutional scale, the directness of the Sumerian token system. A worker held a job the way a farmer held a token: the relationship between the worker and economic security was relatively direct, mediated by stable organizations with interpretable rules. Permanent employment, hierarchical authority, credentialed expertise, and administrative procedures created a coordination environment legible to participants who possessed what might be called bureaucratic literacy: the competency to read institutional signals, complete forms, follow procedures, manage credentialing processes, and position oneself within hierarchical evaluation systems.
Charles Reich identified the structural transformation in 1964. Wealth in the administrative state no longer consisted primarily of tangible property, the kind one could hold, fence, and defend. It consisted of "government largess": licenses, contracts, subsidies, franchises, professional certifications, and welfare benefits (Reich, 1964). The shift was not merely from one kind of asset to another. It was a shift in the relationship between the holder and the value held. A farmer who owned land held value directly. A professional whose livelihood depended on a state-issued license held value through an administrative system that could grant, condition, or revoke access according to criteria the system itself determined. The license was not the value. The system that issued the license was the value's source, and navigating that system required understanding not the document but the institutional apparatus that produced it. The Supreme Court recognized this structural shift in Goldberg v. Kelly (1970), establishing that government benefits constituted a form of property that could not be terminated without due process. Reich's insight was that the shift from direct property to system-mediated largess changed what competency meant: it was no longer enough to read the document. One had to understand the system.
Platform capitalism completed an analogous shift from government largess to private largess. Where Reich identified the state as the mediator of economic access, platforms created a new form of mediation: access to economic opportunity determined by algorithmic systems that evaluate, rank, and distribute workers according to proprietary criteria (Brescia, 2021). A rideshare driver does not hold a job the way an employee holds a position. The driver holds access to a platform whose algorithms determine ride assignments, pricing, visibility, and continued participation. The platform is not a tool the driver uses. It is the system through which the driver's economic value is constituted. Where bureaucratic coordination required forms, credentials, and procedural knowledge, algorithmic coordination requires profile optimization, rating management, and strategic navigation of opaque recommendation systems. Where the institutional framework evaluated workers through human supervisors applying criteria the worker could read, platforms evaluate workers through computational processes applying criteria the worker cannot observe. The structural parallel is precise: just as navigating Reich's administrative state required understanding the system that issued the license rather than merely reading the license itself, navigating platform capitalism requires understanding the system that produces the score rather than merely reading the score.
The Bretton Woods system (1944 to 1971) created the institutional framework within which bureaucratic literacy operated, and its collapse illustrates the same structural transition at the level of monetary coordination. Bretton Woods established fixed exchange rates: currencies pegged to the dollar at fixed rates, the dollar pegged to gold at $35 per ounce. The relationship between representation and value was direct and legible: a dollar represented a specific quantity of gold, the way a Sumerian token represented a specific quantity of grain. The International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, and the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, combined with permitted capital controls and preserved national policy autonomy, created what one political economist calls "embedded liberalism": multilateral economic openness embedded within social and political norms that ensured domestic stability (Ruggie, 1982). Workers, firms, and governments could coordinate within this framework because the rules were stable, interpretable, and enforced by identifiable institutions. A dollar meant what it said. A career trajectory was legible. The literacy required to navigate the system was bureaucratic: read the documents, follow the procedures, understand the institutional logic.
The suspension of dollar-gold convertibility in August 1971 broke this direct relationship between representation and referent. The dollar no longer represented gold. It represented whatever the global currency market, a system of floating exchange rates determined by aggregate trading behavior, monetary policy decisions, speculative flows, and algorithmic trading systems, determined it represented at any given moment. The value of the dollar became system-constituted rather than referent-anchored. No individual participant could determine the dollar's value by examining the dollar itself. The value emerged from the system's operations, and navigating the system required understanding dynamics that no document could fully capture: interest rate differentials, trade balances, central bank signaling, market sentiment, and increasingly, the computational models that process these signals faster than any human participant. The neoliberal transformation that followed extended this logic from monetary systems to every domain of institutional coordination. Welfare provisions, labor relations, and state sovereignty were replaced by market-based governance whose outcomes emerged from system dynamics rather than administrative determination (Harvey, 2005). Capitalism abandoned hierarchical Fordist structures and developed a network-based form of organization after absorbing the "artistic critique" of May 1968, creating the cité par projets: a world organized through temporary projects, flexible networks, and perpetual adaptation rather than stable positions and long-term employment (Boltanski & Chiapello, 2005). The corporation transformed from a stable social institution into a nexus of contracts (Davis, 2009). The culture of the new capitalism demanded an ideal self oriented to the short term, focused on potential ability rather than accomplishment, and comfortable with perpetual retraining rather than cumulative expertise (Sennett, 2006).
Platform capitalism completed this institutional dissolution. Platforms did not enter pre-existing markets. They created and defined markets, positioning subjects within their logic and extracting value from the intermediary position (Srnicek, 2017). If Fordism was defined by national markets with national state regulation, and post-Fordism by transnational markets with national regulation, then digital capitalism is defined by digital proprietary markets owned and regulated by transnational private companies through algorithmic technology (Törnberg, 2023). Algocracy, governance by algorithm, constitutes a third mode of organizational governance distinct from both bureaucracy and markets, operating through computational processes that share no meta-language with the legal code of bureaucratic governance or the price signals of market coordination (Aneesh, 2009). The structural pattern across all three transitions, from tokens to cuneiform, from gold-backed dollars to fiat currency, from direct employment to platform-mediated work, follows the same logic: direct possession of value gives way to system-mediated access, and the competency required shifts from reading the representation to navigating the system that produces it. Algorithms now mediate coordination relationships that bureaucratic literacy once governed. Hiring decisions that once required reading job postings and navigating interview protocols now require optimizing profiles for proprietary applicant tracking systems. Pricing decisions that once required reading market signals now require interpreting algorithmic surge pricing and personalized pay rates that vary across workers performing identical tasks (Dubal, 2023). Reputation that once accumulated through institutional tenure now accumulates through rating systems whose aggregation methods, weighting schemes, and decay functions are opaque. In each case, the coordination relationship retains the same functional structure while the communication medium through which coordination occurs has shifted from bureaucratic documents to algorithmic processes. The competency required to navigate the new medium is as different from bureaucratic literacy as bureaucratic literacy was from oral negotiation.
Gilles Deleuze anticipated this structural transformation in a five-page essay that has become one of the most cited works in surveillance studies and critical digital theory. Foucault's disciplinary societies, Deleuze argued, operate through enclosures: family, school, barracks, factory, hospital, prison. Each institution molds individuals within bounded spaces using discrete, identifiable techniques of surveillance and normalization. These disciplinary institutions were "finished," replaced by control societies that operate not through discrete molds but through continuous modulation (Deleuze, 1992). Where disciplinary institutions confine, control societies pervade. Where the factory contained internal forces at a point of equilibrium, the corporation is "a spirit, a gas." Where discipline operates through signatures and numbers, fixed identifiers within institutions, control operates through codes: passwords that grant or deny access, algorithms that sort, rank, and route. Deleuze even mapped this onto money: discipline corresponds to minted currency backed by gold, fixed and discrete, while control corresponds to floating exchange rates, continuous and modulatory. The parallel to the Bretton Woods collapse is not incidental. Fixed exchange rates, fixed institutional structures, and fixed individual identities belonged to the same institutional formation. Floating rates, flexible networks, and decomposed data profiles belong to the formation that replaced it.
The concept that makes this concrete for the algorithmacy argument is the dividual. Deleuze's formulation: "Individuals have become 'dividuals,' and masses, samples, data, markets, or 'banks'" (Deleuze, 1992). The dividual is the individual decomposed into data fragments, numerical representations, behavioral traces, and computed scores. Disciplinary societies dealt with individuals as indivisible wholes confined within institutions. Control societies deal with dividuals as divisible data assemblages tracked across networks. The dividual is not a metaphor. It describes the literal operation of algorithmic systems. A platform driver is not evaluated as a whole person but as a collection of acceptance rates, completion rates, average ratings, cancellation frequencies, and GPS traces, each processed independently through different algorithmic subsystems. The "surveillant assemblage" abstracts human bodies from their territorial settings, separates them into discrete data flows, and reassembles them as virtual "data doubles" in different locations (Haggerty & Ericson, 2000). Algorithms construct identities through statistical analysis of these data fragments, inferring categories like gender, race, and consumer type upon users through patterns rather than self-expression, exercising what one researcher calls "soft biopolitics": governance through modulation rather than discipline (Cheney-Lippold, 2011). Algorithmic governmentality operates at the infra-individual level, on data fragments that are meaningless on their own, and at the supra-individual level, on aggregate profiles and statistical patterns, while bypassing the individual as a reflexive subject entirely (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). This is the deepest structural consequence of the shift from direct possession to system-mediated access. The system does not evaluate you. It evaluates your dividuated data fragments, and the competency required to navigate this condition cannot be captured by any form of literacy because literacy presupposes a legible document addressed to a whole reader. There is no document. There is a system. And the system addresses not you but your data double.
The compression of historical timelines makes this transition uniquely challenging. The full arc from Sumerian proto-writing to near-universal literacy in industrialized nations spans approximately 5,100 years. The Gutenberg-to-mass-literacy transition spans approximately 460 years. The Long 1968 institutional break occurred roughly 58 to 62 years ago, and the transition to algorithmic coordination is still in its early stages. Compared to the Gutenberg transition, the current shift compresses an analogous institutional transformation by a factor of 7 to 8. The institutional supports that made mass literacy possible developed over 270 to 440 years after the printing press. No comparable institutional frameworks for algorithmic competency yet exist despite the technology being deeply embedded in labor markets, consumer markets, and civic life. Three categories of acceleration, technological acceleration, acceleration of social change, and acceleration of the pace of life, create a self-reinforcing feedback loop in which each form of acceleration drives the others, producing what one theorist calls a "shrinking of the present": a decreasing period during which past experience reliably predicts future conditions (Rosa, 2013). The shrinking of the present is precisely the condition that makes formal training fail: instruction based on yesterday's algorithmic behavior becomes obsolete before it can be applied, because the algorithmic environment has already incorporated the aggregate behavioral effects of that instruction into its model. The Collingridge dilemma compounds the problem: "When change is easy, the need for it cannot be foreseen; when the need for change is apparent, change has become expensive, difficult and time-consuming" (Collingridge, 1980). Combined with Rosa's acceleration, the window for understanding algorithmic effects keeps shrinking while the pace of algorithmic change keeps increasing.
4.3 Grammatization as the mechanism
A single mechanism connects the oracy-literacy transition to the bureaucracy-algorithm transition. Bernard Stiegler names it grammatization: the process by which a continuous flux is divided into a system of discrete, reproducible formal elements (Stiegler, 1998). The term derives from linguist Sylvain Auroux's observation that the alphabet grammatized speech by decomposing continuous vocal streams into discrete letter-units. Stiegler generalizes the concept across the full historical sequence. Writing grammatized speech: continuous oral discourse became a system of discrete letters, words, and syntactic structures that could be stored, copied, and manipulated independently of the speaker. Industrial machinery grammatized gesture: continuous skilled movement became a system of discrete mechanical operations that could be specified on Jacquard punch cards, standardized, and performed by machines regardless of the operator's craft knowledge. Algorithms grammatize coordination: continuous processes of negotiation, trust-building, reputation assessment, and skill evaluation become discrete computable units, scores, ratings, rankings, and behavioral profiles, that can be processed, compared, and acted upon by computational systems regardless of the participants' tacit understanding (Tinnell, 2015).
Each grammaticalization produces both capability and loss. Stiegler's pharmakon concept: every grammaticalization is simultaneously remedy and poison, and outcomes depend on political organization, not technological destiny. Writing enabled administration at scales oral memory could not sustain. It also externalized memory, creating dependence on external records and eroding the mnemonic capacities that oral cultures cultivated. Industrial machinery enabled production at speeds and volumes that manual labor could not achieve. It also externalized skill, creating dependence on machine systems and eroding the craft knowledge that artisanal production required. Stiegler formalizes these losses as three successive proletarianizations: the loss of savoir-faire, workers' practical knowledge externalized in industrial machines; the loss of savoir-vivre, consumers' knowledge of living well captured by marketing and cultural industries; and the loss of theoretical knowledge, cognitive workers' knowledge externalized in digital technologies (Stiegler, 2010). Each proletarianization creates a population that depends on externalized systems it cannot fully understand or control. Digital tertiary retention produces a structural condition in which the automation of cognition constitutes a new stage of proletarianization affecting even the capacity for theoretical knowledge itself (Stiegler, 2016).
Platform algorithms extend this sequence by discretizing continuous social processes into computable units. A driver's accumulated navigational knowledge becomes a rating score. A freelancer's professional reputation becomes a search ranking. A content creator's audience relationship becomes an engagement metric. A job applicant's qualifications become a keyword-matching score. In each case, the algorithm captures selected dimensions of a continuous social process, discards the rest, and produces a discrete formal representation that determines coordination outcomes. The representation is not neutral. It encodes the platform's optimization objectives, reflects the historical biases of the training data, and reshapes the social process it claims to measure (Beer, 2017; Bucher, 2018). This is the dividual in operation: algorithms do not process whole individuals but dividuated data elements, fragments of behavior decomposed according to the platform's grammatization schema. Algorithmic governmentality governs at the infra-individual level, on data fragments that are meaningless on their own, and the supra-individual level, on statistical models and behavioral profiles, while circumventing the individual as a conscious, reflexive agent (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). This is why Stiegler's third proletarianization takes a form absent from prior stages: the system grammatizes not only what people do but what they are predicted to do, and Stiegler himself describes this condition as "the zero degree of writing" in dialogue with Rouvroy (Rouvroy & Stiegler, 2016).
The recursive condition distinguishes algorithmic grammatization from all prior forms. Writing grammatized speech but did not change in response to how people read. The cuneiform tablet recorded a transaction, and the record remained stable regardless of what the farmer did next. Industrial machinery grammatized gesture but did not adapt to individual workers' movement patterns in real time. The Jacquard loom reproduced the same pattern whether the operator was a master weaver or a child apprentice. Algorithmic systems grammatize coordination and simultaneously adapt to the coordination they grammatize. The system being learned is also learning the user, creating a recursive loop in which the formal representation and the social process it represents co-evolve (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). Four consequential aspects of learning algorithms produce this recursive condition: black-boxed performance, where outcomes resist human-interpretable explanation; comprehensive digitization, where all interactions become data inputs; anticipatory quantification, where algorithms produce predictions that shape the behavior they subsequently measure; and hidden politics, where social assumptions are inscribed in software without public scrutiny. Anticipatory quantification is the mechanism that makes the recursion operational: the algorithm does not merely record past behavior (like a cuneiform tablet) but predicts future behavior and adjusts its operations based on those predictions, changing the environment the user encounters and therefore changing the behavior the algorithm will record next.
Yuk Hui, extending Stiegler, formalizes this recursive property as tertiary protention. Stiegler's concept of tertiary retention names the externalization of memory in technical objects: cave paintings, writing, film, databases all store the past. Hui proposes that algorithmic systems externalize not just memory but anticipation: recommendation engines anticipate desire before it is articulated, smart objects predict need before it is felt, hiring algorithms rank candidates before they apply (Hui, 2018, 2019). Algorithms grammatize not just memory but futurity. The discretization becomes opaque and potentially irreversible when the algorithmic "grammar" is hidden from those whose behavior it discretizes (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). Recursivity in Hui's formulation is "not mere repetition, but rather like a spiral: it repeats and deviates from itself every time when it calls itself." Each cycle of algorithmic grammatization produces a new condition for the next cycle, which is why the system never stabilizes in a form that static knowledge could capture.
The compensating competency follows the same structural pattern across all three transitions. Writing's loss of mnemonic capacity was compensated by literacy: the competency to encode and decode written symbols, compose and interpret documents, and navigate the institutional architectures, libraries, archives, schools, that written records made possible. Industrial proletarianization's loss of craft knowledge was compensated by technical education: the competency to operate, maintain, and improve machine systems, navigate industrial labor markets, and participate in the institutional architectures, unions, professional associations, regulatory bodies, that industrial production required. The pattern is structural: capability and loss are coupled, and the compensating competency emerges from engagement with the grammatized environment rather than from instruction about the grammatization process.
The most recent comprehensive review of the orality-literacy-digitality relationship confirms that the current moment requires comparative historical analysis rather than simple analogy (Emigh, 2024). The transition is neither a return to oral conditions, as the "secondary orality" thesis proposes, nor a straightforward extension of literacy to new media, as the "digital literacy" thesis assumes. It is a structurally novel transition that shares formal properties with prior transitions, the grammaticalization mechanism, the elite-to-mass competency trajectory, and the institutional lag between technological capability and social adaptation, while introducing the recursive property that distinguishes algorithmic coordination from every prior form. Algorithmic proletarianization's loss of coordination knowledge demands a corresponding competency: the capacity to navigate opaque algorithmic intermediaries, build working models of systems that resist direct observation, express intentions through constrained digital channels, and participate in coordination architectures that no existing institutional framework supports. This is the shift the entire historical sequence predicts. When the Sumerian farmer could no longer hold the token that represented his grain, he needed scribal literacy. When the Bretton Woods dollar could no longer be exchanged for a fixed quantity of gold, participants needed financial system literacy. When the platform worker can no longer read the criteria by which the algorithm evaluates performance, a new competency is required. It is no longer enough to read the document. One must navigate the system. This competency has a name. The next section defines it.
5. Algorithmacy
5.1 Algorithmacy defined
Oracy refers to the competency of coordinating through speech. Andrew Wilkinson coined the term in 1965 by analogy with literacy and numeracy, arguing that spoken communication required its own pedagogical framework because the cognitive operations involved in effective oral coordination differ from those involved in reading and writing (Wilkinson, Davies, & Atkinson, 1965). Literacy refers to the competency of coordinating through written texts. The cognitive operations writing enables, including abstraction, analytic classification, decontextualized reasoning, and systematic comparison across categories, are genuinely different from those oral communication supports, not merely faster or more portable versions of the same operations (Goody, 1977; Ong, 1982; Dehaene, Cohen, Morais, & Kolinsky, 2015). Each term in the sequence names a competency, not a condition. The suffix denotes a capacity for effective action within a specific communication architecture: oracy within oral coordination, literacy within textual coordination. Algorithmacy names the competency for coordinating through an opaque algorithmic intermediary.
The definition requires precision because neighboring constructs occupy adjacent conceptual territory. A new construct is warranted only when it occupies unique conceptual space along the dimensions of definition, scope conditions, semantic relationships, and logical coherence (Suddaby, 2010). A concept that merely relabels an existing construct commits the “jangle fallacy,” using a new name for a phenomenon already captured (Kelley, 1927). Six existing constructs name aspects of the algorithmic phenomenon. Algorithmacy differs from each of them because it addresses a dimension none of them captures: the communicative competency for navigating triadic coordination structures in which an opaque algorithmic intermediary transforms both parties’ inputs in accordance with optimization objectives that neither controls.
Algorithmic literacy measures declarative knowledge about algorithms: awareness that algorithms curate online experience and factual understanding of how algorithmic processes operate (Dogruel, Masur, & Joeckel, 2022). The construct captures a real dimension. The dimension is insufficient. Higher algorithmic knowledge does not predict more effective behavioral engagement with algorithmic systems (Swart, 2021). Knowing the grammar of a language does not make one an effective communicator in that language. Algorithmic literacy is to algorithmacy as grammar is to fluency: a necessary component that does not constitute the whole. Algorithmic competency, the closest existing construct, measures four dimensions specific to platform workers: understanding, embracing, leveraging, and remediating algorithmic management, validated across five samples of Chinese on-demand laborers (Zhou, Lei, Liu, Huang, & Hou, 2025). The construct addresses gig worker adaptation to algorithmic management through a human resource management lens. It does not ground competency in the triadic communication structure that produces the variance it measures, limiting its explanatory reach to contexts where algorithmic management is the explicit organizational form. AI literacy defines seventeen competencies for critically evaluating, communicating with, and using AI technologies (Long & Magerko, 2020). The competencies are real, but they address a different relationship: individual engagement with AI systems rather than coordination among distributed human actors via algorithmic intermediaries. Electracy names the shift in the civilizational apparatus from literacy to electronic media (Ulmer, 2003). The concept operates at the level of historical periodization, not at the level of individual competency, and its scholarly community has not engaged with algorithmic platform studies. Algocracy names governance by algorithm (Aneesh, 2009). Algorithmic governmentality names a form of power that bypasses the conscious subject through datafication and behavioral modulation (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013). Both diagnose the governance structure without specifying the navigational capacity individuals develop in response to it. The relationship between these governance concepts and algorithmacy parallels the relationship between bureaucracy and literacy: bureaucracy names the coordination architecture, literacy names the competency required to operate within it. Algocracy names the coordination architecture; algorithmacy names the competency.
Algorithmacy is a communication competency, not a knowledge state, a personality trait, or a technical skill. The distinction matters because competency integrates knowledge, skill, and situated practice in ways that knowledge alone cannot (Campion, Fink, Ruggeberg, Carr, Phillips, & Odman, 2011). The communication competence tradition in CMC research established that mediated communication requires its own competency framework integrating motivation, knowledge, and skills (Spitzberg, 2006). Recent extensions incorporate AI, robotics, and algorithmic systems into technology-mediated communication competence, positioning algorithmic navigation as a communicative challenge rather than a purely technical one (Koponen, Turunen, Laajalahti, Julkunen, & Spitzberg, 2025). Algorithmacy extends this tradition by specifying the structural features of the coordination environment that make algorithmic communication qualitatively different from prior forms of mediated communication: the intermediary is not a passive channel but an active third party, the coordination structure is triadic rather than dyadic, and the intermediary’s optimization objectives are independent of both communicating parties.
The construct is multidimensional. At this stage of theoretical development, the paper specifies the structural features that define the construct’s domain rather than proposing a validated dimensional model, following the principle that concept validity must precede construct validity (Locke, 2012). The dimensional structure appropriate for empirical operationalization depends on whether algorithmacy is best modeled as a latent construct (a deeper competency manifesting through observable dimensions), an aggregate construct (a composite formed from its dimensions), or a profile construct (a configuration of dimensional characteristics) (Law, Wong, & Mobley, 1998). That determination requires the empirical work proposed in the research agenda. The theoretical analysis can specify the structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination that any valid operationalization must capture.
5.2 Algorithmacy as the mechanism producing differential sensemaking
The divergent sensemaking trajectories documented by Möhlmann, Salge, and Marabelli (2023) reflect the differential development of algorithmacy. Workers who achieve better coordination outcomes recognize and compensate for three structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination. These features are not platform-specific design choices. They are structural constraints that appear wherever an opaque algorithmic intermediary mediates between distributed actors. They derive from the triadic architecture established in Section 3: the algorithmic third occupies the position of a constitutive rule system that transforms inputs according to its own logic, and the three features name the specific communicative consequences of that structural position.
Signal asymmetry describes the condition in which the algorithm interprets a worker’s actions through models that the worker cannot observe, assigning meanings that diverge from the worker’s intentions. A driver who declines three consecutive ride requests may intend to wait for a higher-value trip. The algorithm may classify the pattern as unreliable, thereby reducing future assignment quality. The worker and the intermediary read the same action through different interpretive frameworks, and the worker cannot access the intermediary’s framework to correct the misalignment. This condition is what one research team calls “broken loop learning”: the algorithm records all of the human’s task actions but remains structurally ignorant of the human’s cognitive processes, motivations, and strategic reasoning (Tarafdar, Page, & Marabelli, 2022). The concept of legibility captures the same dynamic at the institutional scale: coordination systems simplify complex human activity to make it administratively readable, destroying the practical, local, and tacit knowledge that resists formal codification (Scott, 1998). Algorithmic systems perform this simplification computationally, at speeds and scales that make human correction impossible in real time. Workers who develop algorithmacy recognize that signal asymmetry is a structural condition, not an error to be resolved. They adjust their behavior to account for the gap between what they intend and what the algorithm reads, developing what ethnographic research describes as “anticipatory compliance”: preemptive behavioral adjustment based on working models of how the algorithm will interpret their actions (Bucher, Schou, & Waldkirch, 2021).
Intent compression describes the condition in which rich human intentions must be translated into narrow platform-legible inputs, with information lost at every stage of translation. A freelancer seeking “interesting projects that develop my skills and lead to long-term client relationships” must distill this intention into a profile comprising keywords, skill tags, hourly rates, and portfolio samples. The platform captures selected dimensions of the freelancer’s intent and discards the rest. The compression is always lossy: the platform’s formal representation cannot fully capture the meaning the worker intends to express. Workers navigating opaque algorithmic evaluation develop strategies to compress behavior into forms they hope will satisfy unknown criteria, experiencing what one ethnographer documents as the “invisible cage” of working within an evaluative system whose standards remain unpredictable (Rahman, 2021). Algorithms define “relevance” by creating representations that are “imperfect but sufficient” for computational processing, producing what one researcher calls “algorithmic identities,” simplified computational models of complex human actors (Gillespie, 2014). Workers who develop algorithmacy recognize that compression is structural, not incidental. They learn to optimize the dimensions the platform captures while pursuing goals through channels the platform does not monitor, a dual-track strategy that experienced workers on every studied platform develop independently.
Opaque mediation describes the condition in which the algorithmic intermediary breaks the feedback loop between a worker’s action and its coordination outcome. In direct communication, a speaker can observe the listener’s response and adjust. In bureaucratic coordination, a worker can read the evaluation criteria and calibrate performance. In algorithmic coordination, the intermediary processes inputs through computational logic that is opaque for three distinct reasons: corporate secrecy (platforms protect proprietary systems), technical complexity (machine learning models resist human-interpretable explanation), and intrinsic opacity (the mathematical operations that produce outputs do not map onto human-comprehensible causal reasoning) (Burrell, 2016). Even nominally transparent systems do not restore the feedback loop because algorithmic systems “do not contain complexity but enact complexity,” producing emergent behaviors that resist explanation even when individual components are visible (Ananny & Crawford, 2018). Workers who develop algorithmacy do not overcome opacity. They build working models that are productive despite being incomplete, treating the algorithm as an environment to be navigated through structured experimentation rather than a system to be fully understood through declarative knowledge. This is why formal training fails: training provides declarative knowledge about how algorithms work, but the competency that produces better outcomes is the procedural capacity to navigate systems whose workings cannot be fully known.
The three features are not independent dimensions to be measured separately. They are structural consequences of a single architectural fact: the algorithmic intermediary occupies the position of a constitutive third party with autonomous interests and opaque operations. Signal asymmetry arises because the third party interprets inputs through its own models. Intent compression arises because the third party accepts only formally specified inputs. Opaque mediation arises because the third party processes inputs through logic it does not share. Any coordination environment in which an opaque algorithmic intermediary mediates between distributed actors will exhibit all three features, regardless of platform type, industry, or regulatory context. This structural generality is what distinguishes algorithmacy from platform-specific skills: the competency addresses features shared across all triadic algorithmic coordination rather than the particular interface of any single platform.
The advantages algorithmacy confers compound through informational feedback. Workers who recognize signal asymmetry produce inputs that generate more favorable algorithmic responses. More favorable responses produce richer data about how the algorithm interprets different inputs. Richer data accelerates the development of more accurate working models. More accurate models produce better inputs. The loop is multiplicative rather than additive: each cycle amplifies the advantage gained in the previous cycle. Workers who fail to recognize signal asymmetry produce inputs that elicit unfavorable responses, yielding impoverished data for algorithmic interpretation, stalling model development, and perpetuating the cycle of unfavorable inputs. The compounding mechanism explains why platform outcome distributions follow power-law shapes rather than normal distributions: small initial differences in algorithmacy amplify through feedback into large outcome differences, producing the extreme inequality documented across ride-hailing, freelance labor, content creation, and e-commerce (Cook, Diamond, Hall, List, & Oyer, 2021; Schor, Attwood-Charles, Cansoy, Ladegaard, & Wengronowitz, 2020). Experimental evidence confirms the mechanism: when participants in an artificial cultural market can observe others’ choices, feedback loops transform micro-variations in initial popularity into macro-divergence in final outcomes, with inequality increasing and success becoming unpredictable (Salganik, Dodds, & Watts, 2006). Platforms with stronger recommendation algorithms exhibit stronger power-law earnings distributions and lower median earnings, directly linking algorithmic mediation strength to the magnitude of inequality (Strauss, Yang, & Mazzucato, 2025). The informational feedback loop is the mechanism through which differential algorithmacy produces the variance phenomenon documented in Section 1.
5.3 Algorithmacy and co-optation
Co-optation is the fourth coordination mechanism alongside markets, hierarchies, and networks (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Philip Selznick defined co-optation as the process of incorporating new elements into an organization’s leadership or policy-determining structure to avert threats to its stability (Selznick, 1949). Platforms co-opt autonomous actors, enrolling providers and users in the practices of algorithmic management without delegating managerial authority, extracting value from both sides while maintaining the appearance of independent entrepreneurship (Stark & Pais, 2020). The mechanism operates through what resource dependence theory identifies as the management of environmental uncertainty: organizations co-opt external elements they depend on but cannot directly control (Pfeffer & Salancik, 1978). Platforms depend on workers’ labor, vehicles, homes, and creative output without owning any of these assets. Co-optation resolves this dependence by enrolling asset-holders into algorithmically mediated participation where the platform extracts data value alongside service value (van Doorn & Badger, 2020).
Co-optation explains enrollment. Algorithmacy explains what develops after enrollment. The two concepts occupy different levels of analysis within the same theoretical architecture. Co-optation refers to the coordination mechanism by which platforms organize distributed actors. Algorithmacy names the competency that actors develop through participation in the coordination mechanism. The relationship parallels prior transitions. Oracy developed through immersion in oral communities: children acquired spoken communication competency by participating in a coordinated environment structured by face-to-face interaction, shared physical context, and immediate feedback. Literacy developed through formal instruction in educational institutions: students acquired written communication competency through structured pedagogy within an institutional coordination environment designed for the purpose. Algorithmacy develops through co-optation: workers acquire algorithmic communication competency by participating in coordination environments whose intermediaries are active, adaptive, and optimize for objectives independent of the worker’s goals. The acquisition mode differs from both immersion and instruction because the coordination environment itself changes in response to the competency being developed.
The recursive condition is the structural signature distinguishing algorithmacy from prior competencies. Writing constituted literate subjects, but writing did not change in response to how people read. Broadcast media constituted audiences, but broadcast schedules did not adapt to individual viewing behavior. Platform algorithms constitute subjects and adapt to the subjects they constitute (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). Human-AI coevolution is formally defined as a condition in which humans and AI algorithms continuously influence each other through potentially endless feedback loops in which each party’s behavior modifies the other’s future behavior (Pedreschi et al., 2023). The recursive condition explains two features of the variance phenomenon. It explains why formal instruction fails: instruction teaches declarative knowledge about a system, but the system changes in response to the behavior that declarative knowledge produces, invalidating the knowledge before accumulated experience can refine it into procedural competency. The temporal mismatch between the instruction’s static knowledge and the algorithm’s dynamic adaptation means that by the time a worker applies what the training taught, the algorithm has already incorporated the aggregate behavioral effects of that training into its model, changing the very patterns the training described. It explains why sensemaking quality compounds: workers who develop richer working models generate inputs that elicit richer algorithmic feedback, which in turn refines their models, which generate even richer inputs. The recursive loop means algorithmacy is not a stock to be accumulated but a flow to be maintained: a continuous process of model-building, testing, updating, and rebuilding as the coordination environment evolves in response to the collective behavior of all participants.
The construct resolves the gap identified in Section 1. Möhlmann’s algorithm sensemaking framework correctly identifies the cognitive process through which workers interpret algorithmic management. It does not explain why sensemaking quality varies so systematically across workers who face identical systems. Algorithmacy provides the mechanism: workers differ in their capacity to recognize signal asymmetry, compensate for intent compression, and build working models despite opaque mediation. These capacities develop through participation in co-optive coordination environments and compound through informational feedback loops. The variance in sensemaking quality is not random. It reflects the differential development of a communication competency whose structural features are shared across every platform domain studied, whose acquisition mode is co-optive rather than instructional, and whose advantages compound through the recursive relationship between the worker’s developing competency and the algorithm’s adaptive response.
6. The constitutive claim
6.1 Platforms constitute subjects
Platforms do not find pre-existing worker types and sort them into categories. They produce worker types through algorithmic architectures that define what a worker can become, what counts as performance, and what coordination outcomes are possible. The distinction between sorting and producing is the distinction between a census and a constitution. A census counts pre-existing categories. A constitution creates the categories within which citizens, rights, and obligations come into existence. Platform algorithms operate as constitutions rather than censuses.
The philosophical foundation for this claim is well established. Categories change the people they classify (Hacking, 1986). When institutions create new classifications, “looping effects” emerge: the classified individuals learn about the classification, alter their behavior in response, and the classification system updates to accommodate the altered behavior, producing a recursive cycle in which categories and categorized co-evolve (Hacking, 1995). Classification systems function as infrastructure, actively producing the categories they claim to describe and generating “torque” between imposed categories and lived experience (Bowker & Star, 1999). Statistical categories constitute the populations they claim to measure: “poverty” and “unemployment” are products of measurement practice, simultaneously real and constructed (Desrosières, 1998). Credit scoring produces “classification situations” that determine life chances, creating new social categories and stratification mechanisms that did not exist before the scoring system (Fourcade & Healy, 2013). These are not abstract philosophical observations. They describe the concrete mechanism through which algorithmic systems produce the subjects they govern.
Platform algorithms perform this constitutive function at computational speed and planetary scale. Algorithmic psychometrics produce the “scalable subject,” a quantifiable psychological position translated through big data into a model subject amenable to computational classification (Stark, 2018). The scalable subject is not a pre-existing entity discovered by measurement. It is a subject position produced by the classificatory apparatus. A “new algorithmic identity” emerges through statistical analysis rather than self-expression: algorithms infer categories such as gender, race, and consumer type about users, creating identity positions the users did not author and may not recognize (Cheney-Lippold, 2011). Rankings do not passively measure the quality of the institutions they rank. They reconstitute those institutions through self-fulfilling prophecy, reactive behavioral change, and commensuration, aligning ranked entities with the ranking criteria (Espeland & Sauder, 2007). The same mechanism operates when platform algorithms rank workers: the ranking determines what counts as a good worker, and workers reshape their behavior to match the criteria the ranking rewards, which changes the population the algorithm encounters, which in turn changes the ranking model.
Empirical research across platform contexts confirms the constitutive pattern. A seven-year ethnography of ride-hailing drivers documents how algorithmic management manufactures consent through “constant and confined choices” that produce the experience of skillful agency within structurally constrained options (Cameron, 2024). The “good bad job” is not a pre-existing condition workers discover. It is a subject position the algorithmic architecture produces through the interaction of narrow choice sets, gamified incentive structures, and variable-ratio reward schedules that keep workers engaged despite objectively precarious conditions. The mechanism parallels what an earlier generation of labor process theory identified in factories: workplace structures produce subjects who consent to their own exploitation not through coercion but through the subjective experience of autonomy within designed constraint (Burawoy, 1979). Algorithmic management extends this constitutive logic by removing the human supervisor from the loop, replacing interpersonal authority with continuous, individualized computational classification that operates without the reciprocal recognition a human manager provides.
Three worker typologies that appear to describe pre-existing personality types are better understood as platform-produced subject positions. “Success Stories,” “Strugglers,” and “Strivers” emerge from the interaction between prior capital and algorithmic navigation capacity, not from stable individual characteristics that precede platform participation (Ravenelle, 2019). Workers trapped in opaque algorithmic evaluation develop divergent reactivity paths, with some experimenting and adapting while others constrain activity or exit, producing trajectories that look like personality differences but trace to differential exposure to algorithmic feedback (Rahman, 2021). Identical algorithmic technologies occasion different organizational structures by altering institutionalized roles and interaction patterns, producing different configurations of professional identity and coordination practice from the same technical system (Barley, 1986). The variance is not in the workers. It is in the constitutive process through which algorithmic architectures produce different kinds of workers from similar starting conditions.
The constitutive claim extends beyond labor platforms. Recommendation algorithms do not detect pre-existing preferences and return them. They produce engagement patterns through selective exposure, creating the taste structures they claim to measure (Seaver, 2019). Four “algorithmic logics” of taste construction on streaming platforms, including datafication, reconfiguration, interpellation, and reproduction, demonstrate how recommendation systems constitute cultural preferences rather than reflecting them (Gaw, 2021). AI writing assistants do not merely help users express pre-existing opinions. They shift users’ attitudes through co-writing, creating new attitudinal positions that persist after the interaction ends (Jakesch, Bhat, Buschek, Zalmanson, & Naaman, 2023). Hiring algorithms do not screen for pre-existing qualifications. They constitute what counts as a qualified candidate through the features they weigh and the historical patterns their training data encodes. In each case, the algorithm preexists the interaction, defines the subject positions available to participants, and produces rather than merely constrains the coordination outcomes.
Performativity theory provides the theoretical architecture for this claim. Financial models brought markets into alignment with their predictions rather than merely describing pre-existing market dynamics: the Black-Scholes option pricing model succeeded empirically not because it discovered prior price patterns but because markets changed in ways that made its assumptions more accurate (MacKenzie & Millo, 2003). Economics does not describe a pre-existing economy. It formats economic reality through models, devices, and calculative practices that constitute the phenomena they claim to represent (Callon, 1998). Five distinct uses of “performativity” operate in organizational and management theory: Austinian (speech acts that do things), Lyotardian (efficiency as self-legitimating), Butlerian (identity through repetition), Callonian (theories shaping markets), and Baradian (material-discursive intra-action) (Gond, Cabantous, Harding, & Learmonth, 2016). Platform algorithms exercise Callonian performativity: they model coordination patterns and, through their operation, bring those patterns into existence. Algorithms take on a “constitutive or performative role in ordering the world,” circulating and imposing particular forms of knowing that reshape rationalities and worldviews (Beer, 2017). “Programmed sociality” names the condition in which algorithms create the conditions for sociality and connectivity rather than merely facilitating them (Bucher, 2018). The algorithm produces the social world within which coordination occurs, rather than coordinating within a pre-existing one.
The Lacanian vocabulary of the Symbolic Order captures the structural position algorithms occupy. The Symbolic Order names the system of rules, categories, and positions that preexists individual subjects and constitutes them as particular kinds of actors within a social field. The subject does not enter the Symbolic Order as a pre-formed agent. The Symbolic Order produces the subject through the process of positioning within its categories. One passage written in 1999 anticipates the algorithmic condition with precision: individuals are “interpellated without even being aware of it: our identity is constituted for the big Other by a series of digitalized informational files… files we are mostly not even aware of” (Žižek, 1999, p. 259). The algorithmic platform occupies this structural position. It categorizes workers through data traces without articulating its logic through discourse, functioning as the “algorithmic big Other” that subjects workers to a symbolic order they cannot directly interrogate (Salter & Dutta, 2023). The algorithm exercises authority not by commanding compliance but by defining the categories within which compliance and resistance both occur. A driver who games Uber’s surge algorithm operates within the algorithm’s symbolic order just as much as a driver who follows every prompt. Both are positioned as subjects within a rule system that defines what gaming and compliance mean.
6.2 The variance phenomenon resolved
The constitutive claim resolves the variance phenomenon documented in Section 1. Identical platforms produce different outcomes, not because workers bring different pre-existing capacities to the same system, but because the constitutive process itself operates differentially. Co-optation enrolls workers into algorithmically mediated participation (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). The algorithmic architecture constitutes workers as particular kinds of platform subjects through classification, scoring, and feedback. Algorithmacy names the competency gradient along which this constitutive process produces differential coordination effectiveness. Workers who develop higher algorithmacy recognize the three structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination, signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation, and build working models that enable more effective navigation despite the constitutive constraints.
The resolution connects three theoretical levels through a mechanism-based explanation. Social mechanisms link macro conditions to individual actions (situational mechanisms), individual actions to other individual actions (action-formation mechanisms), and individual actions to macro outcomes (transformational mechanisms) (Coleman, 1990; Hedström & Swedberg, 1998). The dissertation’s three-level architecture maps onto this framework. The situational mechanism is co-optation: the platform’s algorithmic architecture creates the conditions under which workers must develop navigational competency. The action-formation mechanism is algorithm sensemaking: workers interpret algorithmic cues through focused enactment, selection modes, and retention sources (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023), developing differential algorithmacy through this interpretive process. The transformational mechanism is informational compounding: differential algorithmacy produces differential data quality, which, in turn, produces differential model refinement, yielding the power-law outcome distributions observed at the aggregate level.
The transformational mechanism requires elaboration because it explains why small initial differences in sensemaking quality yield large aggregate outcomes. Groups with similar average characteristics can generate radically different collective results depending on the distribution of individual variation rather than the mean (Granovetter, 1978). Mild individual preferences produce extreme aggregate patterns through tipping dynamics (Schelling, 1971). When participants in an artificial cultural market can observe others’ choices, feedback loops transform micro-variations in initial popularity into macro-divergence in final outcomes, with inequality increasing and success becoming unpredictable (Salganik, Dodds, & Watts, 2006). The number of competitors increases the contribution of variance to competitive advantage: in competitive environments, what matters is not the average competency but the distribution of competency across the population (March, 1991). Applied to algorithmacy: two platforms with identical mean user competency produce radically different aggregate outcomes if the variance in algorithmacy differs, because compounding amplifies the tails of the distribution rather than the center.
The compounding mechanism is informational rather than merely financial. Workers who recognize signal asymmetry produce inputs that generate more favorable algorithmic responses. More favorable responses provide richer data about how the algorithm interprets different inputs. Richer data accelerates the development of more accurate working models. More accurate models produce still better inputs. The loop operates through cumulative advantage: a favorable position becomes a resource producing further favorable positions, generating the right-skewed distributions that characterize every platform domain studied (DiPrete & Eirich, 2006; Merton, 1968). Platforms with stronger recommendation algorithms exhibit stronger power-law earnings distributions and lower median earnings, directly linking the strength of algorithmic mediation to the magnitude of outcome inequality (Strauss, Yang, & Mazzucato, 2025). The mechanism is not exploitation in the traditional sense. It is constitutive differentiation: the algorithmic architecture produces different subjects through the same process, and the differences compound through informational feedback into the systematic variance that the existing literature documents but does not explain.
The resolution integrates three bodies of scholarship that have developed independently. Möhlmann’s algorithm sensemaking framework correctly identifies the cognitive process through which workers interpret algorithmic management. Stark and Vanden Broeck’s co-optation theory correctly identifies the mechanism by which platforms coordinate distributed actors. The variance literature correctly documents that identical platforms produce non-identical outcomes. Algorithmacy connects these three contributions by naming the competency that develops through sensemaking within co-optive structures and explaining why that competency varies systematically across equally co-opted participants. Algorithm sensemaking is the cognitive process. Co-optation is the coordination mechanism that creates the conditions for sensemaking. Algorithmacy is the competency that develops through sensemaking within co-optive structures. The constitutive claim grounds all three: the algorithmic architecture does not merely provide the environment for sensemaking but produces the subjects who make sense, the categories through which sensemaking occurs, and the feedback structures through which differential sensemaking compounds into differential outcomes.
When robotic surgery systems altered hospital work structures, approved learning pathways failed, forcing surgical trainees into informal “shadow learning” practices invisible to the organization (Beane, 2019). The same dynamic operates across algorithmic platforms. Formal training provides declarative knowledge about how algorithms work, but the competency that produces better outcomes develops through shadow practices: experiential navigation, peer-network learning, structured experimentation, and folk theorization that formal systems neither recognize nor support. The constitutive claim explains why: the algorithm constitutes the learning environment, and the learning environment adapts in response to the learning it produces. Instruction based on yesterday’s algorithmic behavior becomes obsolete because the algorithm has already incorporated the aggregate effects of that instruction into its model. The competency that matters is not knowledge of how the algorithm worked but the capacity to build and rebuild working models as the algorithm evolves. Algorithmacy names that capacity. The next section proposes the questions this framework opens for future research.
7. Questions for future research
This paper introduces algorithmacy as a communication competency for navigating triadic algorithmic coordination, identifies three structural features that define the coordination environment, and proposes a mechanism connecting differential competency development to the variance phenomenon documented across platform domains. The paper opens a research program. It does not deliver a finished theoretical system. Four questions define the program’s priorities.
7.1 What are the structural properties of algorithmacy, and how should they be operationalized?
The paper identifies three structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination: signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation. It does not specify the cognitive operations workers perform when navigating these features, the dimensional structure relating those operations to the overall construct, or the measurement instruments through which the construct should be assessed. These are empirical questions requiring empirical answers.
The first measurement decision concerns the construct’s dimensional architecture. Signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation may function as reflective indicators of a latent competency, with each dimension manifesting the same underlying capacity in different communicative contexts. They may function as formative components of an aggregate construct, with each contributing unique variance that collectively constitutes algorithmacy. They may function as profile dimensions, with different configurations producing qualitatively different competency types (Law, Wong, & Mobley, 1998). The distinction is consequential: latent models predict correlated dimensions, aggregate models permit uncorrelated dimensions, and profile models predict typological clusters. Empirical research should test all three specifications against fit criteria and nomological network predictions before committing to one (Edwards, 2001; MacKenzie, Podsakoff, & Podsakoff, 2011).
The second decision concerns the measurement method. Self-report instruments capture declarative knowledge and subjective experience but systematically miss tacit, procedural, and socially embedded competencies (Hargittai, Gruber, Djukaric, Fuchs, & Brombach, 2020). Performance-based measures capture behavioral outcomes but confound competency with environmental conditions. Experience sampling methods can track algorithmacy’s dynamic, situated character by combining real-time self-reports of algorithmic sensemaking with simultaneously collected platform trace data, capturing how competency fluctuates across tasks, shifts, and platform updates (Beal, 2015; Gabriel et al., 2019). Behavioral experiments using controlled algorithmic environments can isolate specific dimensions by manipulating signal asymmetry, intent compression, or opacity while holding other features constant, following the probe methodology that revealed 62% of Facebook users were unaware of algorithmic curation (Eslami et al., 2015). Qualitative methods, including diary studies, think-aloud protocols, and the “rich pictures” technique, in which participants graphically represent their understanding of algorithmic systems, can externalize tacit algorithmacy that verbal self-report cannot capture (Siles, Segura-Castillo, Solís, & Sancho, 2020). The most productive approach will likely combine platform trace data with experience sampling and behavioral probes, triangulating across methods to capture a competency that is simultaneously cognitive, behavioral, and socially situated.
Two existing scales provide starting points rather than destinations. The algorithmic competency scale measures four dimensions (understanding, embracing, leveraging, and remediating) and has been validated among Chinese platform workers (Zhou et al., 2025). The algorithm literacy scale measures two dimensions (awareness and knowledge) and has been validated among German internet users (Dogruel, Masur, & Joeckel, 2022). Neither captures the communicative dimension that distinguishes algorithmacy: the capacity to coordinate with other humans through an opaque algorithmic intermediary rather than merely to understand or adapt to algorithmic management. An algorithmacy instrument must assess navigation within a three-party coordination structure, not individual-level adaptation to a two-party management relationship.
7.2 Does algorithmacy transfer across platforms?
The paper’s most counterintuitive prediction concerns transfer. If signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation constitute a shared deep structure beneath surface-level platform differences, competency developed on one platform should transfer to structurally similar platforms, despite their appearances. The current consensus in learning science holds that far transfer rarely succeeds: meta-analyses of cognitive training across chess, music, working memory, and other domains consistently find near-zero transfer effects to structurally dissimilar tasks (Sala & Gobet, 2017; Gobet & Sala, 2023). A second-order meta-analysis synthesizing 233 studies concludes that “the lack of training-induced far transfer is an invariant of human cognition” (Sala, Aksayli, Tatlidil, Tatsumi, Gondo, & Gobet, 2019).
The algorithmacy framework predicts that these meta-analyses may have systematically misclassified a specific category of transfer. Transfer distance is multidimensional, varying across content, context, temporal distance, functional distance, and modality (Barnett & Ceci, 2002). Existing meta-analyses test transfer between genuinely dissimilar deep structures: working memory training to fluid intelligence, chess training to mathematical reasoning, music training to spatial ability. These domains share no deep structural features. Platform-to-platform transfer presents a different case. An Uber driver and a YouTube creator operate on platforms that look entirely different on the surface: different interfaces, content types, user bases, and business models. Experts classify problems by deep structural principles while novices classify by surface features (Chi, Feltovich, & Glaser, 1981). The deep structural features of algorithmic coordination, signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation are shared across both platforms. A driver who has learned to recognize when the algorithm reads their behavior differently than intended (signal asymmetry on Uber) has developed a structural competency that applies when a creator encounters the same dynamic on YouTube. The transfer is “far” on surface dimensions but “near” on deep structural dimensions. Structural, not surface, similarity drives analogical transfer (Gentner, 1983; Holyoak & Koh, 1987). Exposure to two structurally similar but surface-dissimilar analogs facilitates schema induction, which in turn enables transfer to novel domains (Gick & Holyoak, 1983). Open skills requiring adaptation to varied situations show stronger transfer effects than closed skills in organizational meta-analyses (Blume, Ford, Baldwin, & Huang, 2010). Algorithmacy is an open skill operating on shared deep structure.
The experimental test is straightforward. Three conditions: direct communication (no algorithmic intermediary), untrained algorithmic coordination (participants navigate an unfamiliar platform without structural training), and ALC-trained algorithmic coordination (participants receive training in signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation before navigating an unfamiliar platform). The critical prediction is not that training improves performance over no training, which is obvious and uninteresting. The critical prediction is that general structural training produces coordination outcomes that match or exceed platform-specific training, directly contradicting the far-transfer consensus. This prediction requires non-inferiority testing rather than standard null hypothesis significance testing, because the hypothesis concerns equivalence rather than difference (Lakens, 2017; Weber & Popova, 2012). A non-significant t-test does not demonstrate equivalence. The two one-sided tests procedure establishes whether the difference between conditions falls within a pre-specified equivalence bound, providing positive evidence for the claim that structural training is at least as effective as platform-specific training (Schuirmann, 1987).
7.3 How does algorithmacy develop, stratify, and compound over time?
The paper documents differential algorithmacy at a single point in time. It does not trace the developmental trajectory from initial platform enrollment through experienced navigation, the stratification dynamics that determine who develops higher algorithmacy and who does not, or the temporal dynamics through which early advantages compound into large outcome differences. Each requires longitudinal research.
The developmental question asks how algorithmacy emerges through co-optive participation. Workers do not arrive at platforms with pre-formed algorithmacy. They develop it through the recursive process described in Sections 5 and 6: navigating the algorithm, observing outcomes, building working models, testing those models, and refining them as the algorithm adapts. Diary studies and experience sampling methods tracking workers from initial platform enrollment through their first months of participation would capture this developmental trajectory in real time. The acquisition curve may prove nonlinear: rapid initial development as workers encounter obvious algorithmic patterns, followed by a plateau as easily detectable patterns are exhausted, and then a second acceleration as workers discover deeper structural features through community learning or deliberate experimentation. Alternatively, the curve may show threshold effects: minimal development until a critical mass of experience enables schema formation, followed by rapid competency growth. Neither pattern has been documented because no longitudinal study of algorithmic competency development currently exists (Gagrčin, Naab, & Grub, 2024).
The stratification question asks whether algorithmacy is embedded in power relations that shape who develops it and who does not. Street’s (1984) ideological model of literacy argues that literacy is never a neutral technical skill but is always embedded in social practices that distribute its benefits unequally. The same analysis applies to algorithmacy. Approximately 61% of a Norwegian national sample had low or no algorithmic awareness, with awareness concentrated among younger, more educated, and male respondents (Gran, Booth, & Bucher, 2021). Digital skills increase over time, but gaps between groups widen rather than narrow (van Deursen & van Dijk, 2015). Early reading skill creates a virtuous cycle in which good readers read more, build vocabulary, improve comprehension, and read still more, while poor readers fall progressively further behind (Stanovich, 1986). The same “Matthew effect” likely operates for algorithmacy: users who develop early competency engage more effectively with algorithmic systems, learn to optimize their information environments, and compound their advantage, while those who lack early competency receive impoverished algorithmic feedback that stalls development. Cross-sectional research examining the relationship between algorithmacy and socioeconomic position, race, gender, education, and age would test whether the construct reproduces or disrupts existing stratification patterns.
7.4 Implications for organizations
Platform organizations, enterprises deploying algorithmic management, and firms adopting AI tools face the variance phenomenon directly. The algorithmacy framework generates a specific prediction for organizational practice: training programs focused on declarative knowledge of how algorithms work will produce limited improvement in navigational competency, while programs that develop procedural capacity through structured exposure to signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation may prove more effective. The negative correlation between platform-provided training and gig worker income supports this prediction (Zheng, Zhan, & Xu, 2024).
Error management training, which encourages learners to make and learn from errors rather than avoid them, produces effect sizes of d = 0.80 for adaptive transfer to structurally novel tasks (Keith & Frese, 2008). Productive failure designs, in which learners struggle with problems before receiving instruction, consistently outperform instruction-first approaches on conceptual understanding and transfer (Sinha & Kapur, 2021; Kapur, 2016). Both approaches share a structural feature: they privilege experiential navigation of complex environments over declarative instruction about those environments. Organizations developing algorithmacy training should design structured experiences in which workers encounter the three features of triadic algorithmic coordination under conditions that permit safe experimentation and reflective learning, rather than delivering factual presentations about how algorithms operate. Algorithmic environments are “wicked” learning environments where feedback is delayed, opaque, and confounded (Hogarth, Lejarraga, & Soyer, 2015). Training must match the target environment’s structure rather than simplifying it into a “kind” environment that mismatches the conditions workers will encounter.
Organizations should attend to the social infrastructure supporting the development of algorithmacy. Experienced workers develop algorithmic competency through community knowledge-sharing and informal learning rather than through formal organizational channels (Waldkirch, Bucher, Schou, & Grünwald, 2021). When formal learning pathways fail to support competency development in technology-rich environments, workers develop “shadow learning” practices invisible to the organization (Beane, 2019). Organizations that formalize and support these informal learning practices, creating communities of algorithmic practice rather than algorithmic training programs, may accelerate competency development more effectively than instruction-based approaches.
8. Contributions and limitations
This paper makes three contributions, each at a different level of generality.
The first contribution is the construct itself. Algorithmacy names a communication competency distinct from algorithmic literacy, AI literacy, electracy, algocracy, algorithmic governmentality, and secondary orality. The distinction is structural, not evaluative. Each existing framework correctly captures a dimension of the algorithmic phenomenon. None captures the communicative competency required to navigate triadic coordination structures in which an opaque algorithmic intermediary transforms both parties’ inputs in accordance with optimization objectives that neither party controls. The term occupies a specific position in a historical sequence: oracy names the competency for oral coordination, literacy names the competency for written coordination, and algorithmacy names the competency for algorithmic coordination. The -acy suffix names a capacity for effective action within a communication architecture, not merely awareness that the architecture exists. The construct generates predictions that existing constructs cannot: algorithmacy should transfer across platforms sharing deep structural features despite surface dissimilarity, it should develop through co-optive participation rather than instruction, and it should compound through informational feedback into the power-law outcome distributions observed across every platform domain studied.
The second contribution identifies a shared structural limitation across every available framework for understanding human interaction with algorithmic systems. Computer-Mediated Communication, Human-Machine Communication, AI-Mediated Communication, and five parallel concepts (algorithmic literacy, electracy, algocracy, algorithmic governmentality, secondary orality) each inherit a two-party structural assumption from communication theory’s dyadic foundations. The limitation is not a failure of individual scholarship. It is a structural inheritance from Shannon’s information theory through Clark and Brennan’s grounding theory to the present, preserved across five decades of theoretical refinement because each framework improved understanding within the dyadic architecture without questioning the architecture itself. The paper traces this inheritance through its theoretical development and demonstrates that the dyadic structure cannot accommodate triadic coordination for the same structural reasons that dyadic leadership arrangements cannot sustain distributed authority: the emergent properties that produce stability, including coalition dynamics, autonomous third-party interests, and constitutive mediation, require a minimum of three interdependent parties.
The third contribution extends co-optation theory by specifying the competency mechanism through which co-optation produces differential coordination effectiveness. Co-optation is the fourth coordination mechanism alongside markets, hierarchies, and networks (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). The concept explains how platforms enroll autonomous actors into algorithmically mediated participation. It does not explain why actors with equal enrollment achieve different coordination outcomes. Algorithmacy provides the mechanism: workers differ in their capacity to recognize and compensate for the three structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination; those differences develop through sensemaking within co-optive structures and compound through informational feedback. Algorithm sensemaking (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023) is the cognitive process. Co-optation is the coordination mechanism that creates the conditions for sensemaking. Algorithmacy is the competency that develops through sensemaking within co-optive structures and explains why sensemaking quality varies systematically across equally co-opted participants.
The paper has limitations that bound its claims and define the scope of future empirical work.
The paper is conceptual rather than empirical. The three structural features of triadic algorithmic coordination, signal asymmetry, intent compression, and opaque mediation, are derived from theoretical analysis of the coordination architecture rather than from factor-analytic decomposition of observed variance. The dimensional structure of algorithmacy, the measurement instruments appropriate for assessing it, and the nomological network relating it to adjacent constructs all require empirical investigation. The paper proposes the construct. Empirical work must validate it.
The triadic argument may overstate the distinctiveness of the current transition. Every communication technology creates new coordination challenges, and the claim that algorithmic coordination is qualitatively different from prior forms risks the presentist bias that treats the current technological moment as unprecedented, even though prior generations made similar claims about their own innovations. The paper addresses this risk through the historical sequence in Section 4, showing that the structural features distinguishing algorithmic coordination (the recursive property, the constitutive character, the compression of the institutional transition) are genuinely novel rather than recurring. The argument is strongest in coordination environments where an opaque algorithmic intermediary actively transforms both parties’ inputs. It is less clearly applicable to contexts in which algorithms function as passive tools or transparent information systems, and the boundary conditions distinguishing constitutive from instrumental algorithmic environments require specification.
The Lacanian analogy between the algorithm and the Symbolic Order’s “big Other” requires precise boundary conditions. The analogy illuminates the structural position algorithms occupy: a system of rules preexisting individual encounters that constitutes subjects through classification rather than coercion. The analogy risks overgeneralizing by attributing to all algorithmic systems the constitutive power that may characterize only opaque, adaptive, optimization-driven systems operating at scale. Transparent algorithms, user-controlled recommendation systems, and open-source platforms may not occupy the “big Other” position. The constitutive claim applies to the specific class of algorithmic intermediaries defined by the three structural features, not to all computational systems.
The scope is limited to algorithmically mediated coordination and does not address all digital competencies. Algorithmacy names one competency within a broader ecology of digital capabilities that includes technical skills, data literacy, critical media analysis, and creative production. The paper does not claim that algorithmacy subsumes these capabilities or that it is the most important among them. It claims that algorithmacy names a specific competency that existing frameworks do not capture, that this competency explains variance in coordination outcomes that existing frameworks cannot account for, and that the competency develops through mechanisms that existing training approaches do not support. The questions this framework opens for empirical investigation, whether algorithmacy transfers across platforms, how it develops and stratifies over time, and what organizational practices support its development, define a research program whose value will be determined by the answers those questions produce.
