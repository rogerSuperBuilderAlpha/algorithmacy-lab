# When co-optation coordinates: Algorithm sensemaking as a boundary condition on platform coordination effectiveness

Roger

Bentley University

March 2026

---

## Abstract

Platforms coordinate distributed actors through co-optation, enrolling autonomous participants into algorithmically mediated structures that simultaneously direct, evaluate, and discipline their work. Co-optation now stands as a fourth coordination mechanism alongside hierarchy, markets, and networks (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Yet structural enrollment guarantees only that the platform extracts what it requires from participants: data, availability, behavioral compliance. Workers who are deeply enrolled in opaque algorithmic systems do not necessarily achieve the integrating conditions that define effective coordination: accountability, predictability, and common understanding (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009). Two ride-hail drivers encounter identical algorithms in the same city and earn vastly different incomes. Algorithm sensemaking, the active interpretive process through which workers develop, test, and revise working models of algorithmic management (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023), determines when co-optive enrollment produces effective coordination rather than mere compliance under opacity. The direct relationship between algorithmic directiveness and coordination effectiveness should be weak or negative, because intense algorithmic control increases opacity and undermines worker predictability (Rahman, 2021). Algorithm sensemaking moderates this relationship: workers with high sensemaking convert deep enrollment into effective coordination. A multi-platform comparative survey across three tiers of algorithmic directiveness (ride-hail, delivery, marketplace) combines structural and perceptual operationalizations, adapts Amspoker et al.'s (2022) validated Coordination Practices Survey for platform contexts, and measures algorithm sensemaking via scales derived from Möhlmann et al. (2023). The study specifies a boundary condition on co-optation, repositions sensemaking as a moderator rather than a mediator, and offers a template for cross-platform coordination research.

*Keywords:* co-optation, coordination effectiveness, algorithm sensemaking, algorithmic management, platform work, moderation

## Introduction

Identical algorithmic architectures produce wildly divergent outcomes for structurally similar participants. Across ride-hail platforms, delivery services, freelance marketplaces, and content recommendation systems, workers who face the same algorithms, serve the same markets, and begin with comparable resources end up at opposite ends of the income distribution (Cook, Diamond, Hall, List, & Oyer, 2021; Hall & Krueger, 2018; Schor, Attwood-Charles, Cansoy, Ladegaard, & Wengronowitz, 2020). The variance is not noise. It is systematic, persistent, and poorly explained by existing coordination theory.

Coordination theory identifies four mechanisms through which organizations manage dependencies among distributed actors: hierarchy, markets, networks, and co-optation (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009; Powell, 1990; Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024; Williamson, 1975). Co-optation, the most recently formalized of the four, describes the process by which platforms absorb autonomous actors into algorithmically mediated participation structures where the act of participation itself produces the behavioral patterns the platform requires (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). The concept traces a lineage from organizational absorption of external elements to defuse opposition (Selznick, 1949), through inter-organizational goal-setting via co-optation as a strategic alternative to competition, bargaining, and coalition (Thompson & McEwen, 1958), to the contemporary platform context where algorithmic management manufactures consent through constant and confined choices (Cameron, 2024). Ride-hail drivers become platform workers through participation, not through contracts or commands. The algorithm co-opts their labor by making participation itself the mechanism of coordination (Goods, Veen, & Barratt, 2019; Peszko, 2024).

Co-optation theory correctly identifies the mechanism of enrollment. It does not explain differential outcomes after enrollment. The limitation is asymmetric focus: co-optation guarantees that the platform extracts what it needs from participants. The algorithm captures behavioral data, enforces availability norms, and channels worker activity into platform-legible patterns regardless of whether the individual worker understands the system. From the platform's perspective, co-optation always succeeds at enrollment. From the worker's perspective, being deeply enrolled in an opaque algorithmic system does not ensure the integrating conditions that define effective coordination: knowing who is responsible for what (accountability), anticipating how work will unfold (predictability), and sharing a common perspective on the task (common understanding) (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009). A driver who follows every algorithmic prompt without understanding surge logic, rating decay, or dispatch priority is structurally enrolled but coordinatively blind. Under conditions of high algorithmic opacity, workers who cannot understand evaluation criteria become hypervigilant and modify behavior in counterproductive ways, creating what one researcher calls an "invisible cage" (Rahman, 2021). Co-optation explains the platform's success at enrollment. It does not explain the worker's success at coordination.

Algorithm sensemaking is the boundary condition that determines when co-optation produces coordination effectiveness for the worker. The construct names the active interpretive process through which platform workers develop, test, and revise working models of algorithmic management, encompassing behavioral experimentation, strategic information seeking, and cognitive model maintenance (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023). Workers who engage in stronger sensemaking build more accurate representations of algorithmic behavior, anticipate algorithmic responses to their actions, and adjust their coordination strategies accordingly (Bucher, 2017; DeVito, 2021). Sensemaking does not merely transmit the effects of co-optation. It determines when co-optation translates into effective coordination. Recent information systems research provides direct precedent for positioning sensemaking as a moderator of platform-level relationships rather than a mediator of them (Soh & Grover, 2022). Workers under high algorithmic directiveness with high sensemaking leverage their deep enrollment into effective navigation. Workers under high algorithmic directiveness with low sensemaking are enrolled without comprehension: structurally compliant but coordinatively ineffective.

A multi-platform comparative survey design exploits structural variation in algorithmic directiveness across platform types. Ride-hail platforms exercise near-total algorithmic direction over dispatch, pricing, and evaluation. Delivery platforms retain algorithmic dispatch but grant greater task-level discretion. Marketplace platforms surface recommendations and evaluate performance but leave task selection and execution to sellers. These structural differences constitute natural variation in the intensity of algorithmic coordination (Kellogg, Valentine, & Christin, 2020). Surveying workers across all three tiers, following the multi-platform approach essential for developing theories of platform labor (Schor et al., 2020), tests how structural algorithmic directiveness interacts with individual sensemaking to produce coordination outcomes.

Three contributions follow. First, a boundary condition on co-optation: co-optation coordinates effectively when participants can make sense of the algorithmic system that coordinates them. Second, a repositioning of algorithm sensemaking from mediator to moderator, generating distinct empirical predictions about whether sensemaking transmits or conditions the effects of algorithmic structures. Third, a structural operationalization of algorithmic directiveness that complements individual-level perceptual measures and offers a template for cross-platform coordination research.

## Theory and hypotheses

### Co-optation as a coordination mechanism

Coordination theory addresses a fundamental organizational problem: how distributed actors manage dependencies among their activities to achieve collective outcomes (Malone & Crowston, 1994; Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009). Three mechanisms have long dominated the field. Hierarchy coordinates through authority and direct supervision (Williamson, 1975). Markets coordinate through price signals and contractual exchange (Coase, 1937). Networks coordinate through trust, reciprocity, and relational ties (Powell, 1990). Each mechanism implies a distinct relationship between the coordinating structure and the actors it coordinates. Hierarchy commands. Markets incentivize. Networks obligate.

Co-optation coordinates by absorbing autonomous actors into participation structures where participation itself produces the coordination the system requires (Stark & Vanden Broeck, 2024). Digital platforms enroll drivers, couriers, hosts, and sellers into algorithmically mediated participation where behavioral compliance emerges through participation rather than through commands, contracts, or social bonds. Platform workers become coordinated actors not because they agree to a set of rules, but because acting within the algorithmic architecture produces the behavioral patterns the platform requires (Cameron, 2024; Goods et al., 2019; Peszko, 2024). The concept extends a long theoretical lineage. Selznick (1949) described organizational absorption of external community leaders to secure compliance without ceding substantive control. Thompson and McEwen (1958) generalized co-optation as one of four strategies organizations use to manage environmental relations, positioning it as an inter-organizational coordination mechanism. Stark and Vanden Broeck (2024) complete the generalization to algorithmic platforms, where co-optation operates through computational architectures rather than through personal or institutional relationships.

Co-optation differs from the other three mechanisms in a structural respect that matters for this study. In hierarchy, markets, and networks, the coordinating structure and the coordinated actors maintain mutual legibility. A manager can observe a subordinate's work. A buyer can evaluate a seller's price. A network member can assess a partner's trustworthiness. In algorithmic co-optation, the coordinating structure operates through logic that neither the platform worker nor the end customer can directly observe (Burrell, 2016; Kellogg et al., 2020; Pasquale, 2015). The algorithm reads worker behavior through models the worker cannot inspect, assigns evaluations through criteria the worker cannot fully specify, and adjusts coordination parameters through processes that respond to aggregate patterns invisible to any individual participant. Co-optation in algorithmic contexts is coordination under opacity.

A critical asymmetry follows. Co-optation succeeds on the platform's terms regardless of whether the worker understands the system. The algorithm captures the behavioral data it needs, enforces the availability norms it requires, and extracts the coordination patterns it optimizes for. The driver who blindly follows every dispatch prompt generates the same data stream as the driver who strategically selects rides. Both are co-opted. The platform coordinates both. The distinction between enrolling participants into the platform's coordination structure and managing the quality of their contributions once enrolled maps onto what recent research formalizes as the difference between matching and control (Möhlmann, Zalmanson, Henfridsson, & Gregory, 2021). Co-optation theory specifies the mechanism of enrollment. It does not specify the conditions under which enrollment produces effective coordination for the enrolled actor.

### Coordination effectiveness in algorithmically mediated environments

Coordination effectiveness describes the degree to which interdependent actors successfully manage the dependencies among their activities (Okhuysen & Bechky, 2009). Three integrating conditions support it. Accountability establishes clarity about who is responsible for what. Predictability enables actors to anticipate how work will unfold. Common understanding provides shared knowledge of the task environment and how individual contributions fit within the whole. When all three conditions hold, actors manage their interdependencies without constant renegotiation. When any condition degrades, coordination suffers.

Algorithmic platforms restructure all three conditions. Accountability is algorithmically defined: the platform determines what counts as task completion, assigns performance scores, and allocates consequences for substandard performance. A ride-hail driver's accountability is not negotiated with passengers but computed by the dispatch and rating algorithms (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016). Predictability depends on the worker's ability to anticipate algorithmic behavior: which rides the algorithm will offer, how surge pricing will fluctuate, what acceptance rate the algorithm requires to maintain priority status. Common understanding is asymmetric: the algorithm possesses a comprehensive model of aggregate worker behavior while each worker possesses only a partial, locally constructed model of the algorithm (Curchod, Patriotta, Cohen, & Neysen, 2020; Möhlmann et al., 2023).

A validated instrument directly operationalizes these integrating conditions. The Coordination Practices Survey measures accountability (5 items, α = .93), common understanding (5 items, α = .94), and predictability (5 items), confirmed via multilevel confirmatory factor analysis with 676 respondents across 212 teams (Amspoker, Lester, Spitzmueller, Thomas, & Hysong, 2022). The instrument emerged from a systematic review of 279 coordination items across 37 published scales, which found no intact scale measuring Okhuysen and Bechky's specific constructs (Thomas, Spitzmueller, Amspoker, & Hysong, 2018). An independent effort produced a multidimensional coordination scale operationalizing the same three conditions across explicit and implicit dimensions, providing convergent evidence for the measurement approach (Liu, Keller, & Bartlett, 2017). The predictability subscale exhibited ceiling effects in the healthcare context due to highly standardized protocols. Platform work environments, where algorithmic opacity generates high unpredictability, should provide the response variance that standardized healthcare constrained.

### Algorithm sensemaking

Algorithm sensemaking names the active interpretive process through which platform workers develop, test, and revise working models of algorithmic management systems (Möhlmann, Salge, & Marabelli, 2023). The construct is sociocognitive, not purely cognitive: it encompasses behavioral experimentation, strategic information gathering, and cognitive model maintenance. Three sub-processes compose it. Focused enactment describes deliberate experimentation with algorithmic systems: drivers who test whether declining certain ride requests affects future dispatch, or delivery workers who vary routes to observe effects on algorithmic priority. Selection modes describe strategic choices among available information sources: direct experience, peer networks, platform documentation, online forums. Retention sources describe how workers store and deploy their working models: habitual routines, explicit rules of thumb, continuously updated cognitive maps of algorithmic behavior.

A body of research documents how platform workers develop informal theories of algorithmic behavior, with consistent findings that these representations vary in accuracy and sophistication and that variation predicts navigational effectiveness. The "algorithmic imaginary" names the set of affective and cognitive orientations workers bring to algorithmic encounters (Bucher, 2017). Content creators develop adaptive folk theories of recommendation algorithms through iterative experience (DeVito, 2021). Social media users react with surprise when algorithmic curation becomes visible, revealing that most had no prior awareness of algorithmic mediation (Eslami et al., 2015). TikTok users co-produce algorithmic knowledge and engage in algorithmic resistance through evolving folk theories that shape identity and behavior (Karizat, Delmonaco, Eslami, & Andalibi, 2021). Workers engage in "anticipatory compliance," proactively adapting behavior to perceived algorithmic preferences even without understanding the algorithm, and the quality of sensemaking determines whether this anticipatory compliance is strategic or maladaptive (Bucher, Schou, & Waldkirch, 2021).

Sensemaking quality varies across workers facing identical algorithmic systems. Ride-hail drivers and food delivery workers with higher sensemaking quality exhibit more strategic behavior and report higher earnings (Möhlmann et al., 2023). A recently validated "algorithmic competency" scale for platform workers captures a related phenomenon through four dimensions: understanding, embracing, leveraging, and remediating algorithmic management (Zhou et al., 2025). While not identical to algorithm sensemaking, algorithmic competency provides convergent evidence that differential capacity to navigate algorithmic coordination systems produces differential outcomes.

An unresolved question in this literature concerns sensemaking's theoretical role. Two causal architectures are plausible. Mediation holds that algorithmic directiveness works through sensemaking: enrollment produces sensemaking, which produces coordination. Moderation holds that algorithmic directiveness works better or worse depending on sensemaking: enrollment interacts with sensemaking to produce coordination. The distinction is not merely statistical. It implies different intervention strategies: if sensemaking mediates, improving sensemaking at any level of directiveness should improve coordination; if sensemaking moderates, the benefit depends on the structural context. Direct precedent exists for positioning sensemaking as a moderator of platform-level relationships (Soh & Grover, 2022), and the Weickian theoretical root of focused enactment has been tested as a boundary condition on organizational outcomes (Cortina et al., 2019).

### Hypothesis development

Workers who operate under higher algorithmic directiveness, where the algorithm assigns tasks, constrains execution, evaluates performance, and disciplines non-compliance, are more deeply immersed in the platform's coordination infrastructure. The dispatch algorithm feeds information to compliant drivers, the rating system tracks task completion, and the scheduling algorithm prioritizes workers who demonstrate availability. Greater immersion means greater exposure to algorithmic inputs.

Greater exposure does not straightforwardly translate into more effective coordination. The empirical literature on algorithmic management consistently documents that intense algorithmic control produces opacity-induced anxiety, decreased predictability, and precarious working conditions (Kellogg et al., 2020; Schor et al., 2020; Wood, Graham, Lehdonvirta, & Hjorth, 2019). Opaque algorithmic evaluations create conditions where workers who cannot understand evaluation criteria modify their behavior in counterproductive ways (Rahman, 2021). Algorithmic control transparency does not mitigate technostress as expected, suggesting that structural exposure to algorithmic management creates demands that persist independent of information provision (Cram, Wiener, Tarafdar, & Benlian, 2022). Algorithmic power asymmetries produce hyper-sensitivity and constrained agency even among experienced platform workers (Curchod et al., 2020). The direct relationship between algorithmic directiveness and coordination effectiveness should therefore be weak or negative. Structural enrollment provides access to the platform's coordination infrastructure but simultaneously increases exposure to algorithmic opacity. Without interpretive capacity to convert opaque signals into actionable coordination knowledge, deeper enrollment produces deeper dependence without deeper understanding. Compliance is not coordination.

*Hypothesis 1:* Algorithmic directiveness has a weak or negative direct relationship with coordination effectiveness.

The central argument is that algorithm sensemaking determines when algorithmic directiveness translates into coordination effectiveness. Algorithmic directiveness provides the raw material for coordination: algorithmic inputs, feedback signals, task allocation, performance evaluation. Sensemaking is the active interpretive process that converts that raw material into effective coordination behavior. The interaction is rescuing rather than merely amplifying: sensemaking converts the otherwise negative or null effects of opaque algorithmic enrollment into effective coordination.

Four cells define the interaction. Workers under high directiveness with high sensemaking receive rich algorithmic inputs and convert them into effective coordination strategies through focused enactment, strategic information selection, and accurate model retention. They achieve the highest coordination effectiveness because they have both the material and the interpretive capacity. Workers under high directiveness with low sensemaking comply with algorithmic directives without understanding the logic behind them, producing blind compliance: they follow coordination signals but lack accountability clarity, cannot predict algorithmic behavior, and share no common understanding of the coordination system.

Workers under low directiveness with high sensemaking operate in environments where the algorithm recommends and evaluates but does not assign tasks or constrain execution. These workers possess interpretive capacity, but because the algorithm exerts minimal direction over their core coordination activities, the incremental benefit of sensemaking is smaller. The algorithm throws fewer curveballs at a marketplace seller than at a ride-hail driver; there is less algorithmic opacity to navigate, so the sensemaking rescue effect is less dramatic. Workers under low directiveness with low sensemaking face minimal algorithmic direction and possess minimal interpretive capacity; coordination depends primarily on their own task management rather than on algorithmic navigation.

The moderating pattern is a rescuing interaction: the negative or null relationship between algorithmic directiveness and coordination effectiveness reverses direction when sensemaking is high. Without sensemaking, deeper enrollment increases opacity without increasing effectiveness. With sensemaking, deeper enrollment provides richer material for the interpretive process, and the combination produces effective coordination.

*Hypothesis 2:* Algorithm sensemaking moderates the relationship between algorithmic directiveness and coordination effectiveness, such that the relationship is positive when algorithm sensemaking is high and negative or null when algorithm sensemaking is low.

## Methods

### Research design

A multi-platform comparative survey design exploits structural variation in algorithmic directiveness across platform types. Two operationalizations of the independent variable serve complementary purposes. The structural operationalization classifies platforms into three tiers based on the degree of algorithmic directiveness they exert over workers, following dimensions identified by Kellogg et al. (2020). The perceptual operationalization measures individual workers' perceived algorithmic control within their primary platform. The dual operationalization treats algorithmic directiveness as both a structural feature of the coordination environment and an individual experience, testing whether the moderation holds across both levels of measurement.

Within a single platform, all workers face the same algorithmic architecture, making algorithmic directiveness a near-constant rather than a variable. Cross-platform variation provides the range necessary to detect both main effects and interactions. Multi-platform designs are essential for developing theories of platform labor (Schor et al., 2020).

### Platform selection and classification

Three tiers of algorithmic directiveness emerge from four dimensions of algorithmic control: direction, restriction, evaluation, and discipline (Kellogg et al., 2020). The term "algorithmic directiveness" distinguishes the structural platform feature (how much the algorithm directs the worker's core task) from the broader theoretical construct of co-optation (the coordination mechanism of enrollment through participation). All three tiers involve co-optation. They differ in how directive the algorithmic architecture is within the co-optive structure.

Ride-hail platforms (Uber, Lyft) constitute the high directiveness tier, scoring high on all four dimensions. The dispatch algorithm directs task assignment, restricts task execution through navigation guidance, time windows, and cancellation penalties, evaluates performance through continuous rating and acceptance rate tracking, and disciplines non-compliance through reduced priority, suspensions, and deactivation. Drivers operate within a coordination structure that determines nearly every parameter of their work except the decision to log in (Cameron, 2024; Rosenblat & Stark, 2016).

Delivery platforms (DoorDash, Instacart) constitute the medium directiveness tier. The algorithm directs task assignment and evaluates performance, but grants substantially more discretion over task execution. Delivery workers choose routes, decide whether to accept bundled orders, and manage multi-app strategies with less restrictive penalties than ride-hail. The algorithm directs and evaluates but does not restrict execution as tightly.

Marketplace platforms (Etsy, eBay, Fiverr) constitute the low directiveness tier. The algorithm surfaces and recommends through search ranking, recommendation placement, and featured listings, but does not assign tasks. Sellers choose what to list, at what price, and which clients to accept. Evaluation carries significant consequences for visibility and access, but the algorithm does not direct the core task. It shapes the environment within which autonomous actors coordinate, rather than assigning specific coordination behaviors. Workers in this tier can be highly engaged and economically dependent on the platform while operating under low algorithmic directiveness over task execution.

Two dummy variables code the three-tier classification, with the low directiveness tier as the reference group. Workers on high directiveness platforms should report higher perceived algorithmic control than workers on low directiveness platforms. If they do not, the structural classification requires revision.

### Participants and recruitment

A target sample of 450 to 600 active platform workers distributes 150 to 200 workers per tier. Prolific provides the recruitment infrastructure, supporting screening by platform affiliation and work activity (Palan & Schitter, 2018). Eligibility requires active participation on at least one qualifying platform for a minimum of three months, at least five hours of weekly platform work, and treatment of platform work as a primary or significant secondary income source. These criteria exclude casual or inactive users whose low engagement would confound the directiveness measure. If Prolific recruitment yields insufficient sample sizes for the high directiveness tier, respondent-driven sampling via verified driver communities supplements recruitment under identical screening criteria.

Within each tier, recruitment spans at least two platforms. If all high directiveness workers came from Uber alone, effects attributed to algorithmic directiveness might reflect Uber-specific interface features, local market conditions, or company policies rather than the structural mechanism of interest. Recruiting from multiple platforms within each tier provides within-tier replication.

Power analysis for a medium-sized interaction effect (f-squared = 0.05) in moderated multiple regression with three predictors, their interactions, and five covariates, assuming alpha = .05 and power = .80, indicates a minimum required sample of approximately 395 participants (Cohen, 1992). The target of 450 to 600 accommodates anticipated attrition.

### Measures

*Algorithmic directiveness.* Two operationalizations capture the construct at different levels. The structural measure is the platform tier classification, dummy-coded with the low directiveness tier as the reference category. The perceptual measure adapts instruments from prior algorithmic management research (Cram et al., 2022; Meijerink et al., 2023) into a 16-item scale across four dimensions aligned with Kellogg et al.'s (2020) taxonomy: algorithmic direction ("The platform's algorithm determines which tasks I receive"), algorithmic restriction ("The platform's algorithm constrains how I perform my work"), algorithmic evaluation ("The platform's algorithm continuously scores my performance"), and algorithmic discipline ("Failing to follow the platform's algorithmic recommendations leads to negative consequences for me"). Four items per dimension, rated on a 7-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree), undergo confirmatory factor analysis to assess whether the four dimensions load on a single second-order factor (Bagozzi, Yi, & Phillips, 1991).

*Algorithm sensemaking.* Items adapted from Möhlmann et al.'s (2023) qualitative findings capture three sub-processes: focused enactment ("I actively experiment with different strategies to see how the algorithm responds"), selection modes ("I use multiple information sources, including other workers, online forums, and my own tests, to understand how the platform's algorithm works"), and retention sources ("I have developed a clear working model of what the algorithm rewards and penalizes that I update as I gain new experience"). An initial pool of 24 items generated from the qualitative findings undergoes cognitive interviews with 10 platform workers to assess comprehension and relevance, reducing to 12 items (four per sub-process) based on inter-item correlations and content coverage (DeVellis & Thorpe, 2022). A 7-point Likert scale captures responses. Item wording targets the behavioral and strategic dimensions of sensemaking alongside the cognitive dimension, reflecting the sociocognitive nature of the construct. Zhou et al.'s (2025) validated algorithmic competency scale provides convergent reference for item development, though the present scale targets Möhlmann et al.'s specific three-component structure.

*Coordination effectiveness.* The Coordination Practices Survey (Amspoker et al., 2022) provides the measurement foundation, adapted for platform coordination contexts following scale adaptation best practices (Heggestad, Scheaf, Banks, Monroe, DeSimone, & Rogelberg, 2019). Adaptation treats the algorithm not merely as a tool but as an active, non-human coordination partner, consistent with recent perspectives on working and organizing in the age of algorithmic systems (Faraj, Pachidi, & Sayegh, 2018). The accountability item "It is clear which team members are responsible for completion of specific tasks" becomes "It is clear what I am responsible for and what the platform's algorithm handles in completing a task." The common understanding item "Our team has a shared perspective of how each person's work contributes to the overall goal" becomes "I understand how my work, the algorithm's processes, and the customer's actions fit together to produce a completed task." A sample predictability item: "I can anticipate how the platform will respond when I change my work patterns." Platform work environments should provide the response variance in predictability that standardized healthcare protocols constrained in the original validation.

The predictability items overlap conceptually with algorithm sensemaking retention items. Discriminant validity testing addresses this directly. The conservative HTMT < .85 threshold with bootstrapped confidence intervals assesses whether the constructs are empirically distinguishable (Henseler, Ringle, & Sarstedt, 2015). A CFA model comparison tests whether sensemaking and coordination effectiveness load better on separate factors than on a single factor. If the predictability subscale cannot be discriminated from sensemaking, only the accountability and common understanding subscales constitute the coordination effectiveness construct, following Amspoker et al.'s precedent and consistent with recommendations for addressing construct proliferation (Shaffer, DeGeest, & Li, 2016). Responses use a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree), matching the original validation.

*Controls.* Platform tenure (months), weekly hours worked, multi-platform use (number of active platforms), prior gig work experience (months on any platform), education (highest degree), age, and gender control for variables that plausibly affect coordination effectiveness independent of algorithmic directiveness and sensemaking.

### Analytic strategy

Four stages test the hypothesized moderation.

*Stage 1: measurement model validation.* Confirmatory factor analysis fits a three-factor model (perceived algorithmic directiveness, algorithm sensemaking, coordination effectiveness) and assesses fit against standard criteria: CFI > .90, RMSEA < .08, SRMR < .08 (Hu & Bentler, 1999). Discriminant validity applies both the Fornell-Larcker (1981) AVE criterion and the HTMT ratio (Henseler et al., 2015), with the conservative HTMT < .85 threshold for the sensemaking-coordination pair. Composite reliability and item-level loadings complete the assessment, following current best-practice recommendations (Cheung, Cooper-Thomas, Lau, & Wang, 2024) and construct clarity criteria (Suddaby, 2010).

*Stage 2: moderated regression with structural algorithmic directiveness.* The dependent variable is coordination effectiveness. Two dummy-coded platform tier variables (high and medium directiveness, low as reference), mean-centered algorithm sensemaking, and two interaction terms (high directiveness x sensemaking, medium directiveness x sensemaking) enter hierarchically: controls in Block 1, main effects in Block 2, interactions in Block 3. Significant R-squared change for Block 3 supports the moderation hypothesis. Simple slopes at high (+1 SD) and low (-1 SD) sensemaking probe significant interactions (Dawson, 2014). The Johnson-Neyman technique identifies the exact sensemaking level at which the directiveness-coordination relationship transitions from negative to positive.

*Stage 3: moderated regression with perceived algorithmic directiveness.* Hayes's (2022) PROCESS Model 1 replicates the moderation using individual-level perceived directiveness as a continuous independent variable, with sensemaking as the moderator and coordination effectiveness as the outcome. Convergence between structural and perceptual operationalizations validates the finding. Divergence signals a robustness concern.

*Stage 4: alternative model tests.* An indirect effect model with algorithmic directiveness predicting sensemaking predicting coordination effectiveness (PROCESS Model 4) tests the alternative mediation architecture (Mathieu & Taylor, 2006). If both moderation and mediation hold, a moderated mediation specification follows. Subgroup analyses across education and prior experience levels test boundary conditions on the moderating role of sensemaking.

### Limitations and mitigation

Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference about directional effects. Workers who coordinate effectively may develop better sensemaking as a consequence of positive outcomes rather than the reverse. The theoretical grounding of the moderation direction addresses this partially; longitudinal or experimental designs can address temporal ordering in future work.

Common method variance is a concern because sensemaking, perceived directiveness, and coordination effectiveness are all self-reported. The structural directiveness measure (platform tier) is independent of the self-report instrument and serves as a manipulation check. Harman's single-factor test and the marker variable technique assess common method variance magnitude (Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, & Podsakoff, 2003).

Self-selection into platform types is a potential confound. Workers who choose high directiveness platforms may differ from those who choose low directiveness platforms in ways affecting both sensemaking and coordination. Observable selection factors (education, prior experience, age) enter as controls. Unobserved selection remains a limitation addressable through instrumental variable approaches in future work.

The three-tier classification introduces measurement coarseness. Algorithmic control varies by market, time, and worker status even within a single platform. The dual operationalization allows the individual-level measure to capture within-tier variation that the structural classification misses.
