# The Transition to Algorithmacy — The Argument Across Three Papers

**Roger Hunt III · Bentley University**

*A one-page narrative of how the three papers make one argument.*

---

**The move.** A driver reaches a rider only through a platform that makes the match, sets the fare, and keeps the two from dealing directly. The shape is everywhere in platform work, and the field has no name for it. Its constructs describe a worker on one side of a channel, a partner, or a system: a dyad. The phenomenon is a triad, a worker reaching a counterpart through a third party that constitutes the coordination and holds an interest of its own. One object runs through all three papers: the irreducibility of coordination to its parts. Paper 1 names it, Paper 2 models it, Paper 3 grades it and places real organizations on it.

**Paper 1 names the form and the competency.** A third element can *moderate*, shaping a channel the two parties still own, or it can *mediate*, constituting the coordination on terms of its own and keeping the parties apart. The first is a moderated dyad, and the existing literacies are its competencies. The second is a mediated triad, and the competency it demands has no name. The paper names it *algorithmacy*, places it in the line from oracy to literacy, and specifies it to construct clarity. It is candid about what the competency is: a demand the system offloads onto the worker, not a neutral skill, and naming it does not make the burden fair. The paper leaves one question open. Handed a network, how does an analyst decide which form it is? The surface does not settle it.

**Paper 2 makes the decision computable.** It borrows integrated information theory's measure of irreducibility, Φ, as an analogy and not a homology, and leaves the consciousness theory at the door. A coordination form is modeled as a three-node system over worker, system, and counterpart, and Φ over the minimum-information partition reads whether the mechanism factors. Zero is a dyad, positive a triad. The verdict is the declared mechanism's, and it is auditable: the encodings are stated in full and open to challenge. The paper is exact about the bounds. On every coordination form with a real-world anchor the verdict agrees with a cheap factorization test, so Φ serves as a conservative validator of Paper 1's criterion rather than a discovery engine, and its distinct advantage is a result about the model family, not yet the field.

**Paper 3 grades the model and applies it.** It computes Φ across the complete family of three-node forms and places five large corporations by how they coordinate their parties. The binary dyad-triad verdict is the reliable result; the magnitude is read ordinally at best, a fingerprint of the wiring class, not a validated scale of demand. A securities exchange and a ride-hailing platform code to the same structure and the same score, because the model is blind to the mediator's seat. That blindness is as much a limitation as a result: the parties are represented as symmetric nodes, so power, due process, and recourse are absent from the representation. The cases are provisional, read from public documentation with fieldwork deferred, so they demonstrate the model rather than validate it.

**The contribution.** A name, a procedure, and a placement, where the field had none of the three. The dissertation turns "coordination is becoming triadic" from an observation into a formal apparatus, and it offers a method that travels: compute a structural property from observable records, fix the modeling choices before interpreting them, and put them where they can be contested. What it does not do is validate the model against observed outcomes or measure any worker. That validation, and the matched person-level instrument, are the program this work opens. Literacy became a competency when writing reorganized how a culture coordinated. The claim here is that an analogous change runs through the algorithmic third party now standing between the parties to most platform work.
