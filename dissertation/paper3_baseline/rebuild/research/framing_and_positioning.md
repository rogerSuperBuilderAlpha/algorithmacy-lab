## The headline: two independent literatures already warrant the paper's core moves

**1. Coordination theory — the unit is the structure, not the actor, and it is substrate-independent.**
Malone & Crowston (1994) define coordination as "managing dependencies between activities" and
typologize it by the KIND of dependency and a family of interchangeable mechanisms — not by the
coordinator's identity. They state coordination "can occur in many kinds of systems: human,
computational, biological," and call for "transporting concepts… back and forth between the
different kinds of systems." Crowston's taxonomy deliberately breaks with the org tradition of
locating dependencies between *actors* and locates them between *activities*, and shows the same
mechanism (e.g. market-like task assignment) can be performed by humans or automated without
changing the underlying structure. This is the canonical **form-over-actor** warrant and a direct
precedent for **human/algorithmic equivalence of like structures**.

**2. Brokerage theory — outcomes come from structural position, and the occupant is interchangeable.**
Burt (1992) is the strongest precedent: "Competition is a matter of relations, not player
attributes… Causation resides in the intersection of relations"; the unit is "a role, a market, or
a position in social structure," and its occupant may be "a person, an organization, or a broader
aggregation." That is "structure of mediation, not seat of mediator" stated almost verbatim, from a
canonical source. Halevy, Halali & Zlatev (2019) formally separate **brokerage** (structural
position) from **brokering** (behavioral process) and hold that "different triadic configurations
enable distinct forms of influence" — a triadic structural typology of third-party mediation that
Paper 3's graded W–S–C typology extends. (Caveat: Burt's unit is egocentric, not a W–S–C triad;
invoke Burt for the position-over-occupant *principle*, which the quotes squarely establish.)

## Supporting warrants

- **A graded ladder of coordination structure already exists.** Van de Ven, Delbecq & Koenig (1976)
  classify coordination modes (impersonal / personal / group) and add a fourth, highest-coupling
  interdependence level — "team" (relabeled "intensive" in the workflow-complexity literature; cite
  both) — above Thompson's (1967) pooled / sequential / reciprocal. Paper 3's graded triadic-demand
  bands build on this precedent rather than inventing a ladder from nothing.
- **Two-sided-market theory gives a structural criterion and a functional (substrate-agnostic)
  intermediary.** Rochet & Tirole: a market is two-sided iff the price *structure* (not just level)
  affects volume; the platform is defined by its coordinating function (get both sides on board,
  internalize cross-side externalities), the same definition across cards, consoles, dating, media.
  Relational structure, not the intermediary's identity, is the defining feature.

## The gap (precise)

The algorithmic-management literature frames the platform/algorithm as a **dyadic control actor**
and folds structure into the **technology's affordances**:
- Kellogg, Valentine & Christin (2020), "Algorithms at Work" — the "6 Rs" (restricting,
  recommending, recording, rating, replacing, rewarding); a *new instantiation of contested terrain*
  (Edwards 1979), but framed employer↔worker (dyadic) and stressing algorithmic affordances
  (opacity, comprehensiveness, instantaneousness).
- Vallas & Schor (2020), "What Do Platforms Do?" — platforms as a qualitatively distinct governance
  form (beyond Powell's 1990 markets/hierarchies/networks), but as **actors** ("permissive
  potentates"); structure and actor are not cleanly separated.
- Rahman (2021), "The Invisible Cage" — locates the harm in opacity, an attribute of the evaluator.

So **no existing work scores a triadic Worker–System–Counterpart coordination form by structural
irreducibility (Φ) such that a human institution and an algorithmic platform of identical structure
receive the same score.** Paper 3 fills exactly that gap: it separates the structural role of the
mediator from the mediator's nature, and computes it.

## The strongest reviewer objection, and how to preempt it

**Objection (Rahman 2021 + brokerage-contingency literature):** the human=algorithm equivalence
abstracts away opacity, power, and accountability that genuinely depend on the mediator's nature.
Rahman locates the consequential harm in opacity (not the computational substrate), and his remedies
(transparency, human-in-the-loop) presuppose that algorithmic ≠ human evaluators. Separately, a
contingency literature shows realized brokerage returns depend on the occupant's personality,
gender, status, culture — so identical structure need not yield identical *outcomes*.

**Preemption (scope the claim):** Φ measures the **structural irreducibility of the mediation** —
the analytic object — not the realized distributive or normative consequences. Opacity, power, and
accountability are **orthogonal contingencies layered on top of an identical structural score**, not
part of it. This is continuous with Paper 2's §4 distinction (epistemic opacity vs. compositional
irreducibility are independent properties), so Paper 3 inherits a defended version of the move. State
plainly: two forms of the same structure are the same *coordination problem*; they can differ
arbitrarily in how opaque, powerful, or accountable the mediator is, and the model does not claim
otherwise.
