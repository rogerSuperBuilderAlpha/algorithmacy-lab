# Paper 3 outline: a baseline scale for platform demand

Roger Hunt III
Bentley University

Working outline, v1.

The paper produces the baseline. It computes Φ across real platforms and yields a difficulty scale, so that any coordination form can be placed by how much algorithmacy its navigation demands, the way a readability score places a text by how much reading competency it demands. The measure is a property of the platform, validated against observed coordination outcomes, and not a claim about any individual. The instrument is Paper 2's contribution; the calibrated scale is this paper's.

---

## 1. Introduction: from a measure to a scale

Paper 2 built an instrument that decides whether a coordination form is triadic; an instrument is not yet a scale until its output is anchored to something that varies with difficulty. The introduction states the move from decision procedure to calibrated scale, and commits to the readability analogy as the model: a number that places a platform on a continuum of demand, validated against how people actually coordinate through it.

Moves: state what Paper 2 delivered and what it withheld. Declare the contribution as a calibrated difficulty scale for coordination forms. Commit to the platform as the unit of measurement and to outcome validation as the anchor.

## 2. The unit: the platform, not the person

The scale measures the platform, and the dissertation should say so without flinching. The section establishes that a difficulty score is a property of the coordination form, validated against how participants perform on it, and carries no claim about any individual participant. The person-level competency is the matched sibling that the program builds later, exactly as a reading-ability measure is the sibling of a text-difficulty measure. Calibrating the form comes before matching participants to it.

Moves: state the unit and defend it. Draw the readability parallel: the text score is validated against measured comprehension and says nothing about a given reader. Locate the variance puzzle as the program's destination and explain why it does not belong in this paper. Answer in advance the reviewer who asks where the individuals are.

## 3. The calibration anchor

The scale's credibility rests entirely on the anchor, because Φ over an application-layer matrix gives a number and a number is not a difficulty scale until it is tied to an observable outcome that varies with difficulty. The section names the anchor and the validation logic: a high-Φ platform must demonstrably produce the coordination outcomes a demanding platform should produce, so that the formal measure and the behavioral record move together. The Chicago transportation network company dataset is the natural calibration set, because it carries coordination outcomes at scale.

Moves: state that the anchor is the paper's load-bearing decision, the way the state alphabet was Paper 2's. Specify the observed coordination outcome against which platform Φ is validated. Argue the dataset choice: large-scale interaction records with outcomes, rather than self-report. Concede that a single calibration domain limits generalization, in one sentence, and return: a calibrated scale in one domain is the precondition for porting the method to others.

## 4. Method: computing the scale across platforms

The scale is built by computing Φ over the application-layer matrix of each platform under the pre-registered state-individuation rule, then regressing the formal measure against the calibration outcome. The section specifies the modeling discipline Paper 2's tractability constraint imposes: the state alphabet must be coarse enough to compute and principled enough to defend, and the same rule must apply across every platform so the scores are comparable. The readability precedent governs the validation: the successful difficulty instruments are feature measures tied to outcomes, not deep models of internal structure.

Moves: specify the platform sample and the application-layer state alphabet for each, under the fixed rule. State the Φ computation and the granularity discipline. Specify the validation regression of Φ against the calibration outcome. Report the scale and its anchor fit.

## 5. Results: the baseline

The baseline is a set of platforms placed on a single demand scale, with the formal measure validated against observed coordination. The section reports where each platform falls, whether Φ tracks the calibration outcome as the construct predicts, and what the spread establishes: that coordination forms differ in measurable degree of triadicity, and that the difference predicts how coordination actually goes. The dyadic limit and the irreducible cases anchor the ends of the scale.

Moves: report the platform placements and the anchor validation. Show that the dyadic-limit case sits low and the irreducible cases sit high, as Paper 2's worked application predicts. State the baseline as the calibrated reference future work measures against.

## 6. What the baseline establishes

The baseline establishes that the demand for algorithmacy is measurable and that it varies across the coordination forms ordinary life now runs on. The section states the contribution as a calibrated, portable instrument for placing any coordination form on a scale of triadic demand, names the matched supply-side instrument as the next work, and positions the baseline as the empirical foundation the research program builds on.

Moves: state the contribution. Name the open extensions: the supply-side individual scale, additional calibration domains, the within-platform variance the matched pair will eventually explain. Close on the program the baseline opens.

---

## Two load-bearing decisions, stated honestly

Paper 2 stands or falls on the state-individuation rule. Paper 3 stands or falls on the calibration anchor. Neither is a doubt about algorithmacy. Both are questions about the instrument that measures it, and naming them as such is what makes the dissertation defensible rather than fragile.
