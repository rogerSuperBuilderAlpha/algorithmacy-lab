# Toward a Readability Score for Coordination: Modeling Triadic Demand Across a Population of Organizations, and Testing the Structural Axis

**Roger Hunt III**
Bentley University

*Paper 3 of the dissertation. Full draft; recast as a formal-model and computational study. The model is Paper 2's; this paper computes it across the complete space of three-node forms, across a parametric population of simulated organizations, and across a typology of named real organizations, and then tests its central structural prediction in a controlled agent-based experiment. Bibliography (21 references) verified for citation integrity. All Φ values computed; reproduce with the scripts named in §8.*

## Abstract

Paper 2 built a formal model that classifies whether a coordination form is triadic, where Φ = 0 marks a form that factors into its parties and Φ > 0 a form that does not. A binary classification is not a graded scale, and this paper asks how far the model can be pushed toward one: it computes Φ across the complete space of three-node forms, across a parametric population of eighty-six simulated organizations spanning party count, mediation regime, determination function, and back-channel topology, and across a typology of named real organizations, and it tests the model's central structural prediction behaviorally. Three things hold robustly. The binary dyad/triad classification is clean. The mediation regime orders as the construct predicts, with strictly mediated forms, in which the parties reach each other only through a joint determination, carrying the highest demand once the determination function is controlled. And a controlled coordination experiment confirms that prediction: independent learning agents coordinate far less successfully through a strict joint determination with no direct channel than through the same determination with a channel left open, a large and robust effect. One thing does not hold: Φ as a fine-grained graded scale. Magnitude is dominated by the determination function, and a monotone disjunctive determination with dense coupling inflates Φ to values an order of magnitude above any real organization, while a parity determination is anomalously easy to coordinate through despite a mid-range score. We therefore present Φ as a structural classifier and an ordinal, regime-level indicator of triadic demand rather than as a precise scale, and we locate the named organizations in the moderate band of the population, well below the structurally-possible but organization-less high-Φ tail. The seat of the mediator, human or algorithm, does not set the modeled demand; the determination structure does.

## 1. Introduction

Consider two coordination problems. In the first, a rideshare driver waits for a dispatch while, miles away, a rider opens an app and requests a car; the two never choose each other and never meet until the trip begins, and the only thing that passes between them is the match the platform commits. In the second, a plaintiff and a defendant contest a case; they address each other only through what a judge admits and rules, and the only thing that passes between them is the determination the court commits. The two settings look nothing alike. One is a private platform run by software, the other a public institution run by a person. Yet structurally they are the same coordination: two parties bound together through a third that commits a determination neither controls and both must reason through. A model of coordination, rather than of industry or technology, should see that sameness and score the two settings together. This paper builds such a model, places organizations on it, and tests its central claim.

Paper 1 named a coordination form that the existing constructs do not see, in which a worker coordinates with a counterpart through a determination a system commits between them. Paper 2 built a formal model that classifies whether a given form is of this kind, by computing whether the modeled cause-effect structure is irreducible to the parties; the classification is binary, triadic or dyadic. A classification is useful, but it withholds degree: it says that a form does not reduce to its parts without saying how far from reducible it is, or how one irreducible form compares with another. The demand a coordination form makes is, intuitively, a matter of degree, and the work of this paper is to ask how much of that degree the model can actually support — and to be exact about where it can and cannot.

The destination that organizes the work is the readability score. A readability formula takes a feature of a text and returns a number that places it on a scale of reading difficulty (Flesch, 1948; Kincaid et al., 1975); the number is a property of the text, and it earns its standing in two stages, first computed from the text alone and then, separately, validated by showing that texts which score as more difficult are in fact comprehended less well, across readers, on average (DuBay, 2004; Crossley et al., 2017). This paper builds the form-level score for coordination and takes the first step into the second stage with a controlled test, while being explicit about what the test does and does not license. Naming the destination fixes what kind of object the score is, a property of the form and silent about any individual, and what it would take for it to become a validated measure.

The contribution is threefold and the honesty is part of it. We compute the model across three increasingly concrete domains: the complete space of three-node forms in the modeling vocabulary, which characterizes what the model does across its whole domain; a parametric population of simulated organizations, which is the empirical body of the paper and the source of its central limitation; and a typology of named real organizations, which locates familiar cases. We then test the model's load-bearing prediction, that strictly mediated coordination is harder to coordinate through than the same coordination with a direct channel, in an agent-based experiment. The result is that the binary classification and the regime-level ordering are robust and the prediction holds behaviorally, while the graded magnitude is not a reliable scale, because it is dominated by the determination function in ways that do not track coordination demand. The paper that follows is therefore a measured one: a structural classifier and an ordinal indicator, validated at its core and bounded at its edges, rather than a finished readability score for coordination.

## 2. The coordination form as unit

The readability analogy fixes both what the model is and what it is not. A readability formula is validated against a criterion at the level of the text, and it can be strong at the form level while predicting only weakly whether a given reader understands a given sentence, because individual comprehension is driven by much besides the text (Zheng & Yu, 2017; Lenzner, 2014). That is not a defect; it is a consequence of what the formula measures, a property of the form, which is one input among many to any individual outcome. We borrow that lesson about the unit. We do not borrow the claim to have completed the decades of validation that gave the readability formulas their authority. What we take is the shape of the eventual measure, a form-level score validated against an outcome, and we are building and beginning to test its first half.

The unit is the coordination form, not the person. The variance puzzle that opened Paper 1, the question of why two workers facing the same coordination fare differently, is not this paper's question; it is the question for the matched, person-level instrument the research program builds later, the supply-side sibling of this supply-independent model. A readability score is the sibling of a reading-ability test, and the text score comes first because it does not require a sample of people. This paper's restraint about individuals is the restraint a readability paper shows when it declines to predict whether a given reader will understand a given passage.

The triad the model scores is not a coinage of this dissertation. Simmel observed that a third party does not merely add to a dyad but transforms it, and he distinguished the roles the third can play, among them the disinterested mediator who joins two parties who would not otherwise be joined (Simmel, 1950); organizational research has nonetheless concentrated on dyads, and the triad is the standing corrective (Nooteboom, 2006). Of the forms a triad can take, the relevant one is mediation, in which a relationship between two parties is constituted through a third, as distinct from brokerage and coalition (Siltaloppi & Vargo, 2017). The model is built for the mediation triad, and the platform economy has made it the common form of coordination in ordinary working life.

Positioning the model against the way organizations are usually classified keeps it from reading as a rival taxonomy. Organizations have long been sorted by coordination mode: market, hybrid, and hierarchy (Williamson, 1991); the network as a third mode (Powell, 1990); the coordination mechanisms of mutual adjustment, supervision, and standardization (Mintzberg, 1980); and, closest to a graded scheme, the value-chain governance forms ranged by degree of explicit coordination (Gereffi et al., 2005). The model is orthogonal to these. It does not propose a new category of organization; it places any organization, however the existing schemes classify it, on one continuum according to how irreducibly its modeled coordination runs through a mediator. The instrument is Paper 2's, which derives Φ as the system-level integrated information of the application-layer transition matrix over its minimum-information partition (Albantakis et al., 2023; Marshall et al., 2023); we reuse it and do not re-derive it.

One feature of the coordination form makes the enterprise possible and separates it from an attempt to model the algorithm: the form is observable. The determination structure the model scores is read from the record of what the mediator commits and how the parties respond, which is what interaction logs contain. The model runs over the application layer the parties operate in, not the proprietary mechanism that produces the determinations, so the opacity that defeats approaches aimed at the algorithm itself is beside the point for a model that scores the structure of the determinations, because that structure shows in the record whatever produced it.

## 3. Model and methods

### 3.1 The model

A coordination form is represented as an application-layer system of nodes: a worker (W), a mediating system (S), and one or more counterparts (C), with a node per party. Each node carries a determination-bearing value, and the transition matrix records how each node's next value follows from the current configuration of all nodes. States are individuated by Paper 2's pre-registered rule: a new state begins when the mediator commits a determination that alters its causal disposition toward the parties. Over that matrix the model computes exact IIT-4.0 system Φ, the irreducibility of the cause-effect state over its minimum-information partition, by way of the repository's exact-Φ oracle, which wraps PyPhi (Oizumi et al., 2014; Mayner et al., 2018). As Paper 2 establishes, this is a modeling choice and not a claimed identity: the score is a property of the model that three choices define — the worker–system–counterpart representation, the state-individuation rule, and the party partition — and a different defensible choice could change it.

### 3.2 The catalog: the complete space of three-node forms

A three-node form is fully specified by how each node's next value depends on the other two, and there are sixteen Boolean functions of two inputs, so the complete space is the sixteen-cubed set of wirings, 4,096 in all. We enumerate every one, compute its Φ, and deduplicate identical transition matrices. Most of these wirings are not recognizable coordination forms; the enumeration is a coverage check, not a census of coordination. Its value is that it characterizes what the model does across its whole three-node domain and shows where the interpretable cases fall within it.

### 3.3 A population of simulated organizations

The empirical body of the paper is a parametric population of simulated organizations, generated to be interpretable rather than arbitrary: each corresponds to a recognizable organizational type, and the population spans the design space along four dimensions. The first is **party count**, with one mediator and one to three counterparts plus the worker, giving systems of three to five nodes. The second is **mediation regime**: dyadic, in which the parties coordinate directly and the mediator is not constitutive; strict, in which the parties reach each other only through the mediator; and partial, in which the mediator matches the parties but a direct back-channel is left open. The third is the **determination function**, the rule by which the mediator commits over the parties, drawn from conjunction (AND), disjunction (OR), majority (MAJ), parity (XOR), and a two-of count (THRESH2). The fourth is the **back-channel topology** for partially mediated forms, the set of direct party-to-party links, enumerated exhaustively where the party count is small and sampled across a spread of densities where it is large. Each generated organization is built into its transition matrix and scored by exact Φ, and we record its design parameters and structural features so the analysis can ask what drives the score. The procedure yields eighty-six distinct simulated organizations after deduplication; it is `simulated_orgs.py`.

### 3.4 The typology of named organizations

Alongside the population we model thirteen named real organizations, each as a worker–system–counterpart system whose determination structure is fixed before Φ is computed, derived from how the organization actually coordinates its parties. The modeling choice that does the most work is whether a form is strictly or partially mediated. A strictly mediated form keeps the two parties apart, so they reach each other only through the determination; an algorithmic dispatch platform, an applicant-tracking system, a content-moderation queue, and a court are modeled this way, and each returns Φ = 2.0. A partially mediated form matches the parties but leaves them a direct channel; an online freelance marketplace, a staffing agency, and a real-estate broker are modeled this way, and the added dependence lowers the score to Φ = 0.83. A dyadic baseline decouples the counterpart and returns Φ = 0. The human-mediated cases are included deliberately as the test of orthogonality (Khurana, 2002): if the model responds to triadic structure rather than to algorithms, a court should be modeled like a platform of the same structure.

### 3.5 A test of the structural axis

The catalog, the population, and the typology are computed; none shows that the score tracks anything a person experiences. The model's central prediction — that strict mediation, which routes the parties through a joint determination with no direct channel, is harder to coordinate through than the same determination with a channel left open — is the kind of claim that can be put to a behavioral test, and we run one. Building the structure rather than inferring it turns the three modeling choices into exact controls: the representation is what we construct, one round of play is one state, and the three roles are the parties over which irreducibility is read. Party count is held fixed at three, and the only thing that varies across conditions is the determination structure, so the test isolates the model's novel content, the structure axis, rather than the party-count axis that the cut rideshare analysis could only reach.

Two independent reinforcement-learning agents play a worker and a counterpart who must coordinate on a target the worker holds and the counterpart can recover only through whatever channel the structure permits. The agents' learning rule is fixed in advance and identical across conditions, and the agents never see Φ, the condition, or each other's policy, so any relationship between Φ and their performance is a genuine test rather than a built-in result. Four conditions, all three-party, span the model's bands: a dyadic baseline with a direct channel (Φ = 0), a parity determination (Φ = 0.5), partial mediation with a back-channel (Φ = 0.83), and strict mediation with none (Φ = 2.0). Difficulty is one minus the agents' asymptotic coordination success; the full specification and the frozen agent rule are in the experiment directory. The prediction, fixed before running, is that strict mediation is the hardest, and in particular that removing the back-channel while holding the determination fixed (Φ 0.83 → 2.0) raises difficulty.

### 3.6 What we report

For the catalog and the population we report the distribution of Φ, group means by the design dimensions, and an ordinary least-squares model of Φ on those dimensions, to show which features move the score and how much of its variance a structural account captures. We carry one published critique into the reporting deliberately. Cerullo (2015) showed that trivially regular structures can carry very high Φ, so a high Φ does not certify sophisticated coordination; the population is, in part, an empirical test of how badly that bites. We report effect sizes and the share of variance explained rather than significance, since the units here are computed rather than sampled.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 The Φ landscape of the model family

Across all 4,096 three-node wirings, 44.1 percent are reducible and score zero: most ways of coupling three nodes do not produce an irreducible structure. The non-zero wirings fall onto seven discrete bands, and the levels the named organizations occupy — zero, one-half, roughly five-sixths, and two — are populated bands of this family rather than levels chosen to receive them. A structural model of Φ over the family's features recovers only about a fifth of its variance, so the score is not a relabeled feature count; it carries an irreducibility that no single feature reproduces. Already at this level the determination function matters more than anything else: among genuine two-party mediators, parity determinations score the highest, the first sign of the magnitude problem the population makes severe.

### 4.2 The population of simulated organizations

The eighty-six simulated organizations span party counts three to five and Φ from zero to twenty, with a median of 0.50 and a mean of 1.37; a quarter are reducible. Three readings of the population carry the paper.

First, **the mediation regime orders as the construct predicts, once the determination function is controlled.** Dyadically coordinated forms are uniformly reducible (Φ = 0), because nothing constitutes a third party. In an ordinary least-squares model of Φ on the design dimensions, which recovers about forty-eight percent of the variance, the strict regime carries a positive coefficient and the partial regime a negative one relative to the dyadic baseline: holding the determination and the size fixed, strictly mediated coordination is scored as the most demanding, and partial mediation, which leaves a back-channel, less so. This is the dissertation's central structural claim, now visible across a population rather than at a handful of points.

Second, and decisively for how the paper must present its scale, **the magnitude of Φ is dominated by the determination function in a way that does not track coordination demand.** Grouped by determination, the mean Φ runs from 0.37 for majority and 0.57 for conjunction up to 4.26 for disjunction; the disjunctive (OR) determination, especially when paired with a dense back-channel, drives Φ to four, six, twelve, and in one five-party case twenty. There is nothing about a disjunctive determination — a mediator that commits when either party signals — that makes the coordination it sits inside an order of magnitude more demanding than a conjunctive one. The inflation is a property of the measure, not of the coordination: a densely coupled, monotone system carries enormous integrated information for reasons Cerullo (2015) identified, and those reasons are orthogonal to how hard the parties find it to coordinate. The population turns Paper 2's hedge about magnitude into a demonstrated limit.

Third, **the named real organizations occupy the moderate region of the population, and the extreme-Φ tail corresponds to no recognizable organization.** The dyadic baselines sit at the floor, the partially mediated forms near the population's median, and the strictly mediated platforms and the court near the upper end of the *real* range, around the eighty-seventh percentile, with the higher-order pooled and crowdwork forms just above. The structurally-possible forms above them — the disjunctive, densely coupled systems at Φ from four to twenty — are not organizations anyone would recognize; they are what the model produces when coupling is maximized for its own sake. That the real cases cluster in the moderate band, and the pathological scores belong to forms with no organizational referent, is itself the cleanest statement of where the model is trustworthy: it discriminates and orders the recognizable cases, and it misbehaves only out in a region the recognizable cases never reach.

### 4.3 The typology on the bands

The thirteen named organizations fall on populated bands of the family and at sensible places in the population: dyadic baselines at zero, the freelance marketplace, staffing agency, and broker at 0.83 (partial mediation), and rideshare, food delivery, applicant-tracking, content moderation, and the court at 2.0 (strict mediation), with pooled rideshare and crowdwork at 3.0. The placements are model outputs, and that several organizations share a score is a finding rather than a defect: the model's claim is precisely that forms which couple their parties the same way receive the same score, whatever their industry.

### 4.4 Structure, not the mediator's seat

The human-mediated forms interleave with the algorithmic ones at every level, sorted by structure, and because this is a statement about what the model computes it needs no outcome data to stand. A court, in which two parties reach each other only through what a judge admits and rules, is modeled at 2.0, the same as a rideshare dispatch, an applicant-tracking system, and a content-moderation queue. A staffing agency and a real-estate broker, which match two parties but leave them a direct channel, are modeled at 0.83, the same as an online freelance marketplace. The same procedure that scores Uber scores a courtroom and puts them at the same place. The model responds to determination structure and is indifferent to whether the mediator is software or a person.

### 4.5 The structure axis, tested

The experiment supports the model's central structural prediction and, in the same stroke, confirms the caution about magnitude. Strict mediation is markedly harder to coordinate through than any other condition: the agents reach the coordinated outcome about seventy-one percent of the time under strict mediation against about ninety-five percent under each of the other three, a gap that is large (Cohen's d near twenty-nine) and that persists when the agents are trained far longer, so it reflects a genuine information ceiling rather than insufficient learning. The decisive contrast is the one the dissertation's argument rests on: holding the determination fixed and removing only the direct back-channel, the move from partial to strict mediation (Φ 0.83 → 2.0) drops coordination success by about a quarter. The form the model scores as most demanding is, behaviorally, the hardest to coordinate through, and for the reason the construct names: the parties can reach each other only through a joint determination they cannot route around.

The result is partial in a way that is itself informative, and it agrees with the population. Difficulty is not monotone in Φ across the four conditions: parity (Φ = 0.5) is as easy as the dyadic baseline, because its determination is invertible and the counterpart can recover the target exactly. A higher Φ therefore does not by itself mean harder coordination; what makes the strict case hard is that its joint determination loses information neither party can restore. This is the Cerullo caution made empirical at the level of behavior, the same conclusion the population reached at the level of magnitude: the binary distinction and the regime-level ordering hold, while the graded number does not track difficulty uniformly. It is one in-silico test of one contrast, with agents rather than people; its weight and its limits are read together in the Discussion.

## 5. Discussion

The paper's findings sort cleanly into what the model can be trusted to do and what it cannot, and stating the line precisely is the contribution. What it can do is classify and order. The binary dyad/triad distinction is clean across the whole space of forms; the mediation regime orders as the construct predicts across a population once the determination function is controlled; and the regime-level prediction that strict mediation is the most demanding is borne out behaviorally, with a large effect, in a non-circular experiment. What it cannot do, at least as the scalar Φ now stands, is serve as a fine-grained graded scale. Its magnitude is dominated by the determination function, with a disjunctive determination inflating it an order of magnitude beyond any real organization, and a parity determination scoring in the middle while coordinating as easily as a dyad. The honest object the paper delivers is therefore a structural classifier and an ordinal, regime-level indicator of triadic demand, not a readability score; the readability score remains the destination, and the magnitude problem is the obstacle between here and there.

That the real organizations cluster in the moderate band, and the pathological scores belong to forms with no organizational referent, is what makes the classifier and the ordinal indicator usable in practice despite the magnitude problem. An analyst handed a recognizable organization is working in the region where the model discriminates and orders sensibly; the inflation lives in a corner of the design space — maximally coupled, monotone determinations — that recognizable coordination does not occupy. The limitation is real and it bounds the claim, but it does not reach into the cases the construct is about.

The result situates the model against the way organizations are usually classified, and the cross-cutting is the clearest evidence that it registers something the existing schemes do not. By governance form, a court is a hierarchy, a staffing agency and a broker are hybrids, and a rideshare platform is a market with an active operator (Williamson, 1991; Powell, 1990); these are three different boxes. On the model the court and the platform share a score and the two hybrids separate from each other according to whether the intermediary keeps its parties apart, an ordering that crosses the governance boxes at every turn because it tracks how irreducibly the coordination runs through the mediator rather than the governance form. The value-chain governance grades graded coordination by degree (Gereffi et al., 2005); this paper supplies a structural model of the degree, honest about the part of that degree it cannot yet measure.

Borrowing an integrated-information measure outside consciousness is established but the borrowing comes with exactly this risk, and naming the prior work is part of taking it seriously. Engel and Malone (2018) computed a measure of this family on work groups and decoupled its usefulness from any consciousness reading; the difference here is a structure with an asymmetric mediator at its center rather than a symmetric group. The magnitude problem points to the natural refinement: a single scalar Φ aggregates heterogeneous modes of integration that a decomposition into synergistic and redundant parts would separate (Mediano et al., 2025), and it is plausible that such a decomposition would discount the disjunctive and parity cases that the scalar over-credits. That is the measure-development path the population study makes concrete, and it is future work rather than a result of this paper.

The model also gives the dissertation's larger argument a quantity, read correctly as a model output. Paper 2 found that a form's modeled irreducibility is greatest when the two parties are kept from coordinating directly; this paper shows the regime-level version of that across a population and confirms it behaviorally. The difference between a marketplace that lets a client and a contractor talk and a dispatch platform that does not is, in the model and now in the experiment, a real difference in how hard the coordination is to carry through. As a claim about platform design this is suggestive and now partly tested; as a precise quantity it should be read at the level of the regime, not the decimal.

The analogy to readability should be held to its limits, which are larger than an over-eager draft would suggest. Readability formulas earned their authority by repeated validation against comprehension across corpora and populations (Crossley et al., 2017; Lenzner, 2014; Zheng & Yu, 2017). This model has been put to a single controlled test of its central prediction, with agents, and that prediction held; it has not been validated against real coordination outcomes across organizations or with human participants, and its graded magnitude is, on the present evidence, not yet a scale. The path forward is an empirical program that varies determination structure at a fixed party count against observed outcomes, a human version of the experiment run here, and a decomposition of the measure that resolves the determination-function confound. The model is built and its core is tested; what remains is to validate it more fully and to fix the magnitude.

## 6. Limitations

The model scores the form and not the person, and every claim is about a coordination form as the model represents it; the person-level instrument is named as next work. The catalog, the population, and the typology are internal to the model and are not an outcome validation. Section 4.5 adds one targeted test, a controlled coordination experiment that confirms the central structural prediction in silico, but that is a single in-silico test of one contrast, with agents rather than people, and it neither validates the graded magnitude nor establishes that the model tracks outcomes across real organizations. An earlier version of this work attempted a broader validation against rideshare pooling data; it was cut because, in the pooling model, Φ is a linear function of pool size, so the relationship validated only the party-count axis and not the structural axis that is the model's novel content. That analysis is retained outside the dissertation's claims as a template for the structure-varying validation the model actually requires.

The sharpest limitation is the one the population makes undeniable: **the magnitude of Φ is not a reliable demand scale.** It is dominated by the determination function, with a disjunctive determination and dense coupling inflating it to values an order of magnitude above any real organization, for reasons (Cerullo, 2015) that are orthogonal to coordination difficulty; the behavioral experiment confirms the same failure at the parity case. We therefore claim only the binary classification and the ordinal, regime-level ordering, and read magnitude no more finely than that. The three modeling choices are load-bearing, and a different defensible choice could change the scores. The higher-order forms are modeled with four nodes, so their scores are not strictly comparable in magnitude to the three-node forms. The granularity discipline bounds the state alphabet, applied uniformly so the scores compare.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper takes Paper 2's binary classification and asks how far it can be made graded, by computing Φ across the complete space of three-node forms, across a population of eighty-six simulated organizations, and across a typology of named real organizations, and by testing the model's central prediction in a controlled experiment. The binary dyad/triad classification is robust; the mediation regime orders as the construct predicts, with strict mediation the most demanding, across the population and behaviorally in the experiment; and the named organizations fall in the moderate, well-ordered region of the population. The graded magnitude is not a reliable scale, because it is dominated by the determination function in ways that do not track coordination demand, and so the model is best presented as a structural classifier and an ordinal regime-level indicator rather than a readability score. The path to the readability score runs through a fuller, human validation and a decomposition of the measure that resolves the determination-function confound. Paper 1 named the triad, Paper 2 modeled whether a form is one, and this paper grades the model across organizations, tests its central prediction, and marks exactly where the grading can be trusted and where it cannot; what remains is to validate it more fully and to measure the worker.

## 8. Data and code availability

Every Φ value is computed, and all scripts use the repository's exact-Φ oracle (`proxy_audit.exact_phi`), which wraps PyPhi (Mayner et al., 2018), under `~/iit-playground/venv-4.0/bin/python`. The complete three-node family (§4.1) is `catalog.py` with `analyze_catalog.py`; the simulated-organization population (§3.3, §4.2) is `simulated_orgs.py` (party counts to five with `--with-n5`) with `analyze_simulated.py`, writing `results/simulated_orgs.csv` and the population figure; the typology of named organizations (§3.4, §4.3) is `typology_phi.py`. The structure-axis test (§3.5, §4.5) is `experiment/sim/coordination_sim.py`, with the frozen a-priori agent rule in its docstring, and `experiment/analyze.py`; the design, conditions, and results write-up are in `experiment/`. The exploratory rideshare analysis cut from the dissertation's claims is retained, clearly labeled, in `exploratory/`, with a note explaining why it validates only the party-count axis.
