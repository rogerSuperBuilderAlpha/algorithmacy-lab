# A Readability Score for Coordination: Calibrating Integrated Information as a Measure of Triadic Demand Across Organizations

**Roger Hunt III**
Bentley University

*Paper 3 of the dissertation. Draft. Consolidated bibliography and citation-integrity pass pending.*

## Abstract

Paper 2 built an instrument that decides whether a coordination form is triadic, but a verdict is not a scale: it says that a form is irreducible without saying how much. This paper calibrates the instrument into a scale and reads organizations on it. We treat integrated information, Φ, as a readability score for coordination. The procedure maps any organization's coordination to a three-party application layer of worker, system, and counterpart, computes Φ from the determination structure alone, and places the organization on one continuum of triadic demand. We anchor the scale against an observed coordination outcome in rideshare pooling, where the platform coordinates a driver and several riders who never meet. Across pool sizes, Φ computed without reference to the data tracks the observed friction of the coordination (a correlation of +0.98 over the aggregate forms; a trip-level slope of +16.8 seconds per mile for each unit of Φ), and the relation is stable across time windows and holds within community areas. The calibrated model then places a typology of organizations across five structural levels, from dyadic baselines at zero to higher-order forms at three. A human-mediated court scores the same as an algorithmic platform of the same structure, and a staffing agency scores the same as an online freelance marketplace. The seat of the mediator, human or algorithm, does not set the demand; the determination structure does. The score is a property of the coordination form, not of any individual who works through it.

## 1. Introduction

Paper 1 named a coordination form that the existing constructs do not see, in which a worker coordinates with a counterpart through a determination a system commits between them. Paper 2 built an instrument that decides whether a given form is of this kind, by computing whether its cause-effect structure is irreducible to the parties. That instrument returns a verdict: triadic or dyadic, irreducible or factored. A verdict is useful, but it is not yet a scale. It tells an analyst that a form does not reduce to its parts; it does not tell her how far from reducible the form is, or how one irreducible form compares with another. The demand a coordination form makes is a matter of degree, and a measure of that demand has to be calibrated before its numbers mean anything.

The model for what calibration looks like is the readability score. A readability formula takes a feature of a text, such as its sentence and word lengths, and returns a number that places the text on a scale of reading difficulty (Flesch, 1948; Kincaid et al., 1975). The number is a property of the text, not of any reader, and it earns its standing by tracking an observed outcome: texts that score as more difficult are in fact comprehended less well, across readers, on average (DuBay, 2004; Crossley et al., 2017). A readability score says nothing about whether a particular person will understand a particular passage. It says how much reading competence the text demands, as a property of the text. That is exactly the kind of measure this paper builds for coordination.

We commit to the unit at the outset and defend it in Section 2: the scale measures the coordination form, not the person. The contribution is a portable model that maps any organization's coordination to a worker-system-counterpart application layer, computes Φ from the determination structure, and reads the organization's place on a calibrated scale of triadic demand. The work runs in two stages. We first anchor the scale against an observed coordination outcome in one data-rich domain, so that the numbers are calibrated rather than asserted. We then place a typology of organizations on the calibrated scale, spanning algorithmic platforms, institutional gatekeepers, human-mediated intermediaries, and dyadic baselines. The headline of the second stage is that the model is orthogonal to whether a mediator is software: a human-mediated court scores the same as an algorithmic platform of the same structure. Structure, not the seat of the mediator, sets the demand.

A measure that decides whether coordination is triadic becomes useful when it says how triadic. This paper calibrates it into a scale and reads organizations on it.

## 2. The Coordination Form as Unit

The readability analogy is worth drawing in full, because it fixes both what the measure is and what it is not. A readability formula is validated against a criterion: the formula's scores are correlated with measured comprehension, with cloze-test performance, or with the grade level at which a text is in fact understood (DuBay, 2004). The validation is at the level of the text. A formula can correlate well with comprehension across a corpus of texts while predicting only weakly whether a given reader understands a given sentence, because individual comprehension is driven by much besides the text (Zheng et al., 2017; Lenzner, 2014). This is not a defect of the formula; it is a consequence of what the formula measures. It measures a property of the form, and the form is one input among many to any individual outcome.

Our measure has the same character, and the same scope. Claim A of this paper is that a triadic-demand score is a property of the coordination form, validated against how coordination through the form actually goes, and that it carries no claim about any individual who works through the form. The variance puzzle that opened Paper 1, the question of why two workers facing the same coordination fare differently, is not this paper's question. It is the question for the matched, person-level instrument that the research program builds later, the supply-side sibling of this supply-independent measure. A readability score is the sibling of a reading-ability test, and the text score comes first; the same order holds here. A reviewer who asks where the individuals are in this paper is asking for the sibling instrument, and the honest answer is that it is the next paper, not this one.

The triad that the measure scores is not a coinage of this dissertation. It is a century-old object. Simmel observed that a third party does not merely add to a dyad but transforms it, and he distinguished the roles the third can play, among them the disinterested mediator who joins two parties who would not otherwise be joined (Simmel, 1950). The observation that organizational research has nonetheless concentrated on dyads, and that the triad is the standing corrective, has been made directly in organizational economics (Nooteboom, 2006). Of the forms a triad can take, the relevant one here is mediation, in which a relationship between two parties is constituted through a third, as distinct from brokerage, in which the third acts on two others, and coalition, in which three parties align (Siltaloppi & Vargo, 2017). The measure scores the mediation triad.

Positioning the measure against the way organizations are usually classified is what keeps it from reading as a rival taxonomy. Organizations have long been sorted by coordination mode: market, hybrid, and hierarchy, distinguished by their coordinating and control mechanisms (Williamson, 1991); the network as a third mode beyond market and hierarchy (Powell, 1990); the coordination mechanisms of mutual adjustment, supervision, and standardization (Mintzberg, 1980); and, closest to a graded scheme, the value-chain governance forms ranged by their degree of explicit coordination (Gereffi et al., 2005). The scale this paper builds is orthogonal to these schemes. It does not propose a new category of organization. It places any organization, however the existing schemes already classify it, on one continuum according to how irreducibly its coordination runs through a mediator. The instrument that computes the placement is Paper 2's, which derives Φ as the system-level integrated information of the application-layer transition matrix over its minimum-information partition; we reuse it here and do not re-derive it.

The scale measures the form the way a readability score measures a text, and like a readability score, it is silent about any one reader.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 The model

The model is a procedure that takes an organization and returns a comparable number. An organization's coordination is represented as an application-layer system of nodes: the worker (W), the mediating system (S), and the counterpart (C), with a fourth or further node added when a determination binds more than three parties. Each node carries a determination-bearing value, and the system's transition matrix records how each node's next value follows from the current configuration of all nodes. States are individuated by the pre-registered rule of Paper 2: a new application-layer state begins when the mediator commits a determination that alters its causal disposition toward the parties. Over the resulting transition matrix the model computes exact IIT-4.0 system integrated information, the irreducibility of the system's cause-effect state over its minimum-information partition, by way of the repository's exact-Φ oracle, which wraps the standard PyPhi implementation (Marshall et al., 2023; Mayner et al., 2018). The quantity is the system-level φ over the minimum-information partition, not the structure-level sum, and the paper's claims attach to it.

The procedure is applied uniformly. The same individuation rule, the same node convention, and the same granularity discipline are used for every organization, and that uniformity is what makes the scores comparable across organizations that share no common vocabulary. The granularity discipline is the constraint that Φ grows steeply with the number of states, so the application-layer alphabet must stay small; the individuation rule keeps it small in a principled way, because only determination-bearing transitions count. The instrument is gated on the controls Paper 2 reported: a form in which a party is causally decoupled returns Φ = 0, and a fully coupled form returns Φ > 0 that no partition reduces. We do not re-run those controls here; we inherit them.

### 3.2 Hypotheses

We fix the hypotheses before computation, in the spirit of validation as hypothesis testing (Landy, 1986). The affirmative hypothesis, H1, is that Φ computed from determination structure tracks observed coordination difficulty: where the structure makes a greater coordination demand, the coordination is harder to realize and costlier to carry out, so Φ correlates positively with friction and negatively with achievement. The structurally-expected null, H0, is the informative one. It is not that Φ is unrelated to the outcome; it is that Φ tracks only the number of parties bound, and not the determination structure. H0 is the claim that a party count would do as well as Φ. The typology refutes it directly, because Φ separates forms that bind the same number of parties (a partial-mediation triad scores 0.83 and a strict-mediation triad scores 2.0, both with three parties), which a party count cannot do. We use the phrase "claims fixed before computation" rather than "pre-registered," because there is no registry record.

### 3.3 The validation framing and the anchor data

Stage 1 is a concurrent criterion-validity demonstration. A new measure, Φ, is correlated with an external criterion, an observed coordination outcome, on the same units (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955; Alavi et al., 2023). The nearest precedent for the specific move, validating an information-theoretic measure of a work structure against a coordination outcome, is the validation of an effective-complexity measure against the time taken to complete development activities (Schlick et al., 2015).

The anchor domain is rideshare pooling, and the dataset is the City of Chicago's "Transportation Network Providers — Trips" record for 2018 through 2022 (Socrata resource m6dm-c72p), a public, trip-level dataset. We use the subset of trips for which sharing was authorized. Pooling is the apt anchor because it is the constitutive triad: when a rider authorizes sharing, the platform coordinates a driver and one or more other riders who never meet and never choose one another, entirely through the dispatch it commits, and the platform's design keeps the parties from reaching one another directly (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016; Stark & Pais, 2020). The dataset records completed trips only, so the available coordination outcome is pooling itself rather than cancellation or wait. We read the outcome two ways: the achievement share, the fraction of authorized trips that reach a given pool size, and the friction, the trip seconds per mile, which rises as the platform must route a car through additional pickups and drop-offs. Chicago's shared rides were active through this window and were suspended in March 2020, so the calibration period is pre-2020. The dataset is the calibration weight for the scale, not the subject of the paper.

### 3.4 Stage-1 modeling and the typology

Each pool size is a distinct coordination form. A pool of k riders, with the driver and the platform, is a system of k + 2 nodes in which the platform's dispatch is a joint function of every party and each party follows the dispatch. The Φ of each pool size is computed from this structure before any contact with the data, and it is Φ = k + 1: a solo authorized trip scores 2.0, a two-rider pool scores 3.0, a three-rider pool scores 4.0, and a four-rider pool scores 5.0. The prediction that follows is that larger pools, scoring higher, are harder to realize and costlier to carry out.

For the typology, each organization is modeled as a worker-system-counterpart system whose determination structure is fixed in the analysis code before Φ is computed, and the structure is derived from how the organization actually coordinates its parties rather than chosen to produce a number. The modeling choice that does the most work is whether a form is strictly or partially mediated. A strictly mediated form keeps the two parties apart, so they reach each other only through the determination; an algorithmic dispatch platform, an applicant-tracking system, a content-moderation queue, and a court are of this kind. A partially mediated form matches the parties but then leaves them a direct channel; an online freelance marketplace, a staffing agency, and a real-estate broker are of this kind, because the worker and the counterpart coordinate directly once the match is made. The human-mediated cases are included deliberately as the test of orthogonality: market intermediaries have been analyzed as Simmel triads before (Khurana, 2002), and if the measure scores triadic coordination rather than algorithms, a human-mediated form should score the same as an algorithmic one of the same structure.

### 3.5 Statistics and robustness

The anchor is reported as a correlation across the aggregate pool-size forms and, because that correlation rests on only four aggregate points, as a trip-level regression of friction on Φ across all trips, which supplies the effect size and the power. The robustness battery follows the conventions for a quantitative claim at large N. We split the sample at its median date to test stability across time. We recompute the relation within community areas to test whether it survives stratification rather than arising from pooling across heterogeneous areas, a Simpson's-paradox check. We recompute friction under alternative aggregations. We report the effect size and the variance explained rather than the p-value, because at a sample of this size significance is uninformative. Two controls are labeled. The negative control is the set of dyadic baselines, forms that should score zero and do. The positive control is the demonstration that Φ separates forms at a fixed number of parties, which shows that Φ carries information a party count does not, and which is the direct answer to the concern that the anchor's relation runs through party count.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 The anchor

Table 1 reports the anchor over a sample of 193,028 authorized shared trips spanning the pooling era, with outliers filtered to plausible ranges. Φ, computed from structure with no reference to the data, orders the pooling forms exactly as the observed difficulty does, on both the cost axis and the rarity axis.

**Table 1. The calibration anchor (Chicago rideshare pooling).**

| Pool size | Model Φ | Trips | Achievement share | Friction (sec/mi) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (solo) | 2.0 | 66,300 | 33.1% | 162 |
| 2 | 3.0 | 70,700 | 35.3% | 188 |
| 3 | 4.0 | 51,300 | 25.6% | 223 |
| 4 | 5.0 | 12,000 | 5.9% | 231 |

On the cost axis, friction rises monotonically with Φ, from 162 to 188 to 223 to 231 seconds per mile, a correlation of +0.98 across the four forms. Because four points is a thin basis for a correlation, we regress friction on Φ at the level of the individual trip, across all 193,028 trips. The slope is +16.8 seconds per mile for each unit of Φ, with a trip-level correlation of +0.14 and a coefficient of determination of 0.019. The aggregate relation is strong and the per-trip relation is small but robustly positive, which is the expected signature of a form-level measure: Φ predicts a form's difficulty well and predicts an individual trip's pace weakly, because a single trip's pace is dominated by traffic and route. This is the readability pattern, and it reinforces rather than weakens Claim A.

On the rarity axis, the achievement share falls as Φ rises, from a third of authorized trips at the solo and two-rider forms to six percent at the four-rider form. A regression of the log share on Φ has a slope of −0.54, so each unit of Φ multiplies the rate at which the platform realizes the coordination by about 0.58, roughly halving it per step of demand, with a correlation of −0.88. Two-thirds of authorized trips were matched into a pool of at least two.

### 4.2 Robustness

The relation is stable. Splitting the sample at its median date, the aggregate correlation is +0.976 in the earlier window and +0.975 in the later, and the trip-level slope is +16.5 and +15.2. The relation is not an artifact of pooling across heterogeneous areas: friction rises monotonically with pool size within seven of the eight highest-volume community areas, so the trend holds inside strata. The relation is robust to how friction is aggregated, with a correlation of +0.98 under the trip-weighted aggregate, +0.95 under the mean of per-trip pace, and +0.94 under the median. At a sample of 193,028, significance is trivial and uninformative; the effect of record is the slope and the variance it explains.

### 4.3 The typology

Table 2 places the typology on the calibrated scale. Five structural levels emerge from the determination structures, and the level is set by the structure, not by the industry.

**Table 2. The typology on the triadic-demand scale.**

| Φ | Organizations | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| 0.00 | Direct exchange; chat with a model | dyadic; no constitutive mediator |
| 0.50 | Complementary-match marketplace | parity determination |
| 0.83 | Freelance marketplace; staffing agency; real-estate broker | partial mediation |
| 2.00 | Rideshare; food delivery; applicant-tracking; content moderation; court | strict mediation |
| 3.00 | Pooled rideshare; crowdwork | higher-order mediation |

The negative control sits at the floor: the dyadic baselines, a direct two-party exchange and a worker in conversation with a model, score zero, because nothing constitutes a third party. The positive control is the spread at a fixed number of parties: among three-party forms, a parity determination scores 0.50, a partially mediated form scores 0.83, and a strictly mediated form scores 2.00. Φ separates these forms, which a count of parties cannot, and that separation is the structure the anchor's party-count axis does not reach.

### 4.4 Human and algorithmic mediation

The human-mediated forms interleave with the algorithmic forms at every level, sorted by structure. A court, in which two parties reach each other only through what a judge admits and rules, scores 2.00, the same as a rideshare dispatch, an applicant-tracking system, and a content-moderation queue. A staffing agency and a real-estate broker, which match two parties but leave them a direct channel, score 0.83, the same as an online freelance marketplace. The same procedure that scores Uber scores a courtroom, and it puts them at the same place.

## 5. Discussion

The result of Section 4.4 is the paper's load-bearing finding, and its meaning is that the model measures triadic coordination rather than algorithms. The demand a form makes is a property of its determination structure, of whether the two parties are bound only through a committed determination, and the seat of the mediator is immaterial to that structure. A human judge between two litigants and a dispatch algorithm between a driver and a rider make the same coordination demand because their structures are the same. This is what licenses the claim that the result is a model and not a study of platforms. If the scale only ordered software, it would be a description of one industry; because it orders a courtroom and a staffing agency alongside Uber and Upwork, and orders them by structure, it is a measure that travels.

The same point situates the scale against the way organizations are usually classified. The market-hybrid-hierarchy scheme and the network mode sort organizations by their governance form (Williamson, 1991; Powell, 1990). The triadic-demand scale is orthogonal to those schemes: a court is a hierarchy and a broker is a hybrid, yet they fall at different points on this scale, and an algorithmic platform and a human court fall at the same point. The scale does not replace the existing classifications; it cuts across them, in the way a readability score cuts across genre. The closest prior scheme, the value-chain governance forms graded by explicit coordination (Gereffi et al., 2005), shares the instinct that coordination admits of degree; this paper supplies a measure of the degree and an outcome that calibrates it.

The anchor also settles a question Paper 2 left open. Paper 2 pre-registered the continuous-stream case as the boundary of its individuation rule, the case of a platform that commits determinations without pause, where the analyst must choose a window and Φ depends on the choice. The anchor fixes the window not by fiat but by the outcome: the granularity at which Φ best tracks the observed coordination is the granularity that counts. The window that calibrates is the window that counts.

Two features of the results are worth reading correctly. That several organizations share a score is a finding, not a defect; the model's claim is precisely that forms which coordinate the same way make the same demand, whatever their industry. And the gap between the strong aggregate relation and the modest trip-level fit is not a weakness of the anchor but a confirmation of the unit. Φ is a property of the form, and like a readability score it predicts the form's aggregate difficulty far better than it predicts any single instance, because the single instance carries noise the form does not. Coordination has a readability, and it is a property of the form, not the worker.

## 6. Limitations

The scale measures the form and not the person, and every claim in this paper is about a coordination form and its observed outcomes. The person-level instrument is named as next work, not delivered. The calibration rests on one domain, and the portability of the model and the domain of the anchor are different claims that we keep apart: the model places organizations of many kinds, but the outcome relation is validated only in rideshare pooling, and a calibrated scale in one domain is the precondition for porting the method, not a claim that the same outcome would be observed everywhere. The typology placements are structural, derived from each form's determination structure, and they are outcome-validated only insofar as the anchor licenses the scale.

The sharpest limitation is the party-count confound, and we state it plainly. In the anchor, larger pools both raise Φ and add detour, so the anchor's friction relation runs through the number of parties, and the anchor validates the party-count axis of Φ directly. What shows that Φ is more than a party count is the positive control and the typology, where Φ separates forms that bind the same number of parties; that is where the structural content of the measure lives, and it is not what the anchor alone establishes. The two higher-order forms are modeled with four nodes, and their score of 3.0 is therefore not strictly comparable in magnitude to the three-node scale; they show that the scale extends upward when a determination binds more parties, but cross-node magnitudes should be read as ordinal. The granularity discipline bounds the state alphabet, applied uniformly so the scores compare. The anchor is an observational association, not a causal claim.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper turns Paper 2's instrument into a calibrated, portable measure. It maps any organization's coordination to an application layer, computes the irreducibility of that coordination as a property of its determination structure, and places the organization on one scale of triadic demand, calibrated against an observed outcome and demonstrated across organizations of very different kinds. The measure is a readability score for coordination: a property of the form, not the worker. What remains is the worker. The matched, person-level instrument, the additional calibration domains that would broaden the anchor, and the within-form variance that the matched pair will eventually explain are the program this measure opens. Paper 1 named the triad, Paper 2 measured it, and this paper reads organizations on the scale; what remains is the worker.

## 8. Data and Code Availability

All Φ values are computed by `typology_phi.py` (the typology, with no reference to data) and `anchor_chicago.py` (the calibration anchor); the robustness battery is `robustness_anchor.py`. The anchor and robustness scripts query the City of Chicago "Transportation Network Providers — Trips" dataset (Socrata resource m6dm-c72p) through the bounded queries documented in the scripts, and reproduce the reported numbers from the public data.
