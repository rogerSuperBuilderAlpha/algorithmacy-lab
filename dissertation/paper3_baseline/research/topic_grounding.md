## New — the readability precedent (Background §2 + the anchor framing §4)

The genre file (`research/empirical_quantitative_methodology.md`) holds the readability cluster as the
*structural template*; here it is as *content to cite* — the precedent that a score can be a property of a
form, validated against an outcome, saying nothing about an individual:

- **Flesch, R. (1948).** A new readability yardstick. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 32(3). The founding
  readability formula; a text-property score. *(Note: appears in a top applied-psych journal — precedent
  that a form-level difficulty score is a legitimate measurement contribution.)*
- **Kincaid, J. P., Fishburne, R. P., Rogers, R. L., & Chissom, B. S. (1975).** Derivation of new
  readability formulas. Naval Technical Training report. Flesch-Kincaid grade level.
- **DuBay, W. H. (2004).** *The Principles of Readability.* The field survey — readability's validation
  history (formulas validated against comprehension / cloze / grade level).
- **Crossley, S. A., et al. (2017)** and **Rafatbakhsh, E., et al. (2023)** — modern validations of a
  form-level score against measured comprehension / observed test-taker performance (the anchor analog).
- **The honest-limit precedent** — Zheng et al. 2017; Lenzner 2014 — readability formulas can *fail* to
  track perceived difficulty; cite to model candid reporting of where a form-level score's predictive power
  is modest (Paper 3's trip-level R² = 0.019 is exactly this kind of honest limit).

## New — the organizational-forms / governance literature (Background + Results, the typology)

Organizations have long been classified by their **coordination / governance mode**. Paper 3's scale is
**orthogonal** to these classifications: it sorts organizations by how irreducibly their coordination runs
*through a mediator* (Φ), regardless of whether they are called market, hierarchy, network, or platform —
a *readability score for coordination* that any form can be placed on. Positioning the typology against this
canon is what keeps it from reading as a rival classification.

- **[the canonical typology] Williamson, O. E. (1991/1994).** Comparative economic organization: the
  analysis of discrete structural alternatives. *Administrative Science Quarterly*, 36(2). (~7,000+ cites.)
  Market / hybrid / hierarchy, distinguished by coordination and control mechanisms. The reference point for
  "organizations are classified by governance mode."
- **[the network mode] Powell, W. W. (1990).** Neither market nor hierarchy: network forms of organization.
  *Research in Organizational Behavior*, 12. The third mode; the canonical "beyond market and hierarchy."
- **[formal coordination structures — and the Malone link] Malone, T. W. (1987).** Modeling coordination in
  organizations and markets. *Management Science*, 33(10), 1317–1332. Formal micro-level coordination
  structures with macro comparisons — a *formal-modeling* precedent for coordination, by the same Malone who
  later co-authored the Φ-for-groups paper (Engel & Malone 2018). A clean bridge: coordination has been
  formally modeled in *Management Science* before.
- **[coordination mechanisms] Mintzberg, H. (1980).** Structure in 5's: a synthesis of the research on
  organization design. *Management Science*, 26(3). Five coordination mechanisms (mutual adjustment, direct
  supervision, standardization) — a coordination-mechanism typology Paper 3's measure cuts across.
- **[explicit-coordination axis] Gereffi, G., Humphrey, J., & Sturgeon, T. (2005).** The governance of
  global value chains. *Review of International Political Economy*, 12(1). Five governance types ranged by
  **degree of explicit coordination and power asymmetry** — the closest prior to a *graded* coordination
  scale (Paper 3 grades by irreducibility instead).
- *(supporting: Grandori 1997, Organization Studies — interfirm coordination-mode typology; Provan & Kenis
  2008, JPART — modes of network governance; Ebers & Oerlemans 2016, J. Management — hybrid-governance
  typology; Thompson 2003 — markets/hierarchies/networks.)*

**What this licenses for Paper 3:** present the typology not as a new taxonomy of organizations but as a
*measure* that places any organization — however the governance literature already classifies it — on one
scale of triadic demand. The human-mediated contrast class (court, staffing agency, broker) is the proof
the measure is orthogonal: forms the governance literature would file under "hierarchy/authority" (a court)
and "hybrid/intermediary" (a broker) land at the same Φ as algorithmic platforms when their determination
structure matches.

## New — the triad / brokerage lineage (Background §2; the contrast class)

The triad is not a coinage of this dissertation; it is a century-old object in sociology and network theory.
Placing Φ inside that lineage is what makes "a measure of triadic coordination" land as a recognized move,
and it gives the human-mediated contrast class (court, staffing agency, broker) its scholarly home.

- **[the classical source] Simmel, G. (1950).** *The Sociology of Georg Simmel* (K. Wolff, Trans.; orig.
  1908). (3,500+ cites.) The triad transforms the dyad; the three types of the "third" — the disinterested
  **mediator/arbitrator**, the *tertius gaudens*, and *divide et impera*. Paper 3's mediator is Simmel's
  first type.
- **[the dyad-vs-triad gap, in org-economics] Nooteboom, B. (2006).** Simmel's treatise on the triad
  (1908). *Journal of Institutional Economics*, 2(3). States the exact gap the dissertation is built on:
  firm/organization studies "focus on **dyads** … even in network studies, networks were seen mostly as
  aggregates of dyads," whereas sociology has long attended to the **triad** (Simmel → Burt). The
  org-economics articulation of "the field measures dyads; the triad is the corrective."
- **[the three triad forms — locates Paper 3] Siltaloppi, J., & Vargo, S. L. (2017).** Triads. *Marketing
  Theory*, 17(4). Distinguishes three triadic forms: **brokerage** (the third's action), **mediation** (a
  dyad affected through a third party), and **coalition**. Paper 3 measures the **mediation** form — the
  determination-bound triad — precisely.
- **[triad ↔ irreducibility, the conceptual bridge] Christodoulou, M. (2021).** Georg Simmel and the
  ontology of relational emergence. *Journal for the Theory of Social Behaviour*, 51(3). Reads Simmel's
  triad through **irreducibility of relational properties and downward causation** — the bridge from the
  classical triad to Φ's irreducibility. (Use carefully: cite for the conceptual lineage, not as a claim
  that Φ measures Simmelian emergence.)
- **[market intermediaries as Simmel triads — grounds the contrast class] Khurana, R. (2002).** Market
  triads: … market intermediation. *Journal for the Theory of Social Behaviour*, 32(2). Applies Simmel's
  triad to **executive-search firms in the CEO labor market** — i.e., a staffing/search **intermediary**
  joining buyers and sellers. The scholarly home for the staffing-agency/broker contrast class.
- **[brokerage canon] Burt, R. S. (1992).** *Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition.*
  (2,900+ cites.) Brokerage across structural holes; the *tertius* roles. The network-theoretic backdrop;
  note Paper 3 measures *mediation*, not *brokerage-for-advantage* — distinguish them.
