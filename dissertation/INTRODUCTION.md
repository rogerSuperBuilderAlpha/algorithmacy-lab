# Chapter 1. The Transition to Algorithmacy

**Roger Hunt III**
Bentley University

*Introductory chapter of the three-paper dissertation. Frames the overarching problem and the relation among the three papers.*

## 1.1 A puzzle on the platforms

Two drivers work the same rideshare platform in the same city. They hold the same rating, drive the same hours, and accept the same fraction of the trips they are offered. The platform that manages them is one system, applying one set of rules to both. Yet over a month one earns well and the other barely clears expenses, and neither can say why. Two applicants send similar résumés into the same applicant-tracking system for similar roles. The system scores both against the same keyword profile. One is forwarded to a hiring manager and one is not, and neither learns what divided them. A puzzle runs through these cases and through much of platform-mediated work: equivalently positioned participants get divergent coordination outcomes through identical algorithmic systems, and the constructs the field has for studying work with algorithms do not explain the variance.

The constructs are not idle. Communication and organizational research has built a substantial vocabulary for how a person engages a computational system, including computer-mediated communication competence, human-machine communication, AI-mediated communication, algorithmic and AI literacy, and algorithm sensemaking. Each describes a worker on one side of a medium that conveys, scores, or interprets. None of them, applied to the cases above, locates the source of the variance, because each is built around the wrong unit. They describe a person and a system, a dyad, and the coordination that produces the divergent outcomes is not a dyad. It is a worker coordinating with a counterpart she may never meet, a rider, a hiring manager, a customer, through a system that interprets both, acts on both, and pursues objectives of its own across the two of them. The unit is a triad, and the competency a triad demands of its human parties has no name in the field's vocabulary.

## 1.2 The structural change beneath the puzzle

This dissertation argues that the variance puzzle is the surface of a structural change in how coordination is organized, and that the change is the right object of study. Coordination has become triadic. Where a worker once coordinated with a counterpart directly, or through a medium that carried messages without pursuing its own ends, she now coordinates through a system that stands between the two parties and commits determinations, a match, a price, a rank, a forward, that neither party controls and both must reason through. The third party does not merely add to the dyad; it transforms it, in the way the sociologist Georg Simmel observed a century ago that a third party transforms a pair. The platform economy has made Simmel's mediated triad the common form of coordination in ordinary working life, and it has done so faster than the constructs for studying work have adapted.

The competency this form demands is new in the way literacy was new against oracy. A worker in a dyadic exchange can reason one hop at a time, about the party she deals with directly, because the local picture is the whole picture. A worker in a triad cannot. To act well she must reason forward through a determination she does not control to a counterpart she cannot see, and backward from the determination to what produced it, holding the whole three-party structure in view at once. That capacity is what this dissertation calls **algorithmacy**: the communication competency that triadic algorithmic coordination demands. Naming it is the first task, because a capacity that has no name cannot be measured, taught, or studied, and the variance the platforms produce cannot be explained until the competency the platforms demand is named.

## 1.3 The measurement problem and a found tool

Naming the competency is not enough, because the argument has a debt it cannot discharge from within. If algorithmacy is the competency a triad demands, then an analyst studying a particular case has to be able to say whether the case is a triad. And the triad does not announce itself. Counting parties does not settle it, because a setting with three parties is still a dyad if the third is a passive conduit. Reading the interface does not settle it, because a worker who experiences nothing but an app may sit inside an irreducible triad she cannot see. Whether a coordination form is triadic is not given by its surface. It has to be determined, and determining it is a measurement problem the construct alone cannot solve.

The dissertation solves it with a tool built in another field. The distinction between a dyad and a triad is structural: a coordination form is triadic when it does not reduce to the pairwise dealings of its parties, when they must coordinate through a binding determination rather than around it. That property, irreducibility to the parts, is precisely what integrated information theory's measure, Φ, was built to quantify. Integrated information is the degree to which a system's cause-effect structure cannot be accounted for by its parts, and it is zero exactly when the structure factors along the lines between the parts (Albantakis et al., 2023; Oizumi et al., 2014). The thing that makes coordination non-dyadic and the thing Φ measures line up closely, and the dissertation treats that correspondence as a motivated modeling choice — defended, with its assumptions and its critics, in Paper 2 — rather than as an identity the world hands over. The dissertation imports the measure, re-reads its terms for coordination, and leaves the theory of consciousness that produced it at the door. Two disciplines carry the work throughout: where a claim can be computed within the model rather than argued, it is computed; and the model is checked on controls whose answers are known before it is run on cases in dispute. What the dissertation builds is a **formal model**, and it is explicit about what that is and is not — it characterizes what the model yields and surfaces the modeling choices it rests on, and it does not claim the model has been validated against observed coordination outcomes in the world. That validation is named, throughout, as the empirical program this work opens.

## 1.4 Three papers, one argument

The dissertation makes its argument in three papers, ordered from the conceptual to the empirical, each a standalone contribution and each a step the next requires.

**Paper 1 develops the construct.** Through an integrative review of the constructs that describe work with algorithms, it establishes that triadic coordination is a real and distinct form, that the existing dyadic constructs cannot reach it, and that the competency it demands is unnamed. The paper represents each existing construct in its strongest form and shows where it stops short of the triad, and it specifies algorithmacy to the discipline of construct clarity: a definition, a domain, dimensions, a discriminant position against its neighbors, and the measurement program it opens. Paper 1 establishes that the form exists and that a competency is missing. It does not measure either.

**Paper 2 builds the model.** It takes the irreducibility measure and adopts it as a formal model of the question Paper 1 leaves open: given a coordination form, do we model it as triadic? The paper represents a form as a three-node application-layer system of worker, system, and counterpart, fixes the modeling choices — above all a rule for individuating states — before any computation, checks the model on controls whose structure is known, and then runs it. The classification is a number: Φ over the application-layer matrix is zero when the modeled structure factors, a dyad, and positive when it does not, a triad. Crucially, Paper 2 is explicit that this is a modeling choice rather than a claimed identity between Φ and triadicity, and it acknowledges the standing critiques of the measure it borrows. The paper demonstrates the model on worked cases, including the two where the modeled classification runs against appearance, a three-party form that the model scores as a dyad and a worker-alone-with-an-app form it scores as a triad, and it reports a computed result the dissertation reads as a hypothesis about political economy: in the model, coordination is most irreducible when the parties are kept from coordinating directly, so a platform has a structural reason to design the dyad out. Paper 2 supplies the formal model. It does not yet grade how much, or validate against the world.

**Paper 3 grades the model across organizations.** A binary classification is not a graded scale. Paper 3 develops the graded, model-internal side by computing Φ across the complete family of three-node forms in the modeling vocabulary, finding that the scores fall onto a few discrete bands rather than spreading continuously, and then placing a typology of organizations on the populated bands — the way a readability score grades texts, except that the readability score has been validated against comprehension and this one has not. Its headline is a statement about the model, and it needs no observed outcome to hold: the score responds to determination structure and not to technology, so a human-mediated court is modeled at the same score as an algorithmic platform of the same structure. Paper 3 supplies the graded model and reads organizations on it; it is explicit that it does not validate the model against any coordination outcome. What it leaves to the program that follows is exactly that empirical validation — which must vary determination structure at a fixed number of parties — and, beyond it, the matched, person-level instrument that would measure a worker's capacity against a form of known demand.

The papers share one object, and naming it is what makes them one argument rather than three. Paper 1 names the irreducibility of coordination to its parts, Paper 2 models whether a given form has it, and Paper 3 grades the model across organizations. The construct, the model, and the graded landscape are three faces of the same thing, and the through-line is **name → formalize → (future) validate**.

## 1.5 The combined contribution

Taken together, the three papers turn a claim into a formal apparatus. "Coordination is becoming triadic" begins the dissertation as an observation about the platforms; it ends as a defined construct, a formal model of the structural condition that demands it, and a model-internal landscape that grades it. The dissertation contributes a name for a competency the field lacked, a computable formalization of the structural condition that demands it, and a graded account of how strongly any given coordination form, as modeled, makes the demand. It also contributes a method that travels beyond its particular subject: take a structural property of coordination that can be computed from observable records, fix the computation before interpreting it, and put the modeling choices on the table where they can be inspected and contested. The triadic-demand model is one filling of that method, and the discipline that makes it credible — computing rather than asserting, naming the modeling choices rather than hiding them, and distinguishing a formal model from a validated measure — is the discipline the dissertation has tried to hold throughout. The empirical validation that would turn the model into a measure of the world is named as the next arc of the program, not claimed here.

## 1.6 Plan of the dissertation

Chapter 2 (Paper 1) is the integrative review that develops the algorithmacy construct and forecloses the existing constructs that cannot reach the triad. Chapter 3 (Paper 2) builds the integrated-information model and the formal classification of triadicity, with the worked demonstrations and the eliminate-the-dyad result, and names the modeling choices and the critiques it must answer. Chapter 4 (Paper 3) grades the model across the whole family of forms and places a typology of organizations on its bands, cutting an earlier empirical anchor that validated only the trivial party-count axis. Chapter 5 draws the three together: it states the combined contribution, the significance of a formal account of triadic coordination for theory and for the people who work inside it, the research program the dissertation opens, and the bounds the work's load-bearing decisions impose. The variance puzzle that opened this chapter is where the dissertation begins; a formal model of the demand the platforms make, and a program for validating it and then for measuring the competency they require, is where it arrives.
