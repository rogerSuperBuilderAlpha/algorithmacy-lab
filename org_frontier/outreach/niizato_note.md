Subject: Your IIT fish-school work as a precedent for Φ on human coordination

Dear Dr. Niizato,

I'm a management researcher working on whether integrated information can distinguish kinds of human
coordination. Your fish-school papers (PLOS ONE 2020; Entropy 22:726; Sci. Rep. 2024) are, as far as
I can find, the only application of exact PyPhi-computed Φ to a real collective — and the result that
the Φ distribution changes qualitatively at school size four, where mutual information and transfer
entropy do not, has been the most useful precedent for what I'm trying to do.

I've been computing exact IIT-4.0 Φ on small models of coordination forms — a worker coordinating
with a counterpart through a mediating system — to ask whether a form is structurally "triadic"
(irreducible) or "dyadic" (factorable). One small result echoes yours directly: across these forms
the Φ verdict cleanly separates the two classes, while mutual information and transfer entropy do
not — transfer entropy is in fact highest on one of the reducible forms. It seemed worth telling you,
since the methodological point (Φ sees a distinction the dependence measures miss) is exactly the one
your fish-school work makes.

I'd genuinely welcome any reaction — especially on whether the size-dependent transition you found
has an analogue you'd expect in coordination structures, and on pitfalls in reading these small-system
verdicts. The code and write-ups are here (MIT):
https://github.com/rogerSuperBuilderAlpha/iit-experiments  (start with org_frontier/)
