# Agenda: designing coordination — the inverse problem (Q105–Q110) — done

The wave is on the record. Q105 (construction distance), Q106 (the design operations), Q107 (repair), Q108 (controllability), and Q110 (reversibility) are studies; Q109 (the cheapest triad) is subsumed by Q45, which already established the four-edge floor (2(n-1)) for the n=3 triadic forms. The wave gives a prescriptive account of crossing the dyad/triad boundary: the boundary is thin from the build side (Q105), the operations are the law's three reversible levers (Q106), repair is lever-specific (Q107), the parties are knife-edge controls and the hub the robust one (Q108), and the boundary is asymmetric — cheap to break, dear to build (Q110).

A new angle, and a new mode. The program has been descriptive throughout: it reads the verdict of a given
coordination form, the membership of its core, and the shape of its structure. It has never asked the
inverse — how to change a form's verdict. Q93 found that a triad is one edit from collapse and Q104 that
every edge of the minimal triad is load-bearing; both read the destroy side of the boundary. The build side,
and design in general, is unread.

This wave takes the prescriptive turn. Given a dyadic form, what is the minimal intervention that makes it
triadic? Given a triad damaged by substitution or noise, what restores it? Which party, if its reads could
be set, controls the verdict? The questions reframe algorithmacy from a diagnosis into an engineering
target: not only whether a coordination demands the competency, but how a designer would build the
coordination that does, or break the one that does not.

The questions use the existing instrument on the forms already on the record, with no new tool, and follow
the six-stage protocol. The wave feeds the dissertation's design-operations chapter with a computational
account of the operations and their costs.

## Q105 — Construction distance

**Question.** Given a dyadic form, what is the minimal number of single-bit edits that makes it triadic —
the dual of Q93's fragility margin? Is the dyad/triad boundary the same thin distance from both sides?

**What it reads.** For each dyadic form in the 256-form family, the minimal edit distance to a triadic
form. *Discriminator:* a construction-distance distribution, and whether the minimal edit is always at the
mediator (building the binding) rather than the parties.

## Q106 — The design operations

**Question.** Named operations — add the mediator's missing read, require all parties, insert a mediator
between two parties — each have a verdict effect. What is the catalog?

**What it reads.** The verdict before and after each operation on keystone forms. *Discriminator:* a small
set of operations that each move the verdict in a stated direction, a design vocabulary.

## Q107 — Repair

**Question.** A triad damaged by substitution (Q70) or a lossy read (Q91) has lost its verdict or weakened
it. What is the minimal intervention that restores the triad?

**What it reads.** The minimal edit, or the fidelity threshold, that returns a damaged form to triadic.
*Discriminator:* whether repair is the inverse of the damage, or whether a different intervention restores
it more cheaply.

## Q108 — Controllability

**Question.** Which single party, if a designer could set its reads, gives the most control over the
verdict? Is there a control node from which the verdict can be steered either way?

**What it reads.** The verdicts reachable by varying one party's function, for each party. *Discriminator:*
a party from which both verdicts are reachable, against parties that fix the verdict regardless.

## Q109 — The cheapest triad

**Question.** To make k parties coordinate as a triad, what is the minimal number of reads — the smallest
edge count that meets the law?

**What it reads.** The minimal-edge triadic form at each size. *Discriminator:* the edge floor for a triad
at k parties, against the dyadic forms below it.

## Q110 — Reversibility of the boundary

**Question.** Is the dyad/triad boundary symmetric, the same edit distance to cross in each direction, or
is it cheaper to build a triad than to break one, or the reverse?

**What it reads.** The construction distance (Q105) against the fragility margin (Q93) on the same forms.
*Discriminator:* whether the two distances match form by form, or whether the boundary is asymmetric.
