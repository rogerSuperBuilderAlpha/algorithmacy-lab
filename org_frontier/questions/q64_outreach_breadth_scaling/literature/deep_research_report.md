# Q64 — Stage 2 deep research: breadth in mediated outreach

This study extends Q63 along one axis, the number of recipients, so it draws on the same four literatures
and adds the question of one-to-many communication. The references are shared with Q63
(`references.bib`); the open gap is specific to breadth.

## One-to-many communication and segmentation

The economics of spam turns on the cost asymmetry of one-to-many sending: when a message costs almost
nothing, sending it to everyone is rational regardless of fit, which is the pooled-broadcast structure
[rao2012economics]. Field practice splits the difference with segmentation, sending a message tailored to
a group rather than to each person, and reports that tailoring to the recipient's context roughly doubles
reply rates over an undifferentiated blast. Segmentation is the practical middle between a broadcast and a
per-recipient determination, and the structural question is which side of the dyadic/triadic line it
falls on as the group grows.

## Costly signaling at scale

Costly-signaling theory says a signal is informative when its cost is high enough that faking it does not
pay [spence1973job; zahavi1975mate; grafen1990biological]. The per-recipient cost of a genuinely tailored
message rises with the number of recipients, while a broadcast's cost stays flat. The structural
counterpart, tested here, is whether the joint determination that makes outreach triadic survives being
spread across many recipients, or whether breadth forces the agent toward a substitutable or pooled commit
that factors.

## AI-mediated communication and coordination

AI-mediated communication studies an agent that generates or modifies messages on a person's behalf
[hancock2020aimc; jakesch2019aimc]; coordination theory treats the mediating mechanism as part of the
structure [malone1994interdisciplinary]. The lab reads that structure with exact integrated information
[albantakis2023iit4; oizumi2014phenomenology; mayner2018pyphi].

## The open gap

The signaling and spam literatures explain why a tailored message costs more as recipients multiply and
why a broadcast does not. None computes whether a multi-recipient campaign is structurally irreducible, or
how the verdict scales with breadth. The gap is open: build the all-required, substitutable, pooled, and
mixed multi-recipient campaigns, read the verdict and the major complex from k=1 to k=4, and test the
conjunctive-hub scaling law (Φ=n−1) and the substitutability-collapse finding in the outreach frame. The
study is in-silico and inherits the validation gap [axtell1996aligning]; five hypotheses are fixed before
computing [chamberlin1965method; platt1964strong; brodeur2024preregistration].
