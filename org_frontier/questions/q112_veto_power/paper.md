# Q112 — Veto power: everyone can break it, only the mediator captures it

## Question

Q104 found every edge of the minimal triad load-bearing, and Q111 found value concentrated at the mediator.
This study asks the game-theoretic question that joins them: which parties hold a veto, able to collapse the
coordination by withdrawing, and does the veto track the value? If every party can break the coordination
but only the mediator captures its value, then the power to destroy and the power to capture are distinct.

## Method

A party defects by going constant, stopping its participation. For the read-recipient triad, each core
party's defection is checked against the verdict; a read-only spectator's defection is checked against the
major complex. The Shapley values from Q111 and the sub-coalition values give the value side.

## Results

Every core party's defection collapses the triad to dyadic, and the spectator's defection leaves the major
complex {E, M, R} intact. The Shapley values are 1.333 for the mediator and 0.333 for each outer party. The
sub-coalition values are {E, R} = 0 and {M, R} = {E, M} = 2.

| | result |
|---|---|
| H1 every core party holds a veto | confirmed |
| H2 spectator holds no veto | confirmed |
| H3 veto universal, value unequal | confirmed |
| H4 not a unanimity game | confirmed |

## Interpretation

The veto is universal and the value is concentrated, and the two come apart because the coordination is not
a unanimity game. Every bound party can end the coordination (withdraw any one and it collapses), so the
power of refusal is held equally. But the value, divided by marginal contribution, concentrates at the
mediator, because the two outer parties produce nothing on their own while either produces the full value
with the mediator. The mediator is more than a veto player; it is the party productive in every partial
coalition, and that productivity, not the shared veto, is what captures the rent.

This separates two powers the structure assigns to different parties. The workers and the counterpart hold
the power of collapse: each can refuse and end the coordination, so each has leverage over whether it
happens at all. The mediator holds the power of capture: the value flows through it, so it takes the largest
share of whatever the coordination produces. A worker's threat to walk away is as strong as anyone's, but a
worker's claim on the value is a sixth. The asymmetry is not in who can destroy, which is democratic, but
in who is productive, which the structure concentrates at the hub.

The reading completes Q111's account of platform power. Q111 found the platform captures two-thirds of the
value; Q112 finds that capture coexists with a universal veto, so the parties are not powerless — they can
collapse the platform's coordination — but their power is negative, the threat of refusal, while the
platform's is positive, the capture of value. A coordination among parties who each can refuse but only one
of whom is productive is a coordination where the rent goes to the productive party and the leverage is
spread among the rest. That is the political economy of a mediated coordination, read from its structure.

## Limitations

In-silico; exact verdict, major complex, and Shapley value. Defection is modelled as a party going constant;
other defections break the triad too, so the veto does not depend on the choice. The veto-value split is
read on the read-recipient triad, a mediated coordination; the all-required market, where value distributes
equally, is Q113's case, and there the veto and the value would both be symmetric.
