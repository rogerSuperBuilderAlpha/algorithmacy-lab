# Q111 — The Shapley value of integration: the mediator captures the rent

## Question

The program reads which parties are bound into a coordination. It has not asked what a party is worth. This
study opens the political-economy wave by defining value: the worth of a coalition of parties is the
integrated information their subsystem sustains, and a party's Shapley value distributes that worth by
marginal contribution. The question is who captures the value — does the mediator take a disproportionate
share, and does a non-core party take any?

## Method

The value of a coalition S is the Φ of the subsystem on S, with the complement fixed at the all-ones state.
A party's Shapley value is the exact average of its marginal contribution over all orderings. Two forms: the
read-recipient triad, and the triad plus a read-only spectator.

## Results

The mediator's Shapley value is 1.333, two-thirds of the triad's Φ of 2.0; each outer party's is 0.333, a
sixth. The values sum to Φ. The read-only spectator's Shapley value is −0.833.

| party | Shapley value | share |
|---|---|---|
| mediator | 1.333 | 67% |
| worker | 0.333 | 17% |
| recipient | 0.333 | 17% |

| H2 values sum to Φ | confirmed |

## Interpretation

The value goes to the party the others cannot do without. The two outer parties produce nothing alone — their
coalition has value zero — while either pairs with the mediator to produce the full value. The mediator is
in every productive coalition, so its marginal contribution is large in every ordering, and the Shapley
value assigns it two-thirds of the worth. The structural law that the mediator binds the parties is, read as
economics, the law that the mediator captures the value the parties create together.

This is platform power made exact. A platform that stands between two parties who cannot coordinate directly
captures a disproportionate share of the value of their coordination, not by taking it from them but by
being the party whose absence leaves nothing to take. The Shapley value, the fair division by marginal
contribution, gives the platform two-thirds because the platform's marginal contribution is the entire value
whenever it joins the parties to each other. The rent is not an extraction laid on top of the structure; it
is the structure, read in the currency of value.

An outsider captures nothing, and a spoiler captures less. The read-only spectator takes negative value,
because adding it factors the whole system and drops the Φ from two to zero — the Q74 divergence between the
whole system and the major complex, read economically. A party outside the core does not share in the value
the bound parties create, and a party that breaks the whole-system coordination subtracts from it. Value
tracks core membership, with a sign: the bound capture, the absent capture nothing, the disruptive capture
negative.

The result equips the wave. Defining value as subsystem Φ and dividing it by Shapley gives a measure that
agrees with the structural account — the bound parties are the valuable ones, the mediator the most valuable
— and lets the rest of the political economy be read: who can veto (Q112), how substitutability erodes value
(Q113), and whether a principal buys a share at the parties' expense (Q114).

## Limitations

In-silico; exact subsystem Φ and exact Shapley value. The coalition value is the subsystem Φ with the
complement fixed at the all-ones state; another background state could shift the numbers, and the all-ones
state is the integrating one here. The two-thirds capture is read on one triad; its scaling is Q115's
question. The spectator's negative value is a whole-system reading; a major-complex value function would
assign it zero, and the choice between the two is the Q74 reporting rule applied to value.
