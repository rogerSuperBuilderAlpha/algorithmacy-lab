# Q115 — Stage 2 literature: value distribution in a scaling required market

## The question in the literature's terms

Q115 asks how the surplus of a coordinated market divides between unique parties and a growing pool of
required-but-interchangeable agents. In cooperative game theory this is the Shapley value of a market game,
and the relevant prior result is the asymptotic theory of large games: as a player type is replicated, the
marginal contribution of each replica falls toward its competitive (price-taking) value while scarce,
unreplicated types capture the surplus. Shapley and Shubik's market-game program and the equivalence of the
Shapley value with the competitive equilibrium in large markets are the canonical statements.

## What is known

- **Replication erodes the replicated.** In a market game, replicating one side of the market drives that
  side's per-capita value toward its marginal product and shifts surplus to the scarce side. The "glove
  game" (left and right gloves, value only in matched pairs) is the textbook case: the scarce glove captures
  almost all the value as the abundant glove is replicated.
- **The Shapley value is efficient.** It distributes exactly the grand coalition's worth, at every size. The
  efficiency axiom is definitional; here it doubles as a check that the subsystem-Φ coalition value behaves
  as a proper characteristic function (H1).
- **Scarcity, not effort, commands rent.** A required input is necessary but not scarce once many copies
  exist; necessity is shared, scarcity is not. The unique parties in an organizational market are the
  unreplicated type.

## The gap Q115 fills

The market-game results are economic, with value an exogenous characteristic function. Q115 asks whether the
same concentration arises when the characteristic function is the integrated information Φ of the
coordination itself, computed exactly. If the irreducible joint determination of a required market
concentrates its value in the unique parties as the agent pool grows, the economic law of scarcity is
recovered from a purely structural quantity, with no prices or preferences assumed.
