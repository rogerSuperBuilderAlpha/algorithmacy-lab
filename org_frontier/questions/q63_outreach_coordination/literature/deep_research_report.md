# Q63 — Stage 2 deep research: outreach, signaling, and AI-mediated communication

The question sits at the meeting of four literatures. Each is established; none has computed the
structural verdict this study computes.

## Costly signaling

Spence's job-market signaling model showed that an observable, costly action conveys an unobservable
quality when the cost is lower for higher-quality senders, producing a separating equilibrium in which
the signal is informative [spence1973job]. Zahavi's handicap principle reached the same conclusion in
biology: signals are reliable when they are expensive enough that faking them does not pay
[zahavi1975mate]. Grafen put the handicap principle on a formal game-theoretic footing equivalent to
Spence's economic model [grafen1990biological]. The transfer to outreach is direct: a message that costs
the sender something can carry information that a free message cannot, and the question is what the cost
is buying. This study's claim is that the cost buys joint determination of the message on the recipient's
state, which is the structural condition for a triad.

## The economics of spam

Rao and Reiley analyze unsolicited email as a negative externality with an external-to-internal cost ratio
near 100:1, sustained because sending is nearly free [rao2012economics]. Their account makes the cost
asymmetry concrete: when a message costs the sender almost nothing, mass undifferentiated sending is
rational regardless of fit to any recipient. That is the broadcast structure this study models as dyadic.

## AI-mediated communication and disclosure

Hancock, Naaman, and Levy define AI-mediated communication as interpersonal communication in which an
agent modifies, augments, or generates messages on a person's behalf, and set the research agenda for its
effects on trust and authenticity [hancock2020aimc]. Jakesch and colleagues found experimentally that the
mere perception that text was AI-generated lowers its perceived trustworthiness, independent of the text
itself [jakesch2019aimc]. These results bear on the disclosure question: whether identifying a message as
agent-generated changes how it is received. The structural counterpart, tested here, is whether disclosure
changes the coordination structure at all, or only the recipient's reception of it.

## Coordination theory

Malone and Crowston frame coordination as managing dependencies among activities and treat the mediating
mechanism as part of the coordination structure [malone1994interdisciplinary]. The lab's program reads
that structure with exact integrated information [albantakis2023iit4; oizumi2014phenomenology;
mayner2018pyphi], classifying a coordination form as dyadic or triadic by Φ over the minimum-information
partition.

## The open gap

The signaling literatures explain why a costly, tailored message can be informative and why a free mass
message is not. The AI-mediated-communication literature measures how recipients react to automation and
disclosure. Neither computes whether the coordination is structurally irreducible, and no source applies
exact Φ to outreach. The gap is open: build the canonical outreach forms, read the verdict, and test
whether reading the recipient, substitutability, disclosure, a cost proxy, and reciprocity move it as the
lab's structural findings predict.

This study is in-silico and inherits the program's validation gap: a verdict is evidence about the Boolean
model, not about a real inbox [axtell1996aligning]. The design holds five hypotheses before computing
[chamberlin1965method; platt1964strong], with the decision rules fixed in advance [brodeur2024preregistration].
