# Q97 literature — adversarial coalitions and the membership boundary

## The single-agent result

Q84 showed that a single agent positioned outside the core cannot change the dyadic/triadic verdict: a
read-only or emit-only agent leaves the triad intact, and only a bidirectional, pivotal agent alters it,
by joining the core. Influence over the coordination requires membership in it. Q98 sharpened the membership
gate: zero reading or zero influence excludes a party with no leakage. Both results are about single agents.

## The coalition question

Security rarely faces one adversary. A coalition of agents, each individually unable to enter the core,
might coordinate to gain influence the members lack alone — one observes the coordination and passes what
it sees to another that feeds the coordination, so the pair is collectively bidirectional while each is
one-way with respect to the core. Whether collective bidirectionality buys influence without membership is
the open security question. The expected answer, by extension of Q84 and Q98, is that the coalition can
flip the verdict only by placing a member in the core — the member the coordination reads must itself
become bound — but a coalition could in principle route influence around the membership requirement, and
that is untested.

## Method context

The forms extend Q84's adversarial agent to two agents and a bridging loop, read by the program's standard
verdict and major-complex instrument.
