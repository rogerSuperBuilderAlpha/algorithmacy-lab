# Q94 — Stage 3 hypotheses (fixed before computation)

Four hypotheses on whether a coordination form can hold two coexisting irreducible complexes, and what
coupling merges them into one. The program has only ever read the single major complex; this asks when
coordination fragments into disjoint cores. Two minimal mutual-determination units ({A,B} and {C,D}) are
coupled four ways, and the exclusion-resolved set of disjoint maximal complexes is read. Written and
committed before the bridge configurations are read. The uncoupled baseline (two complexes) is the
instrument control.

## H1 — Two uncoupled units hold two coexisting complexes

- **Claim:** A form of two units with no coupling between them has two disjoint irreducible complexes.
- **H0:** It has one complex, or none.
- **Predicted outcome:** two complexes. H0 refuted. Fragmentation is real: a form need not have a single
  core.

## H2 — A shared spectator does not merge the units

- **Claim:** Two units that both read an idle spectator (which reads nothing back) still hold two
  disjoint complexes; the spectator is in neither.
- **H0:** The shared spectator merges the units into one complex.
- **Predicted outcome:** still two. H0 refuted. Passive sharing is not coupling.

## H3 — A one-way bridge does not merge the units

- **Claim:** When the second unit reads the first but not the reverse, the form still holds two or more
  disjoint complexes.
- **H0:** The one-way bridge merges the units into a single complex.
- **Predicted outcome:** still fragmented. H0 refuted. Merging needs mutual coupling, echoing the
  bidirectional condition of Finding 8. This is the study's genuinely uncertain claim.

## H4 — A mutual bridge merges the units into one

- **Claim:** When the two units read each other, the form has a single complex spanning all four nodes.
- **H0:** The mutually-bridged form still holds two disjoint complexes.
- **Predicted outcome:** one spanning complex. H0 refuted.
