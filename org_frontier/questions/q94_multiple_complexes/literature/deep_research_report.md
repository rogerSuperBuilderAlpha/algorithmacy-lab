# Q94 literature — can coordination hold more than one core?

## The single-complex habit

Every membership result in the program reads the major complex — the single maximal substrate of
integrated information. Finding 8 and Q90 locate which parties join that one core. IIT 4.0 does not
require a system to have only one complex: a substrate can decompose into several complexes that do not
overlap, each integrated within itself and reducible across the cut between them (Albantakis et al. 2023).
Whether a coordination form fragments this way, holding two coexisting cores, is unasked.

## Why it matters

A coordination that splits into two independent cores is a different object from one that binds into a
single triad. The question is when a form fragments and when it merges: a shared but passive element, or
a one-way flow, may leave two cores intact, while mutual coupling may fuse them. The expected answer
echoes the bidirectional condition of Finding 8 at the level of whole cores rather than single parties —
two cores merge only when they constrain each other both ways — but it is untested.

## Method context

PyPhi's `irreducible_complexes` returns the lattice of irreducible subsets at a state; resolving it into
disjoint maximal complexes by exclusion gives the count of coexisting cores. The construction uses minimal
two-node mutual-determination units so the merging condition is isolated cleanly.
